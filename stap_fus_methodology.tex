\begin{abstract}
Functional ultrasound (fUS) and ultrafast Doppler imaging face severe interference from tissue motion, phase aberrations, and aliased high-velocity flow, which can obscure slow microvascular dynamics. Low-rank clutter suppression methods such as motion-compensated singular value decomposition (MC--SVD) are widely used, but can degrade in extreme clutter and in the far right tail of flow evidence.

Motivated by airborne radar and RF array processing, we introduce a reduced-dimensional, post-beamformed space--time adaptive processing (STAP) framework for fUS slow-time flow detection. The method operates on localized spatiotemporal tiles with a fixed-parameter operating profile (tile geometry, slow-time aperture, Doppler band geometry, and covariance conditioning) and produces a matched-subspace STAP score, applied to the residual after a coarse baseline clutter filter. An optional knowledge-aided (KA) spatial-spectral prior uses local band-energy features to apply a conservative, shrink-only penalty in high-risk regions while protecting a high-confidence set; by construction, the post-KA score is non-increasing on penalized pixels. We also study conditional execution as a decision-rule variant that skips STAP outside a flow-proxy region and quantify its leakage sensitivity.

Quantitative detection performance is evaluated using a strict low-FPR operating discipline (typically $10^{-4}$--$10^{-3}$, subject to empirical support), reflecting the sensitivity of downstream functional mapping to rare flow artifacts and consistent with tail-rate control philosophies in radar detection and tracking. We report results on labeled k-Wave brain simulations and on structurally labeled \emph{in vitro} phantom acquisitions (Gammex; labels derived from B-mode geometry), and we benchmark against MC--SVD, adaptive/global SVD clutter filters (including spatial-singular similarity cutoffs), block-wise local SVD clutter filters, robust PCA, and tensor HOSVD baselines as well as conventional short-ensemble Doppler scores. We further report labeled within-ensemble motion ladders on phantom data via per-shot motion injection, and label-free stability under controlled motion corruption on kilohertz ultrasound localization microscopy (ULM) IQ blocks. Because most open in-vivo fUS releases do not preserve raw high-PRF complex IQ with independent $H_1/H_0$ labels, we restrict quantitative ROC claims to labeled simulations and structurally labeled phantoms, using in-vivo data only for robustness and telemetry audits. All experiments and figures are accompanied by a command-level reproducibility manifest in the Supplementary Material (exact scripts, arguments, and expected artifact paths).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Microvascular flow detection in ultrasound is an extreme-tail problem: flow evidence occupies a small fraction of pixels while tissue motion, phase aberration, and aliased high-velocity clutter induce heavy-tailed background statistics. In this regime, improvements in global summaries such as AUC can be operationally uninformative; the key requirement is stable detection at ultra-low false-positive rates.

We address this setting with a localized, STAP-inspired detector in which adaptation is temporal (slow-time), while spatial localization enters through tile-local covariance estimation. Instead of relying only on global low-rank suppression, we whiten local slow-time snapshots and score energy in a flow subspace, targeting spatially varying interference patterns that dominate extreme-tail false alarms.
Figure~\ref{fig:leading_structural_fidelity} provides a motivating qualitative example on a structurally labeled flow phantom: at a matched background-FPR operating point, STAP modifies detection decisions in a way that is consistent with the B-mode-derived vessel geometry.

\section{Background and Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:background-problem}

\paragraph{Problem setup.}
For each spatial location (or tile-aggregated snapshot), let $x\in\mathbb{C}^{L_t}$ denote a slow-time vector. We consider
\[
H_0:\ x=c+n,\qquad H_1:\ x=s+c+n,
\]
where $s$ is flow-consistent signal, $c$ is structured clutter/motion, and $n$ is residual noise. In practice, $H_0$ is spatially nonstationary and heavy-tailed, motivating local covariance adaptation and tail-focused evaluation.

\paragraph{Core notation and bands.}
We use band operators $(\Po,\Pf,\Pg,\Pa)$ for clutter/DC, flow, guard, and alias regions, respectively. For short ensembles, these are exact orthogonal DFT-bin projectors; for longer apertures they are leakage-controlled analysis operators and are treated as band-energy statistics with explicit sanity sentinels (\SuppOrApp{app:alg-details}).

\subsection{Related context}
\textbf{STAP and knowledge-aided STAP.} Classical MVDR/LCMV/STAP and knowledge-aided variants shape covariances using external structure \cite{Capon1969Beamformer,FrostBeamformer,WardSTAPTutorial,GuerciSTAPBook2003,WicksKASTAP2006,MelvinGuerciKAParadigm2006,GurramGoodmanKASTAP2006}. In radar, KA is often expressed as covariance shaping/blending or knowledge-aided covariance estimation; here we adopt the KA spirit but implement it as a shrink-only, fail-safe score regularizer on top of a fixed detector statistic, with explicit conditions for inert behavior out of regime.

\textbf{Ultrasound clutter suppression.} Spatiotemporal SVD clutter filters are a standard tool in ultrafast Doppler and fUS \cite{DemeneClutterSVD2015,BarangerAdaptiveSVD2018,Mace2013,Rabut2020,ICDFUS2021}. Real-time feasibility has been studied via randomized/approximate decompositions and downsampling strategies \cite{LokRandomizedSVD2020}, as well as GPU-parallel eigen-based clutter filtering frameworks \cite{CheeGPUEigen2017}. Robust PCA, unfolded/learned RPCA, and tensor/HOSVD variants have also been studied for clutter suppression and motion robustness \cite{CandesRPCA,SolomonDeepUnfoldedRPCA2020,HuangAdaptiveHOSVD2024}. More generally, when raw channel/aperture data are available, aperture-domain multidimensional clutter filtering can provide additional degrees of freedom beyond beamformed slow-time processing \cite{OzgunByramApertureHOSVD2021}. We benchmark STAP against MC--SVD and established SVD-family clutter filters spanning adaptive-global SVD (spatial singular-vector similarity cutoffs), block-wise local SVD with frozen tilewise energy thresholds, and an adaptive-local hybrid that applies the same similarity-drop rule tilewise \cite{BarangerAdaptiveSVD2018,SongBlockwiseLocal2017}, as well as RPCA and HOSVD baselines; conventional short-ensemble Doppler comparators are additionally reported on phantom data.
These baselines are included to represent state-of-practice eigen/SVD clutter filtering beyond fixed-cutoff global Casorati SVD and to preempt the common critique that strict-tail gains are attributable only to localization or cutoff tuning rather than to the detector formulation and tail operating discipline.

\textbf{Relation to eigen-based and local adaptive clutter filtering.} Eigen-based clutter filter design for ultrasound color flow imaging and block-wise/local adaptive clutter filters are closely related ultrasound-native approaches to covariance/eigenspace adaptation \cite{YuLovstakkenEigenReview2010,SongBlockwiseLocal2017,CheeGPUEigen2017}. Song \emph{et al.}\ demonstrate local clutter filtering in \emph{in vivo} settings (e.g., kidney imaging), while Baranger \emph{et al.}\ provide an adaptive singular-vector-similarity rank rule for ultrafast Doppler \cite{BarangerAdaptiveSVD2018}. Our benchmark set includes both literature families directly and a tile-local hybrid that combines Song-style overlap-add localization with a Baranger-style adaptive rank rule. Many open fUS releases do not preserve raw high-PRF complex IQ suitable for labeled Doppler-side detector evaluation, so we use labeled k-Wave pilots and structurally labeled phantoms for quantitative ROC claims and use \emph{in vivo} data for robustness and instrumentation audits. Our contribution differs in the \emph{detector} formulation and evaluation discipline: in contrast to approaches framed primarily as clutter suppression followed by power Doppler scoring, we compute a GLRT-motivated matched-subspace detector score on tile-local slow-time snapshots with explicit Doppler band geometry (flow/guard/alias) and optional tile-local whitening, evaluate at empirically supported ultra-low FPR operating points where background-tail behavior dominates, and treat compute gating (conditional execution) as a distinct decision rule with leakage controls.

\textbf{ULM pipelines and motion correction.} Ultrafast ultrasound localization microscopy (ULM) achieves deep super-resolution vascular imaging by localizing sparse microbubble echoes over high-PRF ensembles \cite{ErricoULM2015}. Robust clutter suppression and motion correction are central to ULM pipelines, including subwavelength motion correction tailored to ultrafast acquisitions \cite{HingotULMMotion2017}. We do not claim improved microbubble localization performance here; rather, we use a public kHz IQ ULM benchmark dataset to report label-free stability and motion robustness of Doppler-side score maps under controlled motion corruption, illustrating how a tail-stable detector can complement upstream motion correction and downstream localization stages.

\textbf{Twinkling artifact and Doppler phantoms.} The Doppler ``twinkling artifact'' has been documented as a persistent color-flow phenomenon on calcifications and other strong reflectors, and is widely used as a diagnostic benchmark for Doppler processing chains. Leonov \emph{et al.}\ provide an open-access database of raw beamformer signals with phantoms including a flow-through Gammex phantom and calculi/metal/plastic objects \cite{LeonovTwinklingDD2021}. We use these data to (i) obtain a structurally labeled phantom ROC via B-mode-only tube segmentation and (ii) construct an artifact-heavy KA hygiene regime where the prior penalty is expected to be conservative and auditable.

\textbf{fUS detection and hemodynamic analysis.} GLM-based detection and atlas-driven ROI analysis are common in hemodynamic fUS \cite{Demeneglmband2016,UrbanWholeBrain2015}; our hemodynamic STAP variant reuses the same banded temporal machinery on PD time series and adds an alias-aware shrink-only prior penalty as a safety layer.

\subsection{Baseline clutter suppression and score conventions}
\paragraph{Beamformed slow-time data.}
Let $X[t,y,x]\in\mathbb{C}$ denote a beamformed slow-time IQ movie over $t=0,\dots,T-1$ and spatial indices $(y,x)$. We first apply a baseline clutter-suppression operator (e.g.\ motion-compensated SVD), producing a residual cube $X_{\mathrm{base}}[t,y,x]$ used for both baseline scoring and as the input to STAP.

\paragraph{Power Doppler proxy and baseline scores.}
From the baseline-filtered cube we define a conventional power Doppler (PD) brightness map used only as a display/proxy map for deterministic preprocessing (e.g.\ proxy masks) and visualization,
\[
  \mathrm{PD}^{\uparrow}(y,x)=\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\left|X_{\mathrm{base}}(t,y,x)\right|^2.
\]
For quantitative evaluation and thresholding we use \emph{flow-evidence} score maps where higher values indicate stronger evidence of flow, and we calibrate thresholds on the right tail of these score distributions. We denote the baseline ROC score map as $S_{\mathrm{base}}(y,x)$ (power Doppler on $X_{\mathrm{base}}$). For short-ensemble regimes we additionally report auxiliary, conventional baseline comparators (e.g.\ log-power Doppler and Kasai lag-1 magnitude \cite{KasaiAutocorrelation1985}) to avoid baseline-score idiosyncrasies dominating conclusions (Notation).
\noindent Note: $\mathrm{PD}^{\uparrow}$ and $S_{\mathrm{base}}$ use the same underlying power statistic (up to fixed scaling); we keep separate names to make it explicit when PD is used only as a display/proxy map (masks/visuals) versus as a ROC score map.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
\label{sec:stap-architecture}
\label{sec:ka-contract-v2} % Backward-compatible label used throughout.

\paragraph{Algorithm 1 (STAP pipeline with optional KA regularization, fixed profile).}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=1.6em,label=\arabic*.]
  \item Baseline clutter suppression (e.g.\ MC--SVD) produces a residual cube $X_{\mathrm{base}}$ and baseline score $S_{\mathrm{base}}(y,x)$.
  \item For each tile $\Omega_i$: form slow-time snapshots $\{x_{i,k}\}_{k=1}^K$ via spatial sampling and, optionally, a sliding-window temporal embedding (Hankel matrix construction), estimate conditioned covariance $R_i$, and whiten $y_{i,k}=R_i^{-1/2}x_{i,k}$.
  \item Compute a matched-subspace ratio score (ACE/MSD-style) on whitened snapshots, e.g.\ $S_{\mathrm{stap,pre}}(i)=\frac{1}{K}\sum_k \frac{y_{i,k}\Hh \Pf\, y_{i,k}}{y_{i,k}\Hh (I-\Pf)\, y_{i,k}+\rho\, y_{i,k}\Hh y_{i,k}+\epsilon}$, and aggregate over overlapping tiles to obtain $S_{\mathrm{stap,pre}}(y,x)$.
  \item If KA enabled: compute a deterministic, shrink-only weight $w(i)\in(0,1]$ from auxiliary band-energy statistics and proxy-support features (details in the Supplementary Material), preserve a protected do-not-penalize set (scores unchanged), and output $S_{\mathrm{stap,post}}(y,x)=w\,S_{\mathrm{stap,pre}}(y,x)$. Else, $S_{\mathrm{stap,post}}(y,x)=S_{\mathrm{stap,pre}}(y,x)$.
\end{enumerate}
\noindent In practice STAP is intended as a second-stage residual covariance whitener: a coarse baseline clutter filter removes gross stationary tissue clutter, and STAP targets the remaining structured, spatially varying interference that dominates extreme-tail false alarms. A STAP-only ablation on phantom data is provided in \SuppOrApp{app:supp-results}.

\subsection{Temporal STAP core and Doppler band geometry}
\paragraph{Tile-based STAP.}
To mitigate the degraded tail sensitivity of global low-rank filters in high-clutter regimes, we apply a localized temporal STAP core on overlapping spatial tiles. We use ``STAP'' in a STAP-inspired sense: the adaptive filter operates in slow time, while spatial localization enters through tile-local covariance adaptation. Let $\Omega_i$ denote tile $i$ and $L_t$ the slow-time aperture. From $X_{\mathrm{base}}$ we construct a set of slow-time training snapshots $\{x_{i,k}\in\mathbb{C}^{L_t}\}_{k=1}^K$ for each tile via spatial sampling and, when applicable, a sliding-window temporal embedding (Hankel construction) in slow time. Here $K$ denotes the number of training snapshots used to estimate the tile covariance; in the fully Hankelized case, each pixel contributes $N=T-L_t+1$ snapshots and pooling across $\Omega_i$ yields $K=N|\Omega_i|$ (or a subsampled/capped variant). We estimate a local covariance
\[
  \wh R_i := \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K x_{i,k}x_{i,k}\Hh,
\]
apply conditioning/diagonal loading to obtain $R_i$, and whiten snapshots as $y_{i,k}:=R_i^{-1/2}x_{i,k}$.
\paragraph{Training support and Hankel dependence.}
In the fully Hankelized case, each pixel in $\Omega_i$ contributes $N=T-L_t+1$ overlapping slow-time snapshots; pooling across the $|\Omega_i|$ pixels yields $K=N|\Omega_i|$ tile-local training vectors. These Hankelized snapshots are temporally correlated due to overlap, so $K$ should not be interpreted as an independent-sample count. Our robust covariance estimators do not require independence, but the effective support can be significantly smaller than $K$; we therefore optionally subsample Hankel columns (stride) and cap the number of columns used for training to bound computation and avoid overweighting highly correlated samples (fixed per regime; \SuppOrApp{app:repro}).

\paragraph{Robust covariance estimation and conditioning.}
We whiten with a regularized covariance $R_i$ rather than the raw SCM $\wh R_i$. In the primary profile we use a Tyler-type M-estimator on the pooled tile snapshots, iterating
\[
  R_i^{(n+1)} \leftarrow \frac{L_t}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K \frac{x_{i,k}x_{i,k}\Hh}{x_{i,k}\Hh (R_i^{(n)})^{-1}x_{i,k}},
  \qquad R_i^{(n+1)} \leftarrow \frac{L_t}{\tr(R_i^{(n+1)})}\,R_i^{(n+1)},
\]
with at most 25 iterations and early stopping when $\|R^{(n+1)}-R^{(n)}\|_F/\|R^{(n)}\|_F < 10^{-4}$ (latency-only experiments may cap iterations; \SuppOrApp{app:repro}). We then apply deterministic conditioning (shrinkage toward $\mu I$ and diagonal loading $\lambda\mu I$, where $\mu=\tr(R)/L_t$ and $\lambda$ is fixed per regime) to enforce target condition numbers. In very short-ensemble regimes we may fall back to a Huber IRLS covariance estimate for numerical stability (\SuppOrApp{app:repro}).

\fbox{\begin{minipage}{0.95\linewidth}
\textbf{Terminology: ``space--time'' in fUS STAP.}
In classical radar STAP, a snapshot stacks spatial array channels and temporal pulse taps, and adaptation uses training snapshots from nearby range bins \cite{WardSTAPTutorial,GuerciSTAPBook2003}.
In beamformed fUS IQ we operate on a single complex slow-time sample per pixel, so we do not exploit array-manifold degrees of freedom; our space--time vector is the slow-time stack $x\in\mathbb{C}^{L_t}$, while ``space'' enters through \emph{localized adaptation}: we estimate $R_i$ from snapshots drawn from a small spatial neighborhood (tile) assumed approximately stationary over that region.
This yields a reduced-dimensional, post-Doppler STAP-like detector: we whiten by $R_i^{-1/2}$ and score energy in an explicitly defined Doppler flow subspace using a matched-subspace statistic (Section~\ref{sec:method}), rather than framing the method solely as clutter suppression followed by power Doppler.
We use the term ``STAP'' to emphasize these defining elements (local covariance adaptation, whitening, and subspace detection) while being explicit that channel-domain STAP/beamforming is out of scope for the present beamformed pipeline.

\paragraph{Band operators and matched-subspace STAP score.}
We partition the Doppler spectrum using band projectors $(\Po,\Pf,\Pg,\Pa)$ (Notation). For short ensembles, these are exact, orthogonal DFT-bin projectors with $\Po+\Pf+\Pg+\Pa=I$; for longer apertures they are leakage-controlled operators that are approximately orthogonal and are treated as band-energy statistics. We define a matched-subspace STAP test statistic by flow-subspace energy on whitened snapshots, e.g.,
\[
  S_{\mathrm{stap,pre}}(i) := \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K
  \frac{y_{i,k}\Hh \Pf\, y_{i,k}}{y_{i,k}\Hh (I-\Pf)\, y_{i,k}+\rho\, y_{i,k}\Hh y_{i,k}+\epsilon},
\]
and form a pixel-level score map $S_{\mathrm{stap,pre}}(y,x)$ by overlap-add aggregation of the contributing tile statistics. This pre-KA score is the primary STAP detector output used for quantitative reporting unless explicitly stated otherwise.

\paragraph{Regularization and self-normalization avoidance.}
If we whitened using the exact sample covariance formed from the same snapshots being scored, the statistic collapses: the whitened sample covariance becomes (approximately) identity and averaged subspace energies concentrate near constants (e.g.\ $\tr(\Pf)$), destroying discrimination. We avoid this by whitening with a \emph{regularized} covariance $R_i$ (robust estimation plus shrinkage/diagonal loading), so that typically $R_i \neq \wh R_i$ and the matched-subspace energy remains informative. We do not rely on an analytic null distribution for CFAR operation; operating points are empirically calibrated on labeled $H_0$ sets under the fixed-profile protocol of Section~\ref{sec:eval}.

\paragraph{Matched-subspace statistic as a whitened subspace detector.}
Fix tile $i$ and consider a single slow-time snapshot $x\in\mathbb{C}^{L_t}$.
Under a Gaussian clutter-plus-noise model,
\[
  H_0:\ x \sim \mathcal{CN}(0,R_i),\qquad
  H_1:\ x = A_f s + n,\ \ s\sim \mathcal{CN}(0,\sigma_s^2 I),\ \ n\sim \mathcal{CN}(0,R_i),
\]
where the columns of $A_f$ span the flow subspace (equivalently the range of $\Pf$).
After whitening $y=R_i^{-1/2}x$, the GLRT for $\sigma_s^2>0$ versus $\sigma_s^2=0$ is monotone with the whitened subspace energy
\[
  T(x)=x\Hh R_i^{-1/2}\Pf R_i^{-1/2}x = y\Hh \Pf\, y,
\]
which is a standard matched-subspace / adaptive energy detector. \cite{KayDetection,ScharfFriedlanderMatchedSubspace}
In implementation we report a self-normalized ACE/MSD-style ratio score that normalizes flow-subspace energy by the orthogonal complement (and a small ridge), which is monotone in $T(x)$ and serves as our pre-KA detector score $S_{\mathrm{stap,pre}}(i)$.

\subsection{Optional shrink-only tail regularization (KA safety layer)}
\noindent While the matched-subspace statistic $S_{\mathrm{stap,pre}}$ effectively isolates flow under standard colored-interference models, structured artifacts can still dominate extreme-tail operating points. We therefore study an \emph{optional} knowledge-aided (KA) layer that applies a conservative, shrink-only score penalty in high-risk regions. The KA mapping is deterministic and uses auxiliary band-energy and proxy-support statistics only as \emph{side information}; proxies are never used to compute ROC/TPR/FPR (only to define protected/candidate sets and compute masks, which are audited separately).

\begin{center}
\fbox{\begin{minipage}{0.95\linewidth}
\textbf{KA in one picture (at-a-glance summary).}
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item \textbf{When it activates:} Only when deterministic sanity/support checks pass (valid band discretization; non-flat risk features; sufficient proxy support). Otherwise it is inert ($w\equiv 1$).
  \item \textbf{What it changes:} Applies a bounded, shrink-only weight $w(i)\in(0,1]$ on an explicitly defined candidate set while preserving a protected do-not-penalize set (scores unchanged); the post-KA score is $S_{\mathrm{stap,post}}=w\,S_{\mathrm{stap,pre}}$.
  \item \textbf{What it cannot do:} It cannot increase any pixel score (no amplification) and proxies are not used for ROC/TPR/FPR reporting; any apparent operating-point uplift reflects background-tail compression under shrink-only mapping.
  \item \textbf{How it fails safely:} If out of regime, it defaults to ``KA off'' ($w\equiv 1$) and logs an explicit reason label, reducing to the pre-KA STAP detector.
\end{itemize}
\end{minipage}}
\end{center}
\paragraph{Shrink-only mapping (guarantee).}
We implement knowledge aid via a bounded weight $w(i)\in(0,1]$ applied in score space:
\[
  S_{\mathrm{stap,post}}(i)=w(i)\,S_{\mathrm{stap,pre}}(i),\qquad w(i)\in(0,1].
\]
Equivalently, define the per-tile scale $\gamma(i):=1/w(i)\ge 1$ and write $S_{\mathrm{stap,post}}(i)=S_{\mathrm{stap,pre}}(i)/\gamma(i)$.
The construction of $w(i)$ (feature definitions, proxy sets, regimes, and deterministic sentinels) is provided in \SuppOrApp{app:ka-contract-theory} and \SuppOrApp{app:failure-modes}.

\begin{lemma}[Shrink-only monotonicity]
\label{lem:shrink_only_monotonicity}
Let $w(i)\in(0,1]$ and define $S_{\mathrm{stap,post}}(i)=w(i)\,S_{\mathrm{stap,pre}}(i)$. For any threshold $\tau$,
\[
  \{S_{\mathrm{stap,post}}\ge\tau\}\subseteq \{S_{\mathrm{stap,pre}}\ge\tau\}.
\]
Consequently, on any subset $A$ (e.g.\ $H_0$), the tail probability cannot increase at fixed $\tau$:
\[
  \mathbb{P}\{S_{\mathrm{stap,post}}\ge\tau\mid A\}\le \mathbb{P}\{S_{\mathrm{stap,pre}}\ge\tau\mid A\}.
\]
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
If $S_{\mathrm{stap,post}}(i)\ge\tau$, then $S_{\mathrm{stap,pre}}(i)=S_{\mathrm{stap,post}}(i)/w(i)\ge\tau$ since $w(i)\le 1$.
\end{proof}
When reporting strict low-FPR operating points where $\tau$ is reselected post-KA to satisfy $\mathbb{P}\{S_{\mathrm{stap,post}}\ge\tau\mid H_0\}=\alpha$, changes in TPR reflect background-tail compression despite scores never increasing.

\subsection{Algorithmic integrity and deterministic safeguards}
To make the architecture auditable and to avoid leakage or discretization artifacts masquerading as performance gains, we enforce deterministic, label-free safeguards:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Conditional execution as a decision-rule variant.} To reduce latency, STAP can be skipped outside a flow-proxy region and replaced by the baseline score there. Because this changes the overall decision rule, any conditional-execution reporting is treated as a distinct algorithmic variant and is accompanied by leakage controls (same-window vs.\ disjoint-window vs.\ randomized proxy masks). Unless otherwise stated, conditional execution uses \emph{disjoint-window} proxy gating for detection reporting.
  \item \textbf{Short-ensemble band discretization sanity.} For extremely short ensembles (e.g.\ $N\in\{5,9,17\}$), the coarse frequency resolution $\Delta f=\mathrm{PRF}/N$ can cause requested bands to collapse or overlap after discretization. If the discretized bands are empty, DC-only, or overlapping, KA is disabled ($w\equiv 1$) with an explicit reason label.
  \item \textbf{Proxy-mask circularity control.} Proxy maps are never used to compute ROC/TPR/FPR; they are used only to define compute masks (conditional execution) or protected/candidate sets (KA), which are audited separately. Quantitative ROC claims are restricted to regimes with independent $H_1/H_0$ labels (simulator truth or B-mode structural geometry).
\end{itemize}

For auditability, we log explicit reason labels so that ``KA off'' ($w\equiv 1$) is an expected success mode under flat or non-actionable evidence. \SuppOrApp{app:ka-contract-theory} provides modality-agnostic sufficient conditions and additional sentinels used to interpret when the KA prior should be active or inert.

\section{Experimental Design}
\label{sec:experimental-design}

\subsection{Fixed-profile protocol and operating-point reporting}
\label{sec:eval}
All hyperparameters (tile geometry, aperture, band definitions, conditioning targets, and prior thresholds) are fixed per regime before reporting results. Detection is evaluated by thresholding the high-score tail (right tail) at fixed FPR targets:
\[
  \mathbb{P}\{S\ge \tau_\alpha\mid H_0\}=\alpha,\qquad
  \mathrm{TPR}(\alpha)=\mathbb{P}\{S\ge \tau_\alpha\mid H_1\}.
\]
For each experiment, we report the supported floor $fpr_{\min}=1/n_{\mathrm{neg}}$ and bootstrap uncertainty at the natural unit of variation (windows or frames, depending on regime). These choices enforce a strict low-FPR operating discipline and avoid over-interpreting unsupported tail points.

\paragraph{Threshold calibration and deployment.}
Unless otherwise stated, $\tau_\alpha$ is calibrated per window (or per cine sequence) on that unit's negative set to report $\mathrm{TPR}(\alpha)$ at the intended operating point; in labeled regimes, the negative set is available for \emph{exact} calibration, while in deployment it would be estimated via a CFAR-style training procedure or from a fixed calibration set. This per-window protocol should therefore not be read as requiring oracle per-window tuning in deployment. We accordingly report a cross-window threshold-transfer audit for Brain-* pilots (Table~\ref{tab:brain_crosswindow_calibration}), which calibrates $\tau_\alpha$ on one window and evaluates on a disjoint window and quantifies the resulting realized FPR drift.

\subsection{Integrity checks and deterministic sentinels}

This work is a reproducible processing pipeline (decode $\rightarrow$ baseline $\rightarrow$ STAP score $\rightarrow$ optional KA regularization). Several practical pathologies can invalidate tail-rate reporting or spuriously activate the KA layer (e.g.\ missing acquisition metadata, coarse short-ensemble band discretization, proxy-mask circularity, or compute-gating leakage). We therefore enforce deterministic, label-free integrity checks that either (i) record provenance and proceed conservatively or (ii) disable KA ($w\equiv 1$) with an explicit reason label. A concise checklist (and where each sentinel is logged) is provided in \SuppOrApp{app:failure-modes}.

\label{sec:results-overview}

\begin{itemize}[nosep]

\paragraph{Why PD-after-filter baselines can collapse in the strict tail.}
A baseline clutter filter can yield nontrivial separation at moderate operating points yet still fail at ultra-low FPR if the background score distribution has an extreme right tail: the background-calibrated threshold $\tau_\alpha$ (e.g.\ the $99.9$th percentile of $H_0$ at $\alpha=10^{-3}$) can lie above essentially all $H_1$ pixels, yielding $\mathrm{TPR}(\alpha)\approx 0$ by construction.
For example, in a representative Brain-OpenSkull window the MC--SVD+PD baseline background quantiles are $q_{0.99}\approx 5.9$ and $q_{0.999}\approx 9.3$ while the flow $q_{0.999}\approx 0.094$, so no flow pixels exceed the $\alpha=10^{-3}$ threshold; Figure~\ref{fig:brain_tail_collapse_visual} visualizes the corresponding tail geometry ($\tau_{10^{-3}}>\max(H_1)$) for the baseline PD score and contrasts it with the STAP score computed on the same residual.
This tail-dominance failure mode motivates both the strict low-FPR operating discipline and detectors that explicitly reshape the tail (here: tile-local covariance whitening and matched-subspace scoring).

\paragraph{Latency and conditional execution.}
We also report representative runtimes for baseline clutter suppression and STAP under a conservative fixed operating profile (tile geometry, slow-time aperture $L_t$, and covariance-conditioning targets fixed \emph{a priori}). Conditional execution reduces runtime by skipping the STAP stage on tiles with negligible proxy flow support and reverting to the baseline score there. Because this changes the overall decision rule, it can change operating characteristics; we therefore treat conditional execution as a distinct variant and require explicit leakage controls when reporting it. Unless otherwise stated, conditional execution uses a \emph{disjoint-window} proxy mask for detection reporting (mask derived from one window and applied to a disjoint window) to avoid same-window proxy leakage.

\noindent Latency is measured via deterministic replay scripts that run both a \emph{legacy} path (eager execution; CUDA graphs disabled) and an \emph{optimized} path (CUDA graphs enabled when supported; \texttt{STAP\_FAST\_CUDA\_GRAPH=1}). We report \emph{steady-state} means over windows/frames 2..N and exclude the first replay window/frame, which includes one-time GPU overheads (initialization, kernel compilation, and optional CUDA-graph capture). The baseline and STAP stages are timed separately (excluding disk I/O), and ``Total'' reports baseline+STAP wall time; full legacy-versus-optimized per-window statistics and parity checks for the RTX~4080~SUPER reruns are written to \path{reports/latency/4080super_latency_summary.csv} / \path{reports/latency/4080super_latency_summary.json}.

\paragraph{Portable-workstation feasibility (tempered).}
A common critique of covariance-adaptive STAP is that local covariance conditioning and the associated matrix factorizations/inversions are prohibitively expensive when applied densely across an image grid. Tables~\ref{tab:latency_summary} and~\ref{tab:latency_summary_4080super} show that, with a batched GPU implementation and steady-state reporting, the Brain-* and Gammex regimes run in sub-second baseline+STAP wall time on both tested GPUs (Brain-* full totals $\approx0.77$--$0.78$~s on RTX~4080~SUPER), while Shin runs in $\approx0.17$~s total on RTX~4080~SUPER under the same steady-state replay protocol.\footnote{Latency is reported from steady-state replay (windows/frames 2..N) and excludes one-time GPU overheads (initialization, compilation, optional CUDA-graph capture) as well as disk I/O. For context, a 64-frame Doppler window at PRF $1500$~Hz corresponds to $64/1500\approx 43$~ms of acquired slow-time data, and a 128-frame window at PRF $1000$~Hz corresponds to $128$~ms. The present Python prototype therefore does not yet achieve real-time throughput at the full acquisition PRF, but it establishes practical feasibility on commodity laptop/workstation GPUs and motivates further optimization and tighter scanner integration.} These results suggest that tile-local covariance adaptation need not require server-class hardware, but they also highlight that throughput remains strongly dataset/profile dependent and should be interpreted with the explicit decision-rule caveats of conditional execution.

\noindent A leakage ablation confirms that conditional execution can inflate apparent performance under same-window proxy gating, while leakage-controlled gating yields a small TPR decrease (order $10^{-3}$) at the target FPRs; we therefore treat conditional execution as a distinct decision rule rather than a cost-free speedup. \paragraph{Dataset and acquisition parameters.}
We use the open-access Doppler Twinkling Artifact database \cite{LeonovTwinklingDD2021}, which provides complex beamformer output from phantom acquisitions on a clinical scanner. Each cine frame contains a contemporaneous B-mode image and a short-ensemble color-flow (CFM) slow-time block with $N\in\{5,9,17\}$ pulses. Because Doppler PRF is not reliably available in the provided metadata for these acquisitions, all Twinkling experiments treat PRF as an explicit experimental assumption frozen per sequence family (listed in \SuppOrApp{app:twinkling_prf_decode}) and enforce band-discretization sanity checks that disable KA ($w\equiv 1$) when discretized Pf/Pa bands are empty or overlapping (Section~\ref{sec:ka-contract-v2}). Decoding/QC details are provided in \SuppOrApp{app:twinkling_prf_decode} (PRF overrides and decoder sanity); reproduction commands and manifests are provided in \SuppOrApp{app:repro}.

\paragraph{B-mode-only structural tube masks (independent $H_1/H_0$).}
For the Gammex flow phantom sequences we derive evaluation masks from B-mode structure only (no Doppler/STAP outputs). Using acquisition geometry metadata that specifies the CFM region-of-interest (ROI) within the B-mode grid (beam and sample offsets and any beam subsampling), we map the B-mode ROI onto the CFM grid and segment the tube lumen on a median log-envelope reference image. This yields (i) a conservative lumen (flow) mask, eroded to avoid wall pixels and (ii) a background mask that excludes a guard band around the lumen and bright reflectors. Mask provenance and QC metrics are recorded with each experiment; Figure~\ref{fig:twinkling_gammex_masks} shows representative overlays. These structural labels support a defensible phantom ROC.

\subsection{ULM Zenodo 7883227 (real kHz IQ): baseline calibration and nonrigid motion robustness}
\label{sec:ulm7883227}

\paragraph{Dataset and loader.}
We use a real kHz IQ dataset released for ultrasound localization microscopy benchmarking \cite{ChavignonZenodo7883227,HeilesULMBenchmark2021} and distributed as HDF5 blocks with accompanying acquisition metadata. Each block contains a dataset \texttt{iq} of shape $(2,H,W,T)=(2,256,180,800)$ float32 representing I/Q over $T=800$ slow-time frames. We load blocks on demand and return complex IQ as a cube of shape $(T,H,W)$ (reproduction details in \SuppOrApp{app:repro}).

\paragraph{One-time label-free baseline selection.}
For ULM we select a single MC--SVD energy-fraction cutoff by a one-time label-free sweep on blocks 1--3 over frames $0{:}128$, and then freeze it for all subsequent ULM motion experiments. The sweep uses baseline-only runs and relies on label-free integrity and stability criteria (no task labels); it yields a frozen energy fraction $e=0.975$ (full sweep details in \SuppOrApp{app:repro}).

\paragraph{Nonrigid motion injection and fairness alignment ablation.}
We evaluate motion robustness by injecting controlled motion directly into the beamformed IQ cube and re-running baseline+STAP under a frozen ULM profile (PRF $=1000$ Hz; $L_t=64$; tiles $8\times 8$; stride 3; SCM covariance; diagonal loading $\lambda=0.07$; baseline MC--SVD with frozen $e=0.975$). Because motion is injected at the beamformed IQ stage, this test stresses post-beamforming robustness to residual motion mismatch; it does not model channel-domain decorrelation or skull-induced phase-aberration physics. Motion ladders span two nonrigid families: \texttt{brainlike} (rigid random-walk translation plus a smooth, depth-decaying elastic warp with temporally correlated coefficients and micro-jitter) and \texttt{elastic} (nonrigid-only; rigid and micro-jitter disabled). For each amplitude we compute label-free robustness endpoints on the baseline score $S_{\mathrm{base}}$ and the matched-subspace STAP score $S_{\mathrm{stap,pre}}$ relative to the no-motion reference under three \emph{common} alignment modes (none / common B-mode / injected GT proxy) plus a diagnostic per-method ``self'' alignment. We additionally report degeneracy checks (std ratios, top-$k$ overlap, and tail quantile ratios) to rule out ``stability by collapse.'' Figure~\ref{fig:ulm_brainlike_e975} shows the main \texttt{brainlike} sweep; additional motion variants are provided in \SuppOrApp{app:supp-results}.

\emph{Endpoint definition.} For a score map $S_{\mathrm{meth}}(a)$ at motion amplitude $a$ and the corresponding no-motion reference $S_{\mathrm{meth}}(0)$ (same block), we compute $\rho_{\mathrm{meth}}(a)$ as the Pearson correlation between the aligned maps on a central crop $\Omega_a$ that excludes a margin of $\lceil a\rceil+2$ pixels to avoid boundary artifacts from warping. Correlations are computed directly on the exported right-tail scores (no log transform) and use the same crop/alignment for baseline and STAP in the ``common'' alignment modes.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection*{A) Robustness in the ultra-low-FPR tail}
This work adapts space--time adaptive processing (STAP), a foundational approach in radar target detection under heavy clutter, to functional ultrasound (fUS) slow-time flow detection. Under the strict low-FPR operating protocol (Section~\ref{sec:eval}), we find that common global or semi-global clutter suppressors (MC--SVD, RPCA, HOSVD) can lose sensitivity in the extreme right tail, while the proposed tile-based matched-subspace STAP score retains measurable sensitivity at ultra-low FPR (i.e., nonzero $\mathrm{TPR}(\alpha)$ at $\alpha\approx 10^{-4}$--$10^{-3}$ under empirically supported tail floors). This behavior is demonstrated on labeled k-Wave simulations and structurally labeled \emph{in vitro} phantoms, where flow/background masks are defined independently from Doppler/STAP outputs via simulator truth or B-mode geometry. The within-ensemble motion ladders on phantom acquisitions further indicate that STAP retains detection stability under controlled per-shot motion corruption, consistent with its reliance on localized covariance rather than global low-rank structure.

\subsection*{B) Knowledge-aided prior as a fail-safe}
The optional knowledge-aided (KA) layer is designed as a conservative safety regularizer rather than an unconstrained signal amplifier. It acts as a shrink-only prior penalty with an auditable (region, reason) output: when spatial-spectral evidence is flat or non-actionable, the prior defaults to $\mathcal{R}_0$ (uninformative; $w\equiv 1$) and leaves the pre-KA detector score unchanged. When the prior lies in $\mathcal{R}_1/\mathcal{R}_2$, it preserves an explicit do-not-penalize protected set (scores unchanged on high-confidence flow locations) and applies only non-increasing shrink on high-risk regions, so that ``KA does nothing'' is an intended success mode out of regime. This posture prioritizes transparent regime diagnostics and avoids score inflation in regimes where spectral evidence cannot justify aggressive intervention.

\subsection*{C) Data limitations: missing raw high-PRF complex IQ}
A key limitation in evaluating Doppler-side covariance detectors is field-wide data availability: most public \emph{in vivo} fUS releases do not include raw, high-PRF complex IQ ensembles needed to evaluate covariance-based Doppler detectors with independent $H_1/H_0$ labels. Many releases are temporally downsampled, post-filtered, or provided only as power-Doppler equivalents, which can irreversibly destroy the high-rate complex phase history needed for spatiotemporal covariance estimation and band-energy integrity checks. Accordingly, while we report real-data instrumentation (band-energy integrity, prior-regime behavior, and label-free stability under controlled motion corruption), comprehensive \emph{in vivo} ROC evaluation of Doppler-side STAP/KA requires high-PRF raw IQ acquisitions prior to proprietary clutter suppression, with reliable metadata and task/landmark structure to define independent evaluation labels.

\section{Conclusion and future work}
This study establishes a deterministic, auditable KA-STAP framework for fUS slow-time flow detection under a tune-once-then-freeze discipline. Using a strict low-FPR operating protocol and independent structural labels where available, we show that localized matched-subspace STAP can preserve separability in regimes where conventional low-rank baselines degrade in the extreme tail, while the KA spatial-spectral prior provides shrink-only, protected-set-preserving risk control with explicit out-of-regime behavior.

The corrected SIMUS/PyMUST moving-scatterer benchmark reported in the Supplement should be interpreted as a realism and fairness checkpoint rather than the final clinical simulator. SIMUS provides a practical open moving-scatterer RF$\rightarrow$IQ$\rightarrow$beamforming path and has been compared against Field~II, k-Wave, FOCUS, and Verasonics in controlled settings, but it remains an approximate linear weak-scattering model and therefore still requires empirical anchoring before it can stand in for a clinical acquisition model \cite{GarciaSIMUSPartI2022,CigierSIMUSPartII2022}. Our clinically anchored \emph{SIMUS-ClinUtility-v2} track keeps that moving-scatterer forward model, but calibrates nuisance realism to real-data telemetry before any method refreeze. The target use cases are awake/intraoperative functional and vascular mapping, adult-human imaging through an acoustically transparent cranial window, and mobile human fUS under walking-like motion \cite{Soloukey2020FUSAwake,Rabut2024,MobileFUS2024,Deffieux2021}. The primary detection task remains unaliased parenchymal microvascular Pf detection, while superficial high-velocity or alias-dominant vessels are treated as clinically meaningful nuisance negatives rather than folded into the positive class. This is the regime where the ultrasound clutter-filtering literature already predicts sensitivity to spatially varying clutter, tissue motion, and local rank-selection errors \cite{DemeneClutterSVD2015,BarangerAdaptiveSVD2018,YuLovstakkenEigenReview2010,SongBlockwiseLocal2017,HuangAdaptiveHOSVD2024}. In practice, \emph{v2} begins by freezing machine-readable anchor envelopes from real IQ/PD datasets before changing any simulator profile: Shin RatBrain and ULM~7883227 provide brain-like slow-time IQ anchors, Gammex provides structured nuisance and artifact-heavy phantom anchors, and Mac\'e provides a functional/readout anchor at the PD/atlas level \cite{ChavignonZenodo7883227,LeonovTwinklingDD2021,UrbanWholeBrain2015}. Under that protocol the accepted nuisance profiles are now \texttt{ClinMobile-Pf-v2} and \texttt{ClinIntraOpParenchyma-Pf-v3}; the more surface-dominated intraoperative profile remains telemetry-only until a stronger real-IQ anchor can be defended.

The intraoperative profile remains the critical realism step, but the main lesson from the held-out seed audits is now more specific. A first mechanically coupled low-frequency background redesign was not enough to stabilize the monolithic \texttt{ClinIntraOp-Pf-v2} scene, so we split the intraoperative track into (i) a competitive \emph{parenchymal mapping} profile, where the accepted ordinary background remains brain-like and superficial high-velocity or specular structures are modeled explicitly as nuisance layers, and (ii) a development-only surface/coupling profile that remains descriptive until stronger real-IQ anchors exist for that regime. This split is more consistent with the clutter-filtering literature, which treats tissue background as coherent and low-rank but spatially heterogeneous enough to motivate adaptive-global and local filters \cite{YuLovstakkenEigenReview2010,SongBlockwiseLocal2017,BarangerAdaptiveSVD2018}, and with the intended intraoperative and cranial-window settings, where the parenchymal target is clinically central even when superficial contamination is present \cite{Soloukey2020FUSAwake,Rabut2024,Deffieux2021}. The decisive telemetry finding was that the competitive intraoperative profile became stable only after decoupling the nuisance realization from the parenchymal scene seed: the parenchymal scene seed is still free to vary geometry, scatterers, and flow, but the nuisance motion/localized-motion/phase template is now frozen. Under that narrower and more defensible hypothesis, the competitive intraoperative profile \texttt{ClinIntraOpParenchyma-Pf-v3} passes the unchanged hard gate on the burned redesign seeds and on untouched confirmation seeds, while the development-only surface/coupling profile remains descriptive.

We have now rerun fresh-seed frozen-profile refreezes on the accepted \emph{v2} structural and functional tracks. Structurally, the conclusion sharpens in two stages. First, if STAP is treated only as the historical \mbox{MC--SVD$\rightarrow$STAP} family, a single frozen STAP profile still gives the strongest nuisance-separation behavior and the best combined low-tail selection score, but it no longer dominates the simpler \mbox{$\mathrm{AUC}(\mathrm{main},\mathrm{bg})$} metric by itself on the harder accepted benchmark; on that single summary metric, the best frozen RPCA family can be slightly higher. Second, once the comparison is made at the level of a \emph{frozen deployed stack} rather than a fixed historical upstream choice, the remaining gap closes without changing detector mathematics. A joint frozen search over the accepted development split selects \mbox{RPCA$\rightarrow$STAP} with \texttt{Brain-SIMUS-Clin-MotionRobust-v0}; on held-out accepted \emph{v2} seeds this stack reaches \mbox{$\mathrm{AUC}(\mathrm{main},\mathrm{bg})=0.828$}, \mbox{$\mathrm{AUC}(\mathrm{main},\mathrm{nuis})=0.920$}, and nuisance \mbox{$\mathrm{FPR}@\mathrm{TPR}_{\mathrm{main}}=0.5$} of 0.031. The same-residual native reference (\mbox{RPCA$\rightarrow$Kasai}) preserves slightly higher \mbox{$\mathrm{AUC}(\mathrm{main},\mathrm{bg})$} but gives far weaker nuisance control (\mbox{$\mathrm{AUC}(\mathrm{main},\mathrm{nuis})=0.442$}, nuisance \mbox{FPR=0.593}), while the fastest strong native simple stack (\mbox{HOSVD$\rightarrow$Kasai}) runs in only $\approx 0.17$~s per held-out case on the RTX~4080~SUPER but again gives much weaker nuisance separation. The latency frontier is therefore more informative than any single runtime row. In the same accepted structural setting, a much faster frozen STAP operating point (\mbox{Adaptive~Global~SVD$\rightarrow$STAP}) runs in $\approx 0.34$~s per held-out case while still improving all three structural summary metrics over \mbox{HOSVD$\rightarrow$Kasai} (\mbox{$\mathrm{AUC}(\mathrm{main},\mathrm{bg})=0.813$}, \mbox{$\mathrm{AUC}(\mathrm{main},\mathrm{nuis})=0.874$}, nuisance \mbox{FPR=0.078}). By contrast, the accepted headline structural stack runs in $\approx 2.15$~s per held-out case, with the STAP stage itself adding $\approx 0.47$~s beyond the upstream RPCA residualizer. For translational readers, the practical structural implication is therefore two-fold: \mbox{RPCA$\rightarrow$STAP} is the highest-quality accepted stack when nuisance suppression is the priority, whereas \mbox{Adaptive~Global~SVD$\rightarrow$STAP} is the clearest low-latency STAP operating point. In both cases STAP is best understood not as ``a better clutter filter'' in isolation, but as a nuisance-focused detector head whose value becomes clearest when paired with a strong upstream residualizer.

The functional checkpoint is more mixed at the full pipeline level but still supports the same detector-head interpretation. Using only the accepted \emph{v2} profiles and a common \texttt{bgcdf\_outside\_glm} readout, the best end-to-end held-out pipeline remains profile-dependent: intraoperative parenchymal mapping prefers an \mbox{RPCA$\rightarrow$PD-after-STAP} stack, while the mobile profile prefers \mbox{HOSVD$\rightarrow$PD}. However, the stricter stage-symmetric head audit remains favorable to STAP: on the broader held-out split, STAP is the better detector head in 11/12 accepted family/profile comparisons, with the sole remaining miss an effective tie. In that audit the mean native-head runtime is $\approx 0.27$~s per held-out functional case, the mean STAP-head runtime is $\approx 0.44$~s, and the mean held-out selection-score gain is +0.088. Taken together, the structural and functional checkpoints support a clear translational narrative: STAP is most useful clinically as a detector head that reduces nuisance-driven errors and improves downstream map hygiene after ordinary clutter suppression, rather than as a universal replacement for upstream residualization.

Accordingly, the next simulator steps are to (i) keep \texttt{ClinIntraOpSurface-Pf-dev0} telemetry-only until a stronger surface-dominated real-IQ anchor can be defended, (ii) extend the accepted-profile functional benchmark to larger held-out splits and real-data retrospective audits, and only then (iii) consider detector-level changes if a single frozen STAP head no longer remains competitive on the accepted clinically anchored benchmark. In parallel, we will continue to reduce latency via hardware-aware implementations of the batched covariance and band-energy kernels toward real-time deployment, and will explore compact frozen learned regularizers only when they preserve the shrink-only auditability and low-FPR reporting guarantees established here.

\section{Practical Band Design for Brain fUS (PRF 1.5--5 kHz, T 8--16)}
\label{sec:band-geometry}

We provide practical band guidelines for selecting Pf/guard/Pa with explicit physical anchors.

For brain fUS at center frequencies $f_0 \approx 6$--$15$ MHz and PRF $\approx 1.5$--$5$ kHz \cite{Mace2013,Rabut2020,Imbault2017,Urban2022,LampeFUSBrain}:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Microvascular brain flows (arterioles/venules) in humans and rodents are typically in the range $v \approx 3$--$20$ mm/s for the vessels that dominate functional responses \cite{Imbault2017,Urban2022}. At $f_0=7.5$ MHz and $c\approx 1540$ m/s, this corresponds to Doppler shifts $f_D\approx 30$--$200$ Hz.
  \item Low--frequency tissue motion (respiration, cardiac pulsation) produces strong spectral content below $\approx 20$ Hz; this should be contained in Po and largely removed by high--pass filtering and/or SVD.
  \item Large--vessel flows (e.g.\ MCA--like arteries) reach $v\approx 0.3$--$0.8$ m/s, corresponding to $f_D$ in the kHz range \cite{TCDVelocities,TCDOptimalFlow}. Depending on PRF and angle, a portion of this energy appears in high Doppler bands and can alias into the $>$400 Hz region corresponding to Pa \cite{ICDFUS2021,MobileFUS2024}.
\end{itemize}

Thus a reasonable fixed band design for brain fUS with $T\approx 8$--$16$ and PRF $\approx 1.5$--$3$ kHz is:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Flow band $\Cf$: $[30,250]$ Hz --- microvascular flows $\approx 3$--$20$ mm/s.
  \item Guard band $\Cg$: $[250,400]$ Hz --- protects against band--edge leakage.
  \item Alias band $\Ca$: $[400, \text{Nyquist}]$ Hz --- high Doppler where large--vessel/aliased flows and certain motion artifacts live.
\end{itemize}

In discrete terms, for $T=8$ at PRF=1.5 kHz (bin spacing $187.5$ Hz), this maps to:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item $\Pf$ on first off--DC bin (with DPSS leakage covering $\approx 30$--$250$ Hz),
  \item guard around the second bin,
  \item $\Pa$ on higher bins with bin--level guard as per BG--1/BG--2.
\end{itemize}

\section{PD-only KA-prior summary panel (hemodynamic): band-energy statistics and aggregation}
\label{app:pdonly-dashboard}

This appendix specifies a PD-only instantiation of the KA spatial-spectral prior and the associated summary-panel aggregation used for systematic plane sweeps (e.g.\ Mac{\'e}/Urban whole-brain mouse fUS). The key discipline is unchanged: \emph{runtime} prior selection is label-free (proxy sets), while atlas-defined $H_1/H_0$ labels are used only \emph{offline} for descriptive reporting and are never used as prior inputs.

\subsection*{A) PD-only band-energy feature definitions (math)}
\paragraph{PD movie and tiling.}
Fix one coronal plane (one ``configuration'') with a PD movie
\[
P[t,y,x]\in\R_{\ge 0},\qquad t=0,\dots,T-1,
\]
sampled at rate $f_s=1/dt$ (Mac{\'e}: $f_s\approx 10$~Hz). Tile each frame into overlapping spatial tiles $\Omega_i$ of size $h\times w$ (Mac{\'e}: $8\times 8$) with stride $s$ (Mac{\'e}: $s=3$).

\paragraph{Tile PD time series.}
Define the tile-averaged PD time series
\[
p_i[t] := \frac{1}{|\Omega_i|}\sum_{(y,x)\in\Omega_i} P[t,y,x].
\]
For robustness across tiles, we optionally use a log-stabilized, mean-removed series
\[
u_i[t] := \log(p_i[t]+\epsilon_{\mathrm{pd}}),\qquad
\tilde u_i[t] := u_i[t]-\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t'=0}^{T-1} u_i[t'].
\]

\paragraph{Sliding-window temporal embedding (Hankel snapshots) and whitening.}
Fix a slow-time aperture $L_t$ (tens of frames in PD-only). Define $K=T-L_t+1$ and form multiple slow-time snapshots using a sliding-window temporal embedding (Hankel matrix construction):
\[
x_{i,k} :=
\begin{bmatrix}
\tilde u_i[k]\\
\tilde u_i[k+1]\\
\vdots\\
\tilde u_i[k+L_t-1]
\end{bmatrix}\in\R^{L_t},\qquad k=0,\dots,K-1.
\]
Estimate a per-tile covariance (SCM or robust variant)
\[
\wh R_i := \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=0}^{K-1} x_{i,k}x_{i,k}\Hh,
\]
apply conditioning/diagonal loading to obtain $R_i$, and whiten
\[
y_{i,k} := R_i^{-1/2}x_{i,k}.
\]
We pool whitened snapshots to a single per-tile slow-time vector
\[
z_i := \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=0}^{K-1} y_{i,k}\in\R^{L_t}.
\]

\paragraph{Hemodynamic band projectors and energies.}
Let $\Cf,\Cg,\Ca$ denote Pf/guard/Pa hemodynamic bands (Mac{\'e}: $\Cf=[0,0.5]$~Hz, $\Cg=(0.5,1.0]$~Hz, $\Ca=(1.0,3.0]$~Hz). Build fixed temporal projectors $(\Pf,\Pg,\Pa)$ onto the span of discrete Fourier tones whose frequencies lie in these bands, and define $\Po := I-\Pf-\Pg-\Pa$ (``other''/out-of-band). Define band energies
\[
E_f(i) := z_i\Hh \Pf z_i,\quad
E_g(i) := z_i\Hh \Pg z_i,\quad
E_a(i) := z_i\Hh \Pa z_i,\quad
E_o(i) := z_i\Hh \Po z_i,
\]
and $E_{\mathrm{tot}}(i) := E_f+E_g+E_a+E_o+\epsilon$.

\paragraph{Risk metrics and Pf-peak statistics.}
We use the same KA feature primitives as in the Doppler regime:
\[
m_{\mathrm{alias}}(i) := \log\frac{E_a(i)+\epsilon}{E_f(i)+\epsilon},\qquad
m_{\mathrm{guard}}(i) := \log\frac{E_g(i)+\epsilon}{E_f(i)+\epsilon},\qquad
r_g(i) := \frac{E_g(i)}{E_{\mathrm{tot}}(i)}.
\]
Define a whitened periodogram of the pooled vector $z_i$,
\[
\wh z_i[n] := \sum_{\ell=0}^{L_t-1} z_i[\ell]\,e^{-2\pi i n\ell/L_t},\qquad
S_i[n] := |\wh z_i[n]|^2,\qquad n=0,\dots,\lfloor L_t/2\rfloor,
\]
and the (non-DC) peak frequency $f_{\mathrm{peak}}(i)=\frac{n_{\mathrm{peak}}}{L_t dt}$ where
$n_{\mathrm{peak}}(i):=\arg\max_{n\ge 1} S_i[n]$. The Pf-peak indicator and occupancy on a set $\mathcal{T}$ are
\[
\pi_f(i) := \mathbf{1}\{f_{\mathrm{peak}}(i)\in\Cf\},\qquad
\mathrm{PfPeakFrac}(\mathcal{T}) := \frac{1}{|\mathcal{T}|}\sum_{i\in\mathcal{T}}\pi_f(i).
\]

\subsection*{B) PD-only proxy sets and prior inputs (math)}
The PD-only prior uses label-free proxy sets based on a PD-derived flow-coverage proxy, analogous to the IQ flow-mask coverage $c_f$.
Let $\bar P[y,x] := \frac{1}{T}\sum_t P[t,y,x]$ be the mean PD image (optionally restricted to a brain mask). Define a high-quantile flow proxy mask
\[
M_{\mathrm{flow}}(y,x) := \mathbf{1}\{\bar P[y,x]\ge Q_{q_{\mathrm{flow}}}(\bar P)\},
\]
and per-tile flow coverage
\[
c_f(i) := \frac{1}{|\Omega_i|}\sum_{(y,x)\in\Omega_i} M_{\mathrm{flow}}(y,x)\in[0,1].
\]
With frozen design thresholds $c_{\mathrm{bg}}$ and $c_{\mathrm{flow}}$ (typical $c_{\mathrm{bg}}\approx 0.05$, $c_{\mathrm{flow}}\approx 0.20$), define
\[
\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}} := \{i:\ c_f(i)\le c_{\mathrm{bg}}\},\quad
\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{flow}} := \{i:\ c_f(i)\ge c_{\mathrm{flow}}\},\quad
\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{nonbg}} := \{i:\ c_f(i)>c_{\mathrm{bg}}\}.
\]
If $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{flow}}$ is too small (insufficient proxy support), a conservative fallback is to define proxy sets via tilewise mean-$\bar P$ quantiles and record this fallback choice in the exported metadata.

\subsection*{C) Prior-regime selection for PD-only (math)}
\paragraph{Baseline score for PD-only prior.}
For Mac{\'e}, the baseline scalar detector is PD-z on tile time series: choose a baseline segment $B$ (e.g.\ first third of frames) and define
\[
s_{\mathrm{base}}(i) := \max_{t\notin B} \frac{u_i[t]-\mu_i^{(B)}}{\sigma_i^{(B)}+\epsilon_z},
\]
with $\mu_i^{(B)},\sigma_i^{(B)}$ the mean and standard deviation of $u_i[t]$ over $t\in B$. Higher $s_{\mathrm{base}}$ means more activation.

\paragraph{Risk-mode selection and integrity checks.}
The PD-only prior follows the same posture as the Doppler-IQ prior:
if risk statistics are flat (e.g.\ $\mathrm{IQR}(m_{\mathrm{alias}}\mid\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}})$ too small) or proxy support is insufficient, we default to the uninformative regime ($\mathcal{R}_0$; $w\equiv 1$) and record an explicit disablement reason in the metadata. If guard dominates (e.g.\ $Q_{0.90}(r_g\mid\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}})$ above a frozen threshold), we disallow the experimental alias-dominant regime $\mathcal{R}_2$ and either remain in a guard-risk safety lane ($\mathcal{R}_1$; requiring tail association of $r_g$) or default to $\mathcal{R}_0$ as out of regime.

\paragraph{Actionability checks (experimental alias-dominant regime $\mathcal{R}_2$, label-free).}
The experimental alias-dominant regime $\mathcal{R}_2$ (research-only eligibility; reporting-only) requires additional evidence that risk statistics explain high-score background tails. In the selected risk mode ($m_{\mathrm{risk}}=m_{\mathrm{alias}}$ or $m_{\mathrm{guard}}$), define
\[
\Delta_{bf} := \operatorname{median}(m_{\mathrm{risk}}\mid\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}})-
               \operatorname{median}(m_{\mathrm{risk}}\mid\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{flow}}),
\]
and within $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}}$ define a tail subset $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{tail}}$ as the top $1\%$ of $s_{\mathrm{base}}$ and a mid subset $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{mid}}$ as a central quantile band; require
\[
\Delta_{\mathrm{tail}} := \operatorname{median}(m_{\mathrm{risk}}\mid\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{tail}})-
                          \operatorname{median}(m_{\mathrm{risk}}\mid\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{mid}})
                          \ge \Delta_{\mathrm{tail},\min}.
\]
Finally, we enforce a Pf-realization eligibility condition for $\mathcal{R}_2$:
\[
\mathrm{PfPeakFrac}(\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{flow}})\ge p_{f,\min}.
\]
If these checks fail, the prior remains in $\mathcal{R}_0$ or $\mathcal{R}_1$ (guarded only) and does not enter $\mathcal{R}_2$.

\paragraph{Coverage sentinels.}
As in the Doppler-IQ prior, penalization must be neither inert nor global: after defining protected/candidate sets (below), the penalized fraction
\[
p_{\mathrm{shrink}} := \mathbb{P}\{g=1\mid \mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{cand}}\}
\]
must lie in frozen bounds (e.g.\ $p_{\min}\le p_{\mathrm{shrink}}\le p_{\max}$), otherwise we default to $\mathcal{R}_0$ ($w\equiv 1$) and record a coverage-out-of-bounds sentinel.

\subsection*{D) PD-only shrink-only prior mapping (optional; summary-panel parity)}
The PD-only instantiation supports both a hard suppression used in the Mac{\'e} systematic-sweep reporting and a soft shrink mapping for summary-panel parity.
Define a protected set $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{prot}}$ (tiles with high flow coverage and/or very high baseline scores) and a candidate set $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{cand}}$ (non-protected tiles in an uncertain score window). Using a background-calibrated risk threshold $\tau_{\mathrm{risk}}=Q_{q_{\mathrm{risk}}}(m_{\mathrm{risk}}\mid\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}})$, define the penalization indicator
\[
g(i)=\mathbf{1}\{i\in\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{cand}},\ m_{\mathrm{risk}}(i)\ge\tau_{\mathrm{risk}}\}.
\]
\emph{Hard suppression (Mac{\'e} sweep):} apply the binary indicator $g$ as a hard suppression on positive activations,
\[
s_{\mathrm{KA}}(i)=s_{\mathrm{base}}(i) - g(i)\max(s_{\mathrm{base}}(i),0),
\]
which is equivalent to setting positive penalized tiles to zero while leaving negative scores unchanged. \emph{Soft shrink (summary-panel parity):} set $w(i)=\max(w_{\min},\exp(-k\,u(i)))$ with a robust normalized exceedance $u(i)\ge 0$ and apply $s_{\mathrm{KA}}(i)=s_{\mathrm{base}}(i)-(1-w(i))\max(s_{\mathrm{base}}(i),0)$. In both cases, $g(i)=0$ (and $w(i)=1$) on $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{prot}}$ enforces the do-not-penalize constraint (protected-set scores unchanged) and $s_{\mathrm{KA}}(i)\le s_{\mathrm{base}}(i)$ enforces shrink-only behavior.

\subsection*{E) Summary-panel schema and aggregation (PD-only)}
For PD-only datasets the summary panel is computed per plane (configuration) and written as a CSV with one row per (scan, plane). Each row records: (i) identifiers and frozen settings (scan/plane, $T$, $f_s$, $L_t$, tile geometry, band edges), (ii) proxy support counts ($|\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}}|$, $|\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{flow}}|$), (iii) prior outputs (region $\in\{\mathcal{R}_0,\mathcal{R}_1,\mathcal{R}_2\}$, reason label, risk\_mode; exported in code as \texttt{state}/\texttt{reason}), (iv) key band-energy statistics ($\mathrm{PfPeakFrac}(\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{flow}})$, $Q_{0.90}(r_g\mid\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}})$, $\mathrm{IQR}(m_{\mathrm{risk}}\mid\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}})$, $\Delta_{bf}$, $\Delta_{\mathrm{tail}}$), and (v) KA coverage/strength when active ($p_{\mathrm{shrink}}$ and scale/shrink quantiles). Atlas-labeled descriptive fields are prefixed \texttt{offline\_} (e.g.\ \texttt{offline\_pf\_peak\_frac\_H1}, \texttt{offline\_pf\_peak\_frac\_H0}, \texttt{offline\_hits\_pre/post}) to make clear that they are not prior inputs.

Summary-panel aggregation mirrors the Doppler-IQ diagnostic panel: histograms of prior regions and (region, reason) pairs, a risk-mode histogram, distributions of key band-energy statistics (Pf peak fraction, guard q90, risk IQR), and (for active planes) shrink coverage/strength summaries. Any atlas-stratified Mac{\'e} plots (e.g.\ PfPeak(VIS/SC/LGd) vs PfPeak(CP/CA/DG), alias separation) are presented as a separate, explicitly offline/descriptive panel.

\subsection*{F) ``Do not overclaim'' messaging (PD-only summary panel)}
When reporting a PD-only prior summary panel, we use the following posture in captions and limitations:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item PD-only band-energy statistics operate on low-rate PD time series and are an auditability check; they do not validate Doppler-side uplift on raw IQ.
  \item The uninformative regime ($\mathcal{R}_0$; $w\equiv 1$) is an intended success outcome when feature evidence is flat or non-actionable; the summary panel is designed to make these cases explicit via reason labels.
  \item Atlas-stratified results on Mac{\'e} are descriptive; prior-positive slices identified by scanning the same dataset are not treated as held-out confirmation.
  \item The experimental alias-dominant regime ($\mathcal{R}_2$) is a reporting discipline requiring additional label-free actionability evidence; $\mathcal{R}_1$ is guarded only and should not be presented as a clinical performance guarantee.
\end{itemize}

\section{Algorithmic details (supplementary)}
\label{app:alg-details}

\subsection{Knowledge-Aided prior regime selection (frozen defaults)}
The KA prior consumes per-tile arrays
$S_{\mathrm{base}}$, $m_{\mathrm{alias}}$, $r_g$, $c_f$, and $\mathrm{PfPeak}$ and returns a compact regime report (region label, a disablement reason when inert, and diagnostic metrics). The reference implementation is provided with the accompanying code. The frozen default thresholds used throughout this repository are:
  \begin{itemize}[nosep]
	  \item \textbf{Proxy thresholds:} $c_{\mathrm{bg}}=0.05$, $c_{\mathrm{flow}}=0.20$.
	  \item \textbf{Candidate/protected score quantiles:} candidate window $q_{\mathrm{lo}}=0.30$, $q_{\mathrm{hi,cand}}=1.0$; optional high-score protection $q_{\mathrm{hi,prot}}=0.99999$ when the protected set includes extreme-score tiles.
	  \item \textbf{Risk threshold:} $q_{\mathrm{risk}}=0.90$ applied to $m_{\mathrm{alias}}$ on $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}}$.
  \item \textbf{Feature integrity (flatness + guard):} $\mathrm{IQR}(m_{\mathrm{alias}}\mid\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}})\ge 0.25$; actionable eligibility disabled if $Q_{0.90}(r_g)>0.25$; ``guard safety'' lane allowed up to $Q_{0.90}(r_g)\le 0.70$ if guard is actionable, requiring $\mathrm{IQR}(r_g\mid\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}})\ge 0.03$ and $\Delta_{\mathrm{tail,guard}}\ge 0.01$.
  \item \textbf{C2 actionability thresholds:} $\Delta_{bf}\ge 0.30$ and $\Delta_{\mathrm{tail}}\ge 0.20$ (computed on $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}}/\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{flow}}$ proxies as in \ifSTAPSUPPLEMENTONLY the main paper\else Section~\ref{sec:ka-contract-v2}\fi).
  \item \textbf{C2 Pf-realization eligibility:} $\mathrm{PfPeakFrac}(\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{flow}})\ge 0.05$ with $|\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{flow}}|\ge 50$.
  \item \textbf{Coverage bounds:} safety lane $p_{\mathrm{shrink}}\in[0.02,0.25]$ (guard lane $[0.02,0.15]$), actionable lane $p_{\mathrm{shrink}}\in[0.05,0.40]$.
	  \item \textbf{Invariance sentinel:} if $\mathrm{IQR}(\log w\mid \text{penalized}) < 0.02$ then return the off state with an invariance/no-effect sentinel.
	  \item \textbf{Sample support:} $n_{\min}=500$ valid tiles, $n_{\mathrm{bg},\min}=200$ background-proxy tiles, $n_{\mathrm{cand},\min}=200$ candidate tiles, $n_{\mathrm{tail},\min}=20$, $n_{\mathrm{mid},\min}=20$.
  \end{itemize}
The report exports one of three states (off / safety / actionable), corresponding to feature-space regimes $\mathcal{R}_0/\mathcal{R}_1/\mathcal{R}_2$ in the main text. When the prior is forced inert, the report includes an explicit reason category (e.g.\ missing inputs, insufficient proxy support, feature flatness, invariance/no-effect sentinels, or invalid band discretization in short ensembles). Reason labels are enumerated in the code and logged in the exported metadata.

\subsection{Score-space shrink mapping and exported maps}
When score-space KA is enabled, per-tile shrink factors $w(i)\in(0,1]$ are derived deterministically from the regime report and applied in score space as
\[
S_{\mathrm{post}}(i)=w(i)\,S_{\mathrm{pre}}(i),\qquad w(i)\in(0,1].
\]
For convenience (and compatibility with legacy \path{pd} arrays), bundles store an equivalent per-pixel \emph{scale} map $\gamma(i)=1/w(i)\ge 1$ as \path{ka_scale_map.npy}, averaged over overlapping tiles and applied only on the union of penalized tiles as
\[
\mathrm{PD}_{\mathrm{post}}(i)=\mathrm{PD}_{\mathrm{pre}}(i)/\gamma(i).
\]
A binary \path{ka_gate_map.npy} stores the penalized region. Protected pixels are enforced by setting $\gamma=1$ on the protected set (flow mask and extreme-score pixels as in \ifSTAPSUPPLEMENTONLY the main paper\else Section~\ref{sec:ka-contract-v2}\fi).

\paragraph{Implementation score conventions.}
Throughout the manuscript, quantitative low-FPR operating points are reported using flow-evidence score maps under a right-tail convention (higher = more flow-like; thresholds calibrated on the high-score tail) intended for labeled ROC and robustness reporting \ifSTAPSUPPLEMENTONLY (see the score taxonomy table in the main paper)\else (Table~\ref{tab:score_taxonomy})\fi. Exported bundles include the baseline score and the pre-KA matched-subspace STAP score, along with optional conventional baseline comparators (e.g.\ log-PD and Kasai) and diagnostic/proxy maps used for gating and audits. Proxy maps are not used as the primary detector scores for labeled ROC reporting. When KA is enabled, the exported maps enforce a shrink-only mapping on the chosen detector score: in this right-tail score space, $S_{\mathrm{post}}=w\,S_{\mathrm{pre}}$ with $w\in(0,1]$; for convenience, bundles also store the equivalent per-pixel scale map $\gamma=1/w\ge 1$ applied on the penalized region.

Brain-Pial128 uses 4 ensembles with 32 pulses each ($T=128$ compounded frames) and concentrates shallow alias in depth $[0.15,0.30]$ with a stronger alias fraction and a vibration tone. Brain-AliasContract and Brain-SkullOR use additional alias/vibration settings and skull/aberration tagging; all simulation parameters are fixed per profile and are specified in the reproducibility manifest (\SuppOrApp{app:repro}).

\subsection{Replay/analysis pipeline used for ROC and latency}

  \item Reuses a fixed pilot per (regime, PRF) and replays baseline+STAP under a frozen profile on 64-frame windows (\texttt{--time-window-length 64}).

\paragraph{Latency reporting protocol (steady-state, CUDA graphs).}
When GPU acceleration is enabled (e.g.\ \path{stap_device=cuda}), the first replay window can include one-time overheads such as CUDA context initialization, kernel compilation, and optional CUDA-graph capture. For any GPU latency numbers reported from this codebase, we therefore use a steady-state replay protocol: \path{scripts/latency_rerun_check.py} replays $N$ windows in a single process (\texttt{--steady-windows $N$} or \texttt{--window-offsets}) and reports per-window timing, with the publishable latency taken as the mean over windows 2..$N$ (window 1 treated as cold-start overhead).

\section{Implementation details (supplementary)}
\label{app:impl-details}

\subsection{RPCA baseline (full)}
The RPCA baseline used in this repository is implemented as \path{sim/kwave/common.py:_baseline_pd_rpca}. It performs a windowed Principal Component Pursuit (PCP) using an inexact augmented Lagrangian method (IALM) on a downsampled Casorati matrix:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Spatial downsampling: complex average pooling by factor 2 when $\max(H,W)>128$.
  \item Temporal windows: default subwindow length $t_{\mathrm{sub}}=80$ (or shorter if $T<80$); windows are processed sequentially.
  \item Optimization: IALM with at most 80 inner iterations per window (\path{max_iters_inner=min(max_iters,80)}), tolerance $10^{-4}$.
  \item Shrinkage: $\lambda = 1/\sqrt{\max(m,n)}$ when not supplied, where $m=T_w$ and $n=H_dW_d$.
  \item Low-rank step: singular value thresholding using either full SVD or a randomized SVD depending on matrix size, with a per-window rank heuristic $k=\min(8,\max(2,\lfloor T_w/10\rfloor))$.
  \item Output PD: sum of sparse-component energy over windows on the downsampled grid, then nearest-neighbor upsampling to the original resolution.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{HOSVD baseline (full)}
The tensor HOSVD baseline is implemented as \path{sim/kwave/common.py:_baseline_pd_hosvd}. The cube $X\in\mathbb{C}^{T\times H\times W}$ is optionally spatially downsampled (complex average pooling). Mode-0/1/2 SVDs are computed on the unfoldings and ranks are chosen either explicitly (\texttt{--hosvd-ranks}) or by energy fractions (\texttt{--hosvd-energy-fracs}, default $(0.99,0.99,0.99)$). The low-rank approximation is reconstructed by projecting onto the multilinear subspace, and PD is the time-averaged residual energy $\frac{1}{T}\sum_t |X(t)-L(t)|^2$. In the brain baselines used in this repository, the Makefile target \texttt{r4c-replay-hosvd} runs HOSVD with \texttt{--hosvd-spatial-downsample 2} and \texttt{--hosvd-energy-fracs 0.99,0.99,0.99}.

\subsection{Adaptive SVD baseline (spatial singular-vector similarity cutoff)}
\label{app:baseline-adaptive-svd}
To preempt the common critique that global SVD baselines are sensitive to an arbitrary fixed rank/energy threshold, we include an adaptive spatiotemporal SVD baseline inspired by Baranger \emph{et al.}\ \cite{BarangerAdaptiveSVD2018}. For a windowed slow-time cube $X\in\mathbb{C}^{T\times H\times W}$ we form the Casorati matrix $C\in\mathbb{C}^{T\times N}$ with $N=HW$ and compute its SVD $C=U\Sigma V\Hh$. Let $v_k\in\mathbb{C}^{N}$ denote the $k$th \emph{spatial} singular vector (column of $V$). Because singular vectors are defined up to an arbitrary complex phase, we measure spatial coherence on magnitudes and define
\[
  s_k := \frac{\langle |v_k|,\ |v_{k+1}|\rangle}{\| |v_k|\|\ \| |v_{k+1}|\|},\qquad k=1,\dots,T-1.
\]
We smooth $\{s_k\}$ by a short median filter and choose the clutter rank $r$ as the first index where $s_k$ falls below a robust threshold $\tau=\mathrm{median}(s)-\kappa\cdot \mathrm{MAD}(s)$, with $r$ clamped to a fixed range $[r_{\min},r_{\max}]$ (all hyperparameters frozen per regime). For the Brain-* pilots ($T=64$) we use median-filter width 7, $\kappa=2.5$, and clamp $[r_{\min},r_{\max}]=[1,32]$. The filtered residual is
\[
  C_{\mathrm{res}} := \bigl(I-U_rU_r\Hh\bigr)\,C,
\]
and the baseline score is the same PD flow-evidence statistic used elsewhere, $S_{\mathrm{base}}(y,x)=\frac{1}{T}\sum_t |X_{\mathrm{res}}(t,y,x)|^2$ after reshaping $C_{\mathrm{res}}$ back to cube form. This baseline is implemented as \path{sim/kwave/common.py:_baseline_pd_svd_similarity}.

\subsection{Block-wise local SVD baseline (overlap-add reconstruction)}
\label{app:baseline-local-svd}
To address reviewer objections that \emph{local} adaptive SVD clutter suppression is already standard in small-vessel ultrasound imaging, we also include a block-wise local SVD clutter filter in the style of Song \emph{et al.}\ \cite{SongBlockwiseLocal2017}. We tile the spatial grid into overlapping blocks $\{\Omega_i\}$ (block size and stride frozen per regime) and form local Casorati matrices $C_i\in\mathbb{C}^{T\times (h w)}$ by stacking slow-time vectors within each block. For each tile, we compute $C_i=U_i\Sigma_i V_i\Hh$ and select a tile-local clutter rank $r_i$ using a deterministic, frozen energy-fraction rule:
\[
  r_i := \min\left\{r:\frac{\sum_{k=1}^r \sigma_{i,k}^2}{\sum_{k=1}^{T}\sigma_{i,k}^2}\ge e\right\},
\]
where $e\in(0,1)$ is fixed. For the Brain-* pilots we use blocks $8\times 8$, stride 3, $e=0.90$, and Hann overlap-add weighting. The local residual is $C_{i,\mathrm{res}}=(I-U_{i,r_i}U_{i,r_i}\Hh)C_i$, reshaped back to a block cube. We reconstruct a full-resolution residual cube by overlap-add with a separable Hann weight inside each block and compute the baseline score as the PD statistic on the reconstructed residual. This baseline is implemented as \path{sim/kwave/common.py:_baseline_pd_local_svd}.

\subsection{Adaptive-local SVD baseline (tilewise similarity-drop rank selection)}
\label{app:baseline-adaptive-local-svd}
For the stricter SIMUS fairness study we additionally include an adaptive-local SVD baseline that combines the two literature ideas above rather than treating them as mutually exclusive. As in the Song-style local baseline, we tile the spatial grid into overlapping blocks $\{\Omega_i\}$, form local Casorati matrices $C_i\in\mathbb{C}^{T\times(hw)}$, and reconstruct by overlap-add with optional Hann weighting \cite{SongBlockwiseLocal2017}. However, instead of a fixed tilewise energy threshold, each tile chooses its clutter rank from the same Baranger-style spatial singular-vector similarity-drop heuristic used by the adaptive-global baseline \cite{BarangerAdaptiveSVD2018}. Concretely, for each tile we compute $C_i=U_i\Sigma_iV_i\Hh$, form tile-local spatial singular-vector similarities
\[
  s_{i,k} := \frac{\langle |v_{i,k}|,\ |v_{i,k+1}|\rangle}{\| |v_{i,k}|\|\ \| |v_{i,k+1}|\|},
\]
smooth $\{s_{i,k}\}$ with the same short median filter used in the global adaptive baseline, and choose
\[
  r_i := \min \left\{k : s_{i,k} < \mathrm{median}(s_i) - \kappa\cdot \mathrm{MAD}(s_i)\right\},
\]
with $r_i$ clamped to a frozen range $[r_{\min}, r_{\max}]$. The local residual remains $C_{i,\mathrm{res}}=(I-U_{i,r_i}U_{i,r_i}\Hh)C_i$, followed by overlap-add reconstruction and PD scoring on the residual cube. This baseline is implemented as \path{sim/kwave/common.py:_baseline_pd_adaptive_local_svd}. In the benchmark tables we report the three SVD-family baselines separately as \emph{Adaptive Global SVD}, \emph{Local SVD (Fixed Energy)}, and \emph{Adaptive Local SVD}.

\subsection{STAP temporal core (full)}
For k-Wave pilots and replay bundles, the reference temporal STAP implementation follows the same conceptual decomposition as the main text: tile extraction and optional sliding-window temporal embedding (Hankel construction) in slow time; per-tile covariance estimation with a robust estimator (Tyler-type or Huber); diagonal loading to enforce conditioning targets; whitening; and band-energy scoring on a fixed Doppler tone grid. Deployment-oriented variants can (i) cap effective training support by subsampling training snapshots and (ii) reduce computation by skipping the STAP stage on tiles with negligible proxy flow support, reverting to the baseline score there. Exact run settings and exported metadata used for the experiments in this manuscript are listed in the reproducibility manifest (\SuppOrApp{app:repro}).

\paragraph{CUDA acceleration strategy (batched GPU implementation).}
For the latency results reported here we implement the STAP PD stage as a GPU-batched pipeline: tiles are extracted on device using tensor \emph{unfold} operators, processed in batches to amortize per-tile overhead, and stitched back to full-resolution maps by overlap-add. This hardware-aware formulation aligns with prior work on GPU eigen-based clutter filtering and randomized/approximate SVD acceleration for real-time Doppler processing \cite{CheeGPUEigen2017,LokRandomizedSVD2020}. Within each batch, the temporal core is expressed as batched linear algebra (sliding-window temporal embedding; Hankel matrix construction; robust covariance estimation; whitening; and band-energy scoring), with rearrangements that favor GEMM-dominated workloads on GPUs. For fixed input shapes, the batched core can optionally be captured as a CUDA graph and replayed to reduce kernel-launch overhead; the first processed window/frame therefore includes one-time GPU overheads and is excluded from reported steady-state means. Exact commands and runtime settings used for the CUDA latency tables are listed in the reproducibility manifest (\SuppOrApp{app:repro}).

\subsection{Covariance blending constraints and KA modes}

We permit anisotropic shrinkage, but only \emph{within} bands. In practice we consider two KA paths:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Updated (commuting band--scalar).} $Q\Hh Q$ is forced to be bandwise scalar with target gains $(s_f,s_a,s_o)$ (typical targets: $s_f=s_o\approx 1$, $s_a\approx 1.5$), small mixing, and no off--block priors; the content of $R_0$ beyond $(\Pf,\Pa)$ is ignored.
  \item \textbf{Blend.} A trace--normalized blend $R_\beta = (1-\beta) R_t^{(n)} + \beta R_0^{(n)}$ with a small diagonal lift. In practice $R_0$ is overridden to a band--scalar form (Pf weight 1, Pa weight $\gamma_a>1$, Po weight 1) so the geometry is explicit; $\beta$ is treated as a fixed hyperparameter per regime.
\end{itemize}
Both modes enforce bandwise structure; there are no off--block priors.

\subsection{CB--1: Band--Block Blending}

We construct
\[
R_\beta := (1-\beta)\,\wh R + \beta \bigl(R_{0,f}\oplus R_{0,a}\oplus R_{0,o}\bigr) + \delta I,
\]
where $R_{0,f} = \Pf R_0 \Pf$, etc., for some prior $R_0$. There are no off--block priors; $\beta\in[0,1)$ and $\delta\ge 0$.

Interpretation: this structure makes $Q$ nearly block--diagonal in $(\Po,\Pf,\Pg,\Pa)$ while still allowing bandwise control to realize OC--2.

\subsection{CB--2: Bandwise Spectral Targets}

We choose $(\beta,\delta)$ to enforce OC--2 explicitly:
\[
\lambda_{\min}(Q\Hh Q|_{\Pf}) \in [s_f^2(1-\delta_f), s_f^2(1+\delta_f)],\quad\text{etc.}
\]
with design tolerances $\delta_\bullet\le 0.1$ and separation $\Lambda = s_a/s_f \ge 1.25$.

\subsection{CB--3: Bandwise Condition Number Safeguard}

We constrain bandwise conditioning:
\[
\kappa(R_\beta|_{\Pf}) \le \kappa_f^{\max},\qquad
\kappa(R_\beta|_{\Pa}) \le \kappa_a^{\max},
\]
avoid an incompatibility between conditioning and preserving flow Rayleigh quotients.

\subsection{Localization (restricting KA in score space)}
\label{app:gating}

A global band--constant transform with $Q$ cannot improve ROC if applied everywhere (it is close to a scalar reweighting of BR/GLRT). To create re--ranking, KA must be \emph{localized} to tiles where:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item scores are dominated by alias/undesired components,
  \item positives are unlikely to lie.
\end{itemize}

We define localization based on a combination of alias metrics, flow coverage, depth, PD, and registration. This supplements G--1/G--2 with a generalized alias score $m_{\mathrm{alias}}$ from Section~\ref{sec:regime}.

\subsection{G--1: Alias--dominant tile selection}

Given a baseline tile $z$, compute per--band energies $(E_o,E_f,E_g,E_a)$ and a generalized alias score $m_{\mathrm{alias}}(z)$ satisfying R--2.

\paragraph{Data--adaptive threshold.}
For BG tiles (or their proxy), collect $m_{\mathrm{alias}}(z)$ and define
\[
\tau_{\mathrm{alias}} := Q_{q_{\mathrm{alias}}}\bigl(m_{\mathrm{alias}} \mid H_0\bigr),
\]
where $Q_q$ is the $q$--quantile and $q_{\mathrm{alias}}\in[0.7,0.9]$ is a design choice (typical: $0.8$ or $0.9$).

Define an alias gate
\[
g_{\mathrm{alias}}(z) = \mathbf{1}\left\{ m_{\mathrm{alias}}(z) \ge \tau_{\mathrm{alias}} \right\}.
\]

\paragraph{Band--edge purity.}
Additionally require that guard energy is small relative to Pf+Pa:
\[
\frac{E_g}{E_o + E_f + E_a + E_g} \le \eta_{\text{edge}},\quad
\eta_{\text{edge}}\le 0.1.
\]

Interpretation: this gate selects the top tail of alias--heavy tiles among negatives, while protecting against band--edge contamination. The distribution of $m_{\mathrm{alias}}$ across classes is governed by R--2; empirical validation (Section~\ref{sec:regime}) must show a nontrivial difference between $H_0$ and $H_1$.

\subsection{G--2: Flow coherence protection}

We can optionally compute a flow coherence metric $\mathrm{FPC}$ (first principal component fraction) within the flow band:
\[
\mathrm{FPC} := \frac{\lambda_1(\wh S_f)}{\tr(\wh S_f)},\qquad
\wh S_f := \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K u_k u_k\Hh,\quad
u_k := \Pf z^{(k)},
\]
where $z^{(k)}$ are projected slow--time vectors across DPSS tapers or within a local ensemble.

Gate \emph{may} require
\[
\mathrm{FPC}\le c_{\text{pc}},\qquad c_{\text{pc}}\in[0.4,0.6].
\]

Interpretation: flow tiles tend to exhibit higher coherence in Pf; when enabled, we suppress penalization when $\mathrm{FPC}$ is large, thereby reducing positive penalization probability $p_+$. Alias/motion tiles have lower flow coherence and pass this condition. In the current runs this check is typically left off; coverage and depth localization carry most of the class separation.

\subsection{G--2': Flow coverage protection}

Let $c_f$ be the tile--wise flow coverage fraction:
\[
c_f := \frac{\#\{\text{pixels labeled as flow}\}}{\#\{\text{pixels in tile}\}},
\]
using a label--free proxy such as a PD--derived flow mask.

We require
\[
c_f \le c_{f,\max},\qquad c_{f,\max}\in[0.1,0.2],
\]
and optionally $c_f \ge c_{f,\min}$ to avoid pure background noise tiles (typical $c_{f,\min}\approx 0$).

Interpretation: we prefer penalization on tiles with \emph{low to moderate} flow coverage. This is the regime where alias/motion dominates and where down--scaling scores is most beneficial. Dense flow tiles are either left to STAP alone or are protected via the flow coherence check. (In the current implementation, coverage is treated as an \emph{upper} bound; missing coverage defaults to ``pass'' in this test.)

\subsection{G--3: Baseline score quantile window}

Let $S_{\text{base}}(z)$ be the baseline BR/GLRT score. Within a given depth stratum, compute score quantiles $q_{0.3},q_{0.95}$ over BG tiles. Gate requires
\[
S_{\text{base}}(z)\in [q_{0.3},q_{0.95}].
\]

Interpretation: avoid penalization on the noise floor (very low scores) and on already high--confidence positives (very high scores). This focuses KA on the part of the negative tail that matters near the target FPR.

\subsection{G--4: Registration quality (optional)}

If motion compensation / registration is used, include a registration quality metric (e.g.\ PSR) and require
\[
\mathrm{PSR}_{\text{reg}}\le \text{PSR}_{\max},
\]
or any similar condition ensuring that tiles with gross registration failure are handled separately.

\subsection{Offline validation of gating}

On a held--out labeled configuration, define
\[
p_+ := \mathbb{P}\{g=1\mid H_1\},\qquad
p_- := \mathbb{P}\{g=1\mid H_0\},
\]
for the combined gate $g$ that incorporates G--1, G--2, G--2', G--3, G--4. Require:
\[
p_-\ge 0.2,\quad p_- \le 0.5,\quad p_+ \le 0.1 p_-.
\]

Interpretation: a nontrivial fraction of negatives are gated (but not too many), while positives are rarely gated. This condition is central to the SL--2 quantile shift argument.

\subsection{Summary: Compact Feasibility Checklist (Updated)}

KA--STAP can be expected to outperform MC--SVD (or STAP--only) in the low-FPR regime if and only if:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.,nosep]
  \item \textbf{Regime preconditions.} Band occupancy R--1, alias separation R--2, and baseline headroom R--3 are satisfied on a held--out validation set.
  \item \textbf{Band design.} BG--1/BG--2/BG--3 hold for the chosen PRF/$T$/DPSS; Pf/Pa are physically meaningful (e.g.\ Pf $\approx$ 30--250 Hz, Pa $\gtrsim$ 400 Hz in brain fUS).
  \item \textbf{Operator geometry.} OC--1 with $\varepsilon_{\text{mix}}\le 0.05$ and OC--2 with $\Lambda = s_a/s_f \ge 1.25$ and $\delta_\bullet\le 0.1$.
  \item \textbf{Blending realizable.} CB--1/CB--2/CB--3 satisfied with chosen $(\beta,\delta)$.
  \item \textbf{Gating conservative and class--differential.} G--1/G--2/G--2'/G--3/G--4 implemented; offline, $p_-\in[0.2,0.5]$ and $p_+\le 0.1p_-$.
  \item \textbf{Regime sentinel.} RS--1 does not trigger (KA not out of regime).
  \item \textbf{Invariance sentinel.} $\wh s_a/\wh s_f \notin[1-\epsilon,1+\epsilon]$ with $\epsilon\le 0.02$; otherwise KA is effectively scalar and can be skipped.
  \item \textbf{Safety guard.} Safety--1 (monotonicity on gated flow tiles) passes.
  \item \textbf{Evaluation discipline.} GLRT controls and tail CI practices (see \ifSTAPSUPPLEMENTONLY the main paper\else Section~\ref{sec:eval}\fi) in place for fair low--FPR comparison.
\end{enumerate}

When all of the above hold, KA applies a non--mixing, alias--inflating band transform chiefly to alias--dominant tiles, pushing negative scores down while largely leaving positives unchanged. This lowers the negative $\alpha$--quantile and increases TPR at fixed small FPR in a manner consistent with the SL--2 quantile shift analysis.
