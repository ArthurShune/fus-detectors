\begin{abstract}
Functional ultrasound is now used for intraoperative, neonatal, and mobile brain imaging, but even after standard clutter suppression, tissue motion, phase instability, and aliased flow can produce false detections that distort vascular maps and contaminate functional readouts. We study a family of localized matched-subspace detector heads applied after conventional clutter suppression of beamformed fUS slow-time data, ranging from a fixed band-limited score to locally whitened variants. Quantitative evidence comes from labeled brain simulations, a structurally labeled flow phantom, a held-out SIMUS moving-scatterer benchmark, and limited real-IQ audits. On the held-out synthetic structural benchmark, the fixed head reduces nuisance FPR from 0.998 to 0.004 at matched recall 0.5. On one open real-IQ rat-brain dataset, only the fully whitened variant shows a modest structural gain, improving AUC from 0.504 for power Doppler to 0.530 on a same-residual localization-derived vessel-core versus shell audit. The dominant mechanism depends on setting: band selectivity on labeled simulations and the held-out benchmark, local whitening on short-ensemble phantom and real-IQ data. These results show that changing only the downstream score head, without modifying the upstream clutter filter, can materially improve map trustworthiness, while real-data functional task-activation validation remains future work.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Functional ultrasound (fUS) is now used for vascular and functional mapping in settings ranging from preclinical neuroscience to awake surgery and cranial-window human imaging. In these settings, the practical problem is often not average sensitivity alone, but that even a few nuisance-driven false detections can distort maps and undermine interpretation. Tissue motion, phase instability, and aliased high-velocity flow therefore matter most when they survive ordinary clutter suppression and populate the extreme right tail of the detector output.

In intra-operative settings, a false-positive activation region can mislead surgical planning. In resting-state connectivity studies, a single artifactual hot spot that survives across blocks can create a spurious seed correlation. In neonatal bedside monitoring, where acquisition windows are short and motion is unavoidable, nuisance leakage in the score map can be hard to distinguish from genuine hemodynamic variation without independent confirmation that is rarely available. These are not average-sensitivity problems; they are rare-event problems driven by the right tail of the score distribution.

We address that problem with a localized matched-subspace detector framework for post-residual fUS. After ordinary clutter suppression, the method asks a local question: within a small neighborhood, is the remaining slow-time signal concentrated in a prespecified flow band, or is it more consistent with structured nuisance? The contribution is a detector-head family: the fixed matched-subspace head is the core and default method, the adaptive variant selectively invokes whitening, and the fully whitened variant applies whitening throughout. Among the downstream heads tested on the held-out SIMUS-Struct benchmark, a band-limited matched-subspace score without local whitening is the strongest fixed choice. That mechanism is not universal: in the short-ensemble Gammex phantom, whitening-dominant scoring is stronger than the matched-subspace ratio on the same residual. Figure~\ref{fig:ulm_pala_headline_hero} previews the clearest real-data result, and the same-residual table below shows the setting dependence directly.

\paragraph{Terminology.}
Throughout the paper, we refer to the three detector modes as the fixed matched-subspace detector, the adaptive detector, and the fully whitened detector. We use the full descriptive terms local slow-time whitening and empirical tail calibration rather than shorter signal-processing abbreviations.

The effect is not confined to the extreme tail. On the representative Gammex frame in Figure~\ref{fig:leading_structural_fidelity}, matching the background FPR at $10^{-2}$ yields zero lumen recovery for the baseline score, whereas both detector-family score maps recover lumen-consistent structure at the same matched background allowance, with the whitened-power map showing the cleaner phantom response. The later Gammex structural ROC tables confirm that whitened power is the stronger phantom score overall in this short-ensemble setting. We use the stricter $10^{-3}$--$10^{-4}$ operating points mainly as a stress test that exposes nuisance sensitivity cleanly, not because the practical relevance begins only there.

This problem matters now because fUS is no longer only a preclinical proof-of-concept modality. It is already used in intra-operative, neonatal, cranial-window, and emerging mobile settings \cite{Imbault2017,DemeneHumanNewborn2017,BarangerNeonates2021,Rabut2024,MobileFUS2025,Deffieux2021}, where rare nuisance-driven false positives can matter more than average image quality.

The focus is downstream detector-head design after fixed residualization. The fixed non-whitened matched-subspace head is the default on the held-out synthetic structural benchmark, and the fully whitened variant helps in selected clutter-heavy or structure-concentration settings. The validation ladder in Table~\ref{tab:validation_ladder_intro} summarizes the evidence order before the notation and benchmark detail.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item A post-residual localized matched-subspace detector family for beamformed fUS slow-time data, centered on a fixed band-limited head and extended by an adaptive whitening switch plus a fully whitened variant.
  \item Held-out evidence that the fixed non-whitened matched-subspace score is the best-performing fixed head among the evaluated downstream heads on the held-out SIMUS-Struct benchmark, together with clutter-heavy stress tests showing where the adaptive and fully whitened extensions help in selected settings.
  \item Same-residual and same-cube comparisons that separate detector-head effects from residualization, using PD/Kasai, whitened power, and the unwhitened ratio as direct comparators.
  \item Limited transfer and replay-feasibility analyses, including fixed-threshold transfer on held-out synthetic and real-IQ audits and workstation replay latency.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Scope of empirical claims.}
The evidence is stratified by data setting. Quantitative efficacy results are reported where independent $H_1/H_0$ labels or an external structural reference are available. The detector family also has a fixed hierarchy throughout the paper: the fixed matched-subspace head is the default method, the adaptive variant selectively invokes whitening, and the fully whitened variant applies whitening throughout. After first definition, we use the short labels kWave-Stress, Gammex-Struct, SIMUS-Struct-Mobile, SIMUS-Struct-Intraop, ULM-CoreShell, ULM-Motion, and Shin-ProxyMotion; SIMUS-Struct denotes the paired mobile and intra-operative structural benchmark.

\section{Background and Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:background-problem}

\paragraph{Problem setup.}
For each spatial location (or tile-aggregated snapshot), let $x\in\mathbb{C}^{L_t}$ denote a slow-time vector. We consider
\[
H_0:\ x=c+n,\qquad H_1:\ x=s+c+n,
\]
where $s$ is flow-consistent signal, $c$ is structured clutter/motion, and $n$ is residual noise. In practice, $H_0$ is spatially nonstationary and heavy-tailed, motivating local covariance adaptation and tail-focused evaluation.

\paragraph{Core notation and bands.}
We use band operators $(\Po,\Pf,\Pg,\Pa)$ for clutter/DC, flow, guard, and alias regions, respectively. For short ensembles, these are exact orthogonal DFT-bin projectors; for longer apertures they are leakage-controlled analysis operators and are treated as band-energy statistics with explicit sanity sentinels (\SuppOrApp{app:alg-details}).

\subsection{Related context}
\ifPAPERONLY
Spatiotemporal SVD, RPCA, HOSVD, and local adaptive clutter filtering are established in ultrafast Doppler and fUS, and learned residualizers are relevant prior work on the residual-formation side of the pipeline \cite{DemeneClutterSVD2015,BarangerAdaptiveSVD2018,CandesRPCA,SolomonDeepUnfoldedRPCA2020,HuangAdaptiveHOSVD2024}. These methods improve the residual itself; none explicitly evaluates the downstream detection statistic as a separable design choice on the same residual. The question here is narrower: once a residual slow-time cube has been fixed, can the downstream score head reduce nuisance leakage more effectively than PD- or Kasai-style readouts? That separates this paper from Yu-, Baranger-, and Song-style local clutter filtering, which adapts the clutter filter itself before a conventional Doppler score is applied \cite{YuLovstakkenEigenReview2010,SongBlockwiseLocal2017,CheeGPUEigen2017}. ULM motion correction, twinkling-phantom structure audits, and hemodynamic fUS readouts therefore enter here as evaluation context and downstream audits rather than as the primary algorithmic contribution; the fuller literature context is kept in the methods companion.
\else
\textbf{Ultrasound clutter suppression.} Spatiotemporal SVD clutter filters are a standard tool in ultrafast Doppler and fUS \cite{DemeneClutterSVD2015,BarangerAdaptiveSVD2018,Mace2013,Rabut2020,ICDFUS2021}. Real-time feasibility has been studied via randomized/approximate decompositions and GPU implementations \cite{LokRandomizedSVD2020,CheeGPUEigen2017}. Robust PCA and tensor/HOSVD variants have also been studied for clutter suppression and motion robustness \cite{CandesRPCA,SolomonDeepUnfoldedRPCA2020,HuangAdaptiveHOSVD2024}. These methods improve the residual itself; none explicitly evaluates the downstream detection statistic as a separable design choice on the same residual. We benchmark against representative ultrasound-native clutter-filter families, including adaptive-global SVD, local block-wise SVD, adaptive-local SVD, RPCA, HOSVD, and conventional Doppler score heads.

\textbf{Learned residualizers and deep-unfolded baselines.} Learned clutter filters and deep-unfolded RPCA variants are relevant prior work \cite{SolomonDeepUnfoldedRPCA2020}, but they operate on a different axis of the pipeline from the one studied here. Our question is whether the downstream score head can improve nuisance control once a residual slow-time cube has already been fixed. A fair learned baseline study for that question would require setting-specific training data, supervision, and retraining protocols for the exact post-beamformed slow-time detection task studied here, and no public trained model exists for that task across the labeled simulation, phantom, and real-IQ settings used in this paper. We therefore treat learned residualization as related prior work rather than as a main comparator family in this detector-head study.

\textbf{What is new relative to Yu, Baranger, and Song.} Eigen-based clutter filter design for ultrasound color flow imaging and block-wise/local adaptive clutter filters are closely related ultrasound-native approaches to covariance/eigenspace adaptation \cite{YuLovstakkenEigenReview2010,SongBlockwiseLocal2017,CheeGPUEigen2017}. Song \emph{et al.}\ demonstrate local clutter filtering in \emph{in vivo} settings (e.g., kidney imaging), while Baranger \emph{et al.}\ provide an adaptive singular-vector-similarity rank rule for ultrafast Doppler \cite{BarangerAdaptiveSVD2018}. Our benchmark set includes both literature families directly and a tile-local hybrid that combines Song-style overlap-add localization with a Baranger-style adaptive rank rule. Many open fUS releases do not preserve raw high-PRF complex IQ suitable for labeled Doppler-side detector evaluation, so we use labeled k-Wave scenarios and structurally labeled phantoms for quantitative ROC claims and use \emph{in vivo} data for robustness and translation audits. Our contribution differs in where the adaptation enters and in the discipline used to test it: Yu-, Baranger-, and Song-style methods adapt the \emph{clutter filter} itself and then typically read out power Doppler or a closely related conventional score, whereas we keep the residual cube fixed and change the \emph{detector head}. Specifically, we evaluate a small family of downstream heads on identical residuals, including deliberately simple ablations such as whitened total power without Doppler band partition and a fixed band-limited ratio without whitening. This lets us test whether the gain comes from matched-subspace geometry, whitening alone, or their combination. The result is a downstream detector-head framework for post-residual fUS, together with a strict-tail evaluation discipline, rather than another local SVD clutter filter.

\textbf{Covariance-aware detection precedent.} Classical covariance-aware beamforming and detection work provide the broad precedent for handling structured interference \cite{Capon1969Beamformer,FrostBeamformer,WardSTAPTutorial,GuerciSTAPBook2003,WicksKASTAP2006,MelvinGuerciKAParadigm2006,GurramGoodmanKASTAP2006}. Here that connection is limited: local slow-time whitening appears only as an optional post-residual variant, and side information enters only through a shrink-only regularizer.

\textbf{ULM pipelines and motion correction.} Ultrafast ultrasound localization microscopy (ULM) achieves deep super-resolution vascular imaging by localizing sparse microbubble echoes over high-PRF ensembles \cite{ErricoULM2015}. Robust clutter suppression and motion correction are central to ULM pipelines, including subwavelength motion correction tailored to ultrafast acquisitions \cite{HingotULMMotion2017}. Here we use a public kHz IQ ULM benchmark dataset to report label-free stability and motion robustness of Doppler-side score maps under controlled motion corruption, illustrating how a tail-stable detector can complement upstream motion correction and downstream localization stages.

\textbf{Twinkling artifact and Doppler phantoms.} The Doppler ``twinkling artifact'' has been documented as a persistent color-flow phenomenon on calcifications and other strong reflectors, and is widely used as a diagnostic benchmark for Doppler processing chains. Leonov \emph{et al.}\ provide an open-access database of raw beamformer signals with phantoms including a flow-through Gammex phantom and calculi/metal/plastic objects \cite{LeonovTwinklingDD2021}. We use these data to (i) obtain a structurally labeled phantom ROC via B-mode-only tube segmentation and (ii) construct an artifact-heavy regularizer-hygiene setting where the optional penalty layer is expected to be conservative and auditable.

\textbf{fUS detection and hemodynamic analysis.} GLM-based detection and atlas-driven ROI analysis are common in hemodynamic fUS \cite{Demeneglmband2016,UrbanWholeBrain2015}. Here we use those analyses as downstream readouts and translation audits; a dedicated hemodynamic scoring variant is left for future work.
\fi

\subsection{Baseline clutter suppression and score conventions}
\paragraph{Beamformed slow-time data.}
Let $X[t,y,x]\in\mathbb{C}$ denote a beamformed slow-time IQ movie over $t=0,\dots,T-1$ and spatial indices $(y,x)$. We first apply a baseline clutter-suppression operator (e.g.\ motion-compensated SVD), producing a residual cube $X_{\mathrm{base}}[t,y,x]$ used for both baseline scoring and as the input to the detector family.

\paragraph{Power Doppler proxy and baseline scores.}
From the baseline-filtered cube we define the baseline ROC score map
\begin{equation}
  S_{\mathrm{base}}(y,x):=\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\left|X_{\mathrm{base}}(t,y,x)\right|^2.
  \label{eq:sbase}
\end{equation}
This is ordinary power Doppler on $X_{\mathrm{base}}$. For short-ensemble settings we additionally report auxiliary conventional comparators such as log-power Doppler and Kasai lag-1 magnitude \cite{KasaiAutocorrelation1985} so that baseline-score idiosyncrasies do not dominate the comparison.

\noindent When the same power statistic is used only as a display or proxy map for deterministic preprocessing, we write
\[
  \mathrm{PD}^{\uparrow}(y,x)\equiv S_{\mathrm{base}}(y,x),
\]
to make it explicit that the quantity is being used for visualization or mask construction rather than as the ROC score itself.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
\label{sec:stap-architecture}
\label{sec:ka-contract-v2} % Backward-compatible label used throughout.

\paragraph{What changes relative to SVD + PD.}
Relative to a conventional ultrafast Doppler pipeline, the proposed detector family changes the final scoring stage rather than replacing upstream clutter suppression. After forming a residual slow-time signal, we score its energy inside a prespecified flow subspace rather than using only aggregate Doppler power. The simplest fixed head uses that score without local whitening, a prespecified adaptive variant promotes clutter-heavy pixels onto a whitened branch only when guard-band evidence warrants it, and a fully whitened variant is retained for settings where stronger local adaptation is beneficial. The goal is not simply to increase vessel conspicuity, but to reduce nuisance-driven high scores in operating regions where false positives matter.

\paragraph{Plain-language interpretation.}
The fixed head is a local band-limited flow detector applied after ordinary residualization. The adaptive branch adds tile-local whitening only where guard-band energy indicates localized clutter.

\paragraph{Pipeline overview.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=1.6em,label=\arabic*.]
  \item Baseline clutter suppression (e.g.\ MC--SVD) produces a residual cube $X_{\mathrm{base}}$ and baseline score $S_{\mathrm{base}}(y,x)$.
  \item \ifPAPERONLY For each tile $\Omega_i$, form the local slow-time support used by the chosen detector head; when whitening is active, apply the fixed setting-specific local covariance transform $y_{i,k}=R_i^{-1/2}x_{i,k}$. \else For each tile $\Omega_i$: form slow-time snapshots $\{x_{i,k}\}_{k=1}^K$ via spatial sampling and, optionally, a sliding-window temporal embedding (Hankel matrix construction), estimate conditioned covariance $R_i$, and either keep the raw snapshots or whiten them as $y_{i,k}=R_i^{-1/2}x_{i,k}$. \fi
  \item Compute the self-normalized matched-subspace score on the chosen tile representation and aggregate over overlapping tiles to obtain the detector score map. The fixed and fully whitened forms are given explicitly in Eqs.~\eqref{eq:s0} and \eqref{eq:sw}; setting $R_i=I$ recovers the simpler band-limited fixed head.
  \item If the adaptive variant is used, retain the fixed head by default and promote pixels onto the whitened branch only where a prespecified guard-band clutter feature exceeds a fixed threshold.
\end{enumerate}
\noindent In practical terms, the method asks a local question after ordinary clutter suppression: within a small spatial neighborhood, does the remaining slow-time activity look more like microvascular flow or more like locally structured nuisance? Detector-core ablations on labeled brain simulations and the Gammex phantom are reported in the main Results.

The hierarchy is simple: fixed is the default method, adaptive selectively invokes whitening, and fully whitened applies whitening throughout. The derivations below formalize those three detector modes.

\paragraph{Three detector variants.}
On tile $i$, let $x_{i,k}$ denote the unwhitened slow-time snapshots and $y_{i,k}=R_i^{-1/2}x_{i,k}$ the whitened snapshots when whitening is active. The fixed detector is
\begin{equation}
  S_{0}(i):=\frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K
  \frac{x_{i,k}\Hh \Pf\, x_{i,k}}{x_{i,k}\Hh (I-\Pf)\, x_{i,k}+\rho\, x_{i,k}\Hh x_{i,k}+\epsilon},
  \label{eq:s0}
\end{equation}
which is the same band-limited matched-subspace ratio with $R_i=I$. The fully whitened detector is
\begin{equation}
  S_{w}(i):=\frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K
  \frac{y_{i,k}\Hh \Pf\, y_{i,k}}{y_{i,k}\Hh (I-\Pf)\, y_{i,k}+\rho\, y_{i,k}\Hh y_{i,k}+\epsilon}.
  \label{eq:sw}
\end{equation}
The detector family has three concrete variants. The fixed detector uses $S_{0}(i)$ everywhere. The prespecified adaptive detector uses
\begin{equation}
  S(i)=
  \begin{cases}
    S_{w}(i), & r^{\mathrm{base}}_{g}(i)\ge \tau_g,\\
    S_{0}(i), & r^{\mathrm{base}}_{g}(i)<\tau_g,
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:sadapt}
\end{equation}
where $r^{\mathrm{base}}_{g}(i)$ is a baseline guard-band energy fraction and $\tau_g$ is fixed once and then held fixed across all reported experiments. The fully whitened detector uses $S_{w}(i)$ everywhere. In words, the adaptive detector keeps the simpler score in clean tiles and only turns on full whitening where the residual itself shows guard-band evidence of localized clutter.

\ifPAPERONLY
\paragraph{Tile support and switching rule.}
All reported detectors operate on overlapping local tiles. In the main quantitative settings, the spatial support is fixed at $8\times 8$ pixels, with slow-time aperture $L_t=8$ for kWave-Stress, $L_t=16$ for Gammex-Struct, and $L_t=64$ for SIMUS-Struct and ULM-CoreShell. The adaptive rule evaluates the baseline guard-band fraction $r^{\mathrm{base}}_{g}(i)$ on each tile and switches from $S_0(i)$ to $S_w(i)$ only when $r^{\mathrm{base}}_{g}(i)\ge \tau_g$; otherwise the fixed score is retained. Full details on sliding-window temporal embedding, covariance estimation, and conditioning are kept in the methods companion.

\else
\subsection{Localized detector core and Doppler band geometry}
\paragraph{Tile-based detector family.}
To mitigate the degraded tail sensitivity of global low-rank filters in high-clutter settings, we study a localized matched-subspace detector family on overlapping spatial tiles. Its primary member keeps the same flow-band score without local whitening ($R_i=I$); its adaptive member uses that same score by default and promotes selected tiles onto a whitened branch; its fully whitened member adds a covariance-adaptive slow-time core in which spatial localization enters through tile-local covariance estimation. Let $\Omega_i$ denote tile $i$ and $L_t$ the slow-time aperture. From $X_{\mathrm{base}}$ we construct a set of slow-time training snapshots $\{x_{i,k}\in\mathbb{C}^{L_t}\}_{k=1}^K$ for each tile via spatial sampling and, when applicable, a sliding-window temporal embedding (Hankel construction) in slow time. Here $K$ denotes the number of training snapshots used to estimate the tile covariance; in the fully Hankelized case, each pixel contributes $N=T-L_t+1$ snapshots and pooling across $\Omega_i$ yields $K=N|\Omega_i|$ (or a subsampled/capped variant). We estimate a local covariance
\[
  \wh R_i := \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K x_{i,k}x_{i,k}\Hh,
\]
apply conditioning/diagonal loading to obtain $R_i$, and whiten snapshots as $y_{i,k}:=R_i^{-1/2}x_{i,k}$.
\paragraph{Training support and Hankel dependence.}
In the fully Hankelized case, each pixel in $\Omega_i$ contributes $N=T-L_t+1$ overlapping slow-time snapshots; pooling across the $|\Omega_i|$ pixels yields $K=N|\Omega_i|$ tile-local training vectors. These Hankelized snapshots are temporally correlated due to overlap, so $K$ should not be interpreted as an independent-sample count. Our robust covariance estimators do not require independence, but the effective support can be significantly smaller than $K$; we therefore optionally subsample Hankel columns (stride) and cap the number of columns used for training to bound computation and avoid overweighting highly correlated samples (fixed per setting; \SuppOrApp{app:repro}).

\paragraph{Robust covariance estimation and conditioning for the fully whitened mode.}
The detector equations are unchanged across settings; only the whitening estimator and conditioning recipe vary. In the reported experiments we freeze one covariance recipe per setting and list it explicitly in Table~\ref{tab:result_table_score_map}: Huber for the SIMUS structural and functional rows, Tyler-type for the labeled-brain and Gammex phantom rows, SCM for the fixed 64-frame ULM structural audit, and adaptive Tyler-type promotion for the Shin real-IQ corroboration rows. Whenever whitening is active, we estimate a tile-local covariance, condition it deterministically with the setting's listed loading and support policy, and then apply the same whitened detector equations. The term \emph{Huber-whitened variant} refers only to the fully whitened SIMUS-family recipe; it is not an umbrella label for every whitened result in the paper.

\paragraph{Band operators and matched-subspace detector score.}
We partition the Doppler spectrum into clutter/DC, flow, guard, and alias bands using projectors $(\Po,\Pf,\Pg,\Pa)$ (Notation). For short ensembles, these are exact, orthogonal DFT-bin projectors with $\Po+\Pf+\Pg+\Pa=I$; for longer apertures they are leakage-controlled operators that are approximately orthogonal and are treated as band-energy statistics. We define a matched-subspace detector statistic by flow-subspace energy on whitened snapshots, e.g.,
\ifPAPERONLY
\[
  S_{\mathrm{det}}(i) := \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K
  \frac{y_{i,k}\Hh \Pf\, y_{i,k}}{y_{i,k}\Hh (I-\Pf)\, y_{i,k}+\rho\, y_{i,k}\Hh y_{i,k}+\epsilon},
\]
and form a pixel-level score map $S_{\mathrm{det}}(y,x)$ by overlap-add aggregation of the contributing tile statistics. This detector score is the primary output used for quantitative reporting unless explicitly stated otherwise.
\else
\[
  S_{\mathrm{det,pre}}(i) := \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K
  \frac{y_{i,k}\Hh \Pf\, y_{i,k}}{y_{i,k}\Hh (I-\Pf)\, y_{i,k}+\rho\, y_{i,k}\Hh y_{i,k}+\epsilon},
\]
and form a pixel-level score map $S_{\mathrm{det,pre}}(y,x)$ by overlap-add aggregation of the contributing tile statistics. This pre-regularization score is the primary detector output used for quantitative reporting unless explicitly stated otherwise.
\fi

\paragraph{Regularization and self-normalization avoidance.}
If we whitened using the exact sample covariance formed from the same snapshots being scored, the statistic collapses: the whitened sample covariance becomes (approximately) identity and averaged subspace energies concentrate near constants (e.g.\ $\tr(\Pf)$), destroying discrimination. We avoid this by whitening with a \emph{regularized} covariance $R_i$ (robust estimation plus shrinkage/diagonal loading), so that typically $R_i \neq \wh R_i$ and the matched-subspace energy remains informative. We do not rely on an analytic null distribution for threshold setting; operating points are empirically calibrated on labeled $H_0$ sets under the prespecified protocol of Section~\ref{sec:eval}.

\paragraph{Matched-subspace statistic as a whitened subspace detector.}
Fix tile $i$ and consider a single slow-time snapshot $x\in\mathbb{C}^{L_t}$.
Under a Gaussian clutter-plus-noise model,
\[
  H_0:\ x \sim \mathcal{CN}(0,R_i),\qquad
  H_1:\ x = A_f s + n,\ \ s\sim \mathcal{CN}(0,\sigma_s^2 I),\ \ n\sim \mathcal{CN}(0,R_i),
\]
where the columns of $A_f$ span the flow subspace (equivalently the range of $\Pf$).
After whitening $y=R_i^{-1/2}x$, a matched-subspace detector based on whitened flow-subspace energy arises naturally under this colored-Gaussian model:
\[
  T(x)=x\Hh R_i^{-1/2}\Pf R_i^{-1/2}x = y\Hh \Pf\, y,
\]
which is the standard whitened subspace-energy statistic for this setting. \cite{KayDetection,ScharfFriedlanderMatchedSubspace}
In implementation we report a self-normalized matched-subspace ratio that normalizes flow-subspace energy by the orthogonal complement (and a small ridge). It is motivated by the same whitened subspace geometry as $T(x)$, but it is not identical to the basic GLRT energy statistic; we use it because it behaves better numerically and yields more stable thresholding in practice.
\ifPAPERONLY
This self-normalized statistic is the detector score $S_{\mathrm{det}}(i)$.
\else
This self-normalized statistic is the pre-regularization detector score $S_{\mathrm{det,pre}}(i)$.
\fi

\subsection{Algorithmic integrity and deterministic safeguards}
To make the architecture auditable and to avoid leakage or discretization artifacts masquerading as performance gains, we enforce deterministic, label-free safeguards:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Conditional execution as a decision-rule variant.} To reduce latency, local whitening can be skipped outside a flow-proxy region and replaced by the baseline score there. Because this changes the overall decision rule, any conditional-execution reporting is treated as a distinct algorithmic variant and is accompanied by leakage controls (same-window vs.\ disjoint-window vs.\ randomized proxy masks). Unless otherwise stated, conditional execution uses \emph{disjoint-window} proxy gating for detection reporting.
  \item \textbf{Short-ensemble band discretization sanity.} For extremely short ensembles (e.g.\ $N\in\{5,9,17\}$), the coarse frequency resolution $\Delta f=\mathrm{PRF}/N$ can cause requested bands to collapse or overlap after discretization. Invalid discretizations are flagged explicitly and excluded from detector reporting.
  \item \textbf{Proxy-mask circularity control.} Here, proxy-mask circularity means reusing a proxy-derived mask both to steer conditional execution and to define the same quantitative endpoint being reported. Proxy maps are never used to compute ROC/TPR/FPR. Quantitative ROC claims are restricted to settings with independent $H_1/H_0$ labels (simulator truth or B-mode structural geometry).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Regimes and scope}
\label{sec:results-overview}

The evidence is stratified by setting so that quantitative detection results remain clearly separated from robustness or transfer audits. The main scope is straightforward: downstream detector-head design matters after fixed residualization; the fixed band-limited head is the default structural choice on the held-out SIMUS benchmark; adaptive and fully whitened variants help in narrower clutter-heavy or structure-concentration settings; and fixed-threshold transfer is encouraging in selected settings but not solved.

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{kWave-Stress.} These are controlled stress tests for strict-tail behavior.   \item \textbf{ULM-CoreShell.} This dataset is used for motion-robustness analyses on real IQ, with controlled rigid and nonrigid motion injection and explicit fairness checks.   \item \textbf{Shin-ProxyMotion.} This open real-IQ dataset provides a second label-free motion-stress evaluation under a prespecified low-frequency operating setting.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\paragraph{Results roadmap.}
We first report the held-out SIMUS benchmark that determines the default detector configuration. We then show clutter-heavy stress tests that define when whitening helps. Next, we report mechanism ablations on labeled brain simulations and structurally labeled phantom data. Finally, we report bounded real-IQ structural and motion-robustness audits together with replay feasibility.

\subsection{SIMUS-Struct benchmark}
\label{sec:accepted-v2-main}
This moving-scatterer benchmark is intentionally narrower than a generic SIMUS parameter sweep. It retains the SIMUS/PyMUST moving-scatterer forward model \cite{GarciaSIMUSPartI2022,CigierSIMUSPartII2022}, but the evaluated settings are chosen to reflect whole-brain, cranial-window, mobile, and intra-operative fUS use cases rather than generic simulator realism alone \cite{UrbanWholeBrain2015,Deffieux2021,Soloukey2020FUSAwake,Rabut2024}. We evaluate two prespecified structural settings, which we refer to jointly as SIMUS-Struct: SIMUS-Struct-Mobile and SIMUS-Struct-Intraop. Both were calibrated against real-data operating characteristics and then fixed before held-out testing. A third, more surface-dominated intra-operative setting remains exploratory and is not used for quantitative claims.
This is a synthetic benchmark motivated by reported fUS operating conditions. It does not substitute for prospective clinical validation. We report one frozen benchmark definition with explicit held-out splits and frozen positive, background, and nuisance masks. The structural checkpoint uses development seeds 125--126 to choose the final method families and settings, and held-out evaluation seeds 127--128 for the results in Table~\ref{tab:accepted_v2_structural_main}. The functional checkpoint uses a disjoint development split (seeds 221--222) and held-out evaluation split (seeds 223--224) under a common downstream readout. The evaluation script computes the reported nuisance-control metrics directly from those frozen masks under a fixed right-tail score convention.

For the structural task, the positive class is unaliased parenchymal Pf-band flow and the negative classes are (i) ordinary background and (ii) explicit nuisance structures such as superficial high-velocity or specular components. The three summary metrics used throughout this section are therefore plain-language clinical surrogates: \emph{AUC$_{\mathrm{main/bg}}$} measures separation of target flow from ordinary background, \emph{AUC$_{\mathrm{main/nuis}}$} measures separation of target flow from clinically relevant nuisance, and \emph{FPR$_{\mathrm{nuis}}$ @ TPR$_{\mathrm{main}}=0.5$} asks how many nuisance detections survive when the detector is tuned to retain half of the target positives. This last quantity is the most direct nuisance-tail metric in the table.

\paragraph{Explicit limitations from the labeled-brain audits.}
These labeled-brain audits do not make full whitening the structural default. \paragraph{Baseline fairness and prespecified settings.}
All baseline hyperparameters are fixed prior to reporting results. For the labeled brain simulation scenarios, the MC--SVD energy-fraction cutoff is selected once and then held at $e=0.90$ for all reported windows. \paragraph{Why PD-after-filter baselines can collapse in the strict tail.}
Figure~\ref{fig:strict_tail_collapse_concept} shows the general mechanism: a baseline clutter filter can still show moderate-FPR separation yet fail at ultra-low FPR if the background score distribution has an extreme right tail. Figure~\ref{fig:brain_tail_collapse_visual} is one dataset-specific example of that geometry on the labeled brain benchmark. This tail-dominance failure mode motivates both the strict low-FPR operating discipline and detector heads that explicitly reshape the tail, beginning with band-limited matched-subspace scoring and, when needed, tile-local covariance whitening.

\subsection{Gammex phantom: structural ROC and within-ensemble motion ladders}
\label{sec:twinkling}

\paragraph{Dataset and acquisition parameters.}
We use the open-access Doppler Twinkling Artifact database \cite{LeonovTwinklingDD2021}, which provides complex beamformer output from phantom acquisitions on a clinical scanner. The \emph{Gammex flow phantom} provides a simple vessel-like geometry that lets us derive independent structure-based flow/background labels from B-mode anatomy alone. Each cine frame contains a contemporaneous B-mode image and a short-ensemble color-flow (CFM) slow-time block with $N\in\{5,9,17\}$ pulses. Leonov \emph{et al.}\ explicitly report PRF for some acquisition families, including the transverse linear-probe Gammex acquisition, but also note that PRF information is not consistently preserved across the database. We therefore fix PRF once per sequence family, using the source-paper value where it is explicitly documented and otherwise recording a sequence-level override in bundle metadata. Band-discretization sanity checks flag invalid Pf/Pa discretizations explicitly. Decoding/QC details are provided in \SuppOrApp{app:twinkling_prf_decode} (PRF provenance and decoder sanity); reproduction commands and manifests are provided in \SuppOrApp{app:repro}. Additional artifact-heavy phantom sanity checks are kept in the methods companion.

\paragraph{B-mode-only structural tube masks (independent $H_1/H_0$).}
For the Gammex flow phantom sequences we derive evaluation masks from B-mode structure only (no Doppler or matched-subspace outputs). Using acquisition geometry metadata that specifies the CFM region-of-interest (ROI) within the B-mode grid (beam and sample offsets and any beam subsampling), we map the B-mode ROI onto the CFM grid and segment the tube lumen on a median log-envelope reference image. This yields (i) a conservative lumen (flow) mask, eroded to avoid wall pixels and (ii) a background mask that excludes a guard band around the lumen and bright reflectors. Mask provenance and QC metrics are recorded with each experiment; Figure~\ref{fig:twinkling_gammex_masks} shows representative overlays. These structural labels support a defensible phantom ROC.

\paragraph{Structural-label operating points (Gammex).}
The same strict-tail geometry of Figure~\ref{fig:strict_tail_collapse_concept} appears in the short-ensemble Gammex phantom, where residual twinkling structure dominates the upper background tail. We therefore pool pixel scores across cine frames and report low-FPR operating points using right-tail thresholding on pooled background pixels, then evaluate TPR on pooled lumen pixels. In these short-ensemble Doppler acquisitions, the background set includes strong artifact pixels whose right-tail scores dominate conventional baselines; consequently, low-FPR thresholds at $\alpha\le 10^{-3}$ can lie above essentially all lumen pixels, yielding numerically zero baseline TPR at the reported operating points. Figure~\ref{fig:twinkling_gammex_baseline_tail} shows one representative pooled baseline comparator. To avoid treating pooled pixels as independent samples, we report 95\% confidence intervals via a frame-level bootstrap: we resample cine frames as the unit of variation while holding the pooled threshold fixed. Table~\ref{tab:twinkling_gammex_structural_roc} summarizes a broader same-residual score comparison for the two Gammex views under those same settings: power Doppler and Kasai on the baseline residual, plus three detector-family score variants evaluated on that identical residual cube (whitened power, the fixed matched-subspace detector, and the fully whitened matched-subspace detector). For compactness we omit log-PD from the main table because it is monotone-equivalent to PD here and numerically identical at the reported operating points; the full pooled summaries remain available in \SuppOrApp{app:repro}.
The practical relevance is already visible at a conventional operating point. On the representative Figure~\ref{fig:leading_structural_fidelity} frame at matched background FPR $10^{-2}$, the baseline thresholded map contains 884 suprathreshold pixels and none lie inside the structural lumen mask, whereas the matched-subspace score recovers 586 lumen pixels while holding the same background allowance. Table~\ref{tab:twinkling_gammex_structural_roc} now reports that relaxed $\alpha=10^{-2}$ point alongside the stricter tail operating points, so the phantom result can be read both as a practitioner-facing map-quality summary and as a stress test.
The same-residual phantom comparison shows that the baseline residual still contains recoverable lumen signal, because changing only the downstream score restores nonzero sensitivity. In this short-ensemble setting, local whitening is the dominant ingredient, and total whitened slow-time power is stronger than the matched-subspace ratio on both views, especially across-view. Gammex therefore supports localized covariance-aware downstream scoring, but not the claim that the matched-subspace ratio is the preferred score in every phantom setting.
The view asymmetry still matters: the across view provides the clearest structural-label phantom evidence, whereas the along view remains a harsher artifact-tail stress test under this short-ensemble profile. A small untuned sanity audit in \SuppOrApp{app:supp-results} gives similar values: on the first 20 cine frames, applying the matched-subspace detector directly to raw registered IQ changes TPR@$10^{-3}$ only from $3.48\times 10^{-4}$ to $2.61\times 10^{-4}$ on the along view and from 0.202 to 0.205 on the across view; perturbing the frozen along-view PRF from 2000 to 3000~Hz leaves TPR@$10^{-3}$ unchanged at $3.48\times 10^{-4}$ in that limited audit.

\subsection{ULM real-IQ audits}
\label{sec:ulm7883227}

\paragraph{Dataset and loader.}
We use a real kHz IQ dataset released for ultrasound localization microscopy benchmarking \cite{ChavignonZenodo7883227,HeilesULMBenchmark2021} and distributed as HDF5 blocks with accompanying acquisition metadata. Each block contains a dataset \texttt{iq} of shape $(2,H,W,T)=(2,256,180,800)$ float32 representing I/Q over $T=800$ slow-time frames. We load blocks on demand and return complex IQ as a cube of shape $(T,H,W)$ (reproduction details in \SuppOrApp{app:repro}).

\paragraph{One-time label-free baseline selection.}
For ULM we select a single MC--SVD energy-fraction cutoff by a one-time label-free sweep on blocks 1--3 over frames $0{:}128$, and then freeze it for all subsequent ULM motion experiments. The sweep uses baseline-only runs and relies on label-free integrity and stability criteria (no task labels); it yields a frozen energy fraction $e=0.975$ (full sweep details in \SuppOrApp{app:repro}). The later PALA-backed structural audit and block-split threshold-transfer analysis use the same open dataset and therefore overlap in source blocks, but this one-time sweep used no localization-derived structural labels and fixed only the baseline MC--SVD energy fraction before those analyses were run.

\subsubsection{Localization-derived structural audit}
\paragraph{ULM-CoreShell.}
To obtain a more anatomical structural readout on the same open real-IQ data, we use the published PALA rat-brain ULM example as an external vascular reference derived from microbubble localization microscopy rather than from the Doppler scores being evaluated \cite{ChavignonZenodo7883227,HeilesULMBenchmark2021}. We first reproduce the corresponding short-horizon PALA PowDop rendering from the Zenodo IQ blocks using the published odd-block selection and SVD cutoff, register that published PowDop crop back to the Zenodo IQ field of view, and then downsample the published super-resolution MatOut vascular rendering onto the short-window evaluation lattice. From that registered localization map we derive a conservative vessel-core versus perivascular-shell label pair: the vessel mask is a peak-based core mask, and the background mask is the surrounding shell, so the endpoint asks whether a short-window detector concentrates score on the vascular core rather than the immediately adjacent parenchyma. That one-time PALA-to-IQ registration is part of the label construction only. For the short-window detector itself we keep frame-to-frame registration disabled in the frozen audit, because re-enabling it changes the structural ROC only below the fourth decimal place while adding substantial latency.

On this endpoint, Table~\ref{tab:ulm7883227_structural_roc} reports PD, Kasai, the fixed head, the adaptive head, and the fully whitened variant on the same residual windows and the same localization-derived vessel-core versus shell masks. The fixed and adaptive heads yield only modest pooled-ROC gains over PD and Kasai, and adaptive is nearly indistinguishable from fixed under this profile. The fully whitened variant is the only head that improves pooled AUC, strict-tail sensitivity, and shell FPR at matched 70\% vessel-core recall at once: AUC 0.530 [0.524, 0.537], TPR@$10^{-3}$ 0.004 [0.003, 0.006], and shell FPR 0.676 [0.665, 0.687], compared with AUC 0.514/0.514, TPR@$10^{-3}$ 0.000/0.000, and shell FPR 0.703/0.702 for the fixed/adaptive heads and AUC 0.504/0.503, TPR@$10^{-3}$ 0.000/0.000, and shell FPR 0.698/0.701 for PD/Kasai.

The pooled advantage is not driven by one favorable block. Relative to the fixed head, the fully whitened variant exceeds AUC on 47/70 audited windows and all 10/10 blocks, lowers shell FPR at matched 70\% core recall on 47/70 windows and 9/10 blocks, and yields nonzero TPR@$10^{-3}$ in 45/70 windows versus 10/70 for the fixed and adaptive heads (Table~\ref{tab:ulm_pala_window_consistency}). The same pattern holds against the adaptive head: 46/70 AUC windows, 47/70 shell-FPR windows, and 10/10 blocks. Against baseline power Doppler, the fully whitened variant also wins AUC on all 10 audited blocks, and an exact paired sign-flip test on the block-mean AUC difference gives $p=0.00195$. Figure~\ref{fig:ulm_pala_headline_hero} gives the visual counterpart to this result by showing a representative same-window comparison between baseline power Doppler and the fully whitened variant. On an RTX~4080 SUPER, the same 64-frame no-registration configuration runs at 41.3~ms steady-state total for a 64~ms acquisition budget under the reported replay conditions. That is replay-feasibility evidence only, not scanner integration or OR-ready deployment. The labels come from a published localization-derived vascular rendering on the same acquisition rather than from an independent modality or task-based ground truth, so this remains a structural rather than functional result.

To reduce overlap between localization-derived label construction and scored windows, we also ran a stricter odd-block-reference / even-block-evaluation variant under the same frozen 64-frame profile. The family ordering remained directionally consistent: the fully whitened variant still led on pooled AUC and strict-tail sensitivity, though with smaller margins than in the main same-acquisition audit. This stricter split reduces, but does not eliminate, the same-acquisition overlap limitation, and the summary is provided in the Supplementary Material. A supplementary falsification control points in the same direction: support-matched randomization of the vessel-core and shell masks collapses the structural audit toward chance, while a large cyclic translation destroys the fully whitened ordering rather than reproducing it.

\subsection{Computational cost and replay feasibility}
\label{sec:latency}

We also report representative runtimes for the final detector family evaluated in this manuscript rather than for a whitening-heavy research configuration. In this evaluation, the labeled-brain stress tests use the fixed matched-subspace default with conditional execution, the Shin real-IQ evaluation uses the adaptive variant, and Gammex uses the short-ensemble raw-baseline plus fixed matched-subspace head. Conditional execution changes the decision rule by deciding which tiles receive the adaptive detector, so it is reported as part of the detector configuration rather than as a free acceleration of the same method. Unless otherwise stated, conditional-execution reporting uses the same leakage-controlled, disjoint-window proxy discipline as the detection results.

Latency is measured by replaying multiple windows or frames in a single GPU process and reporting \emph{steady-state} means over windows/frames 2..N. The first replay window/frame is excluded because it includes one-time startup overheads. We time the baseline and detector stages separately, excluding disk I/O, and ``Total'' reports baseline plus detector wall time. ``Budget'' denotes the acquired slow-time duration of the corresponding ensemble: $64/1500\approx 42.7$~ms for the brain windows, $128/1000=128$~ms for Shin, and $17/2500=6.8$~ms for the Gammex cines. The reported real-time factor (RTF) is therefore $\text{Total}/\text{Budget}$.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection*{What the detector family adds}
Post-beamformed fUS benefits from a better detector, not only from better residualization. Ordinary clutter suppression can still leave structured right-tail false detections, and the central result of this paper is that changing only the downstream score head can materially change nuisance control on the same residual cube. The mechanism depends on setting: matched-subspace geometry is the main driver on the labeled-brain stress tests and the held-out SIMUS benchmark, whereas local slow-time whitening becomes more important in short-ensemble or structure-concentration settings. Gammex-Struct is the clearest counterexample to a universal matched-subspace claim: on the same residual phantom data, whitened power is stronger than the matched-subspace ratio on both views. These results define a detector family with a clear fixed default and narrower whitened variants rather than one head or one mechanism that wins everywhere.

For a practitioner, the immediate implication is that the same SVD residual already used in existing pipelines can yield a more trustworthy score map by changing only the final scoring step. The fixed head requires no additional training data and no retuning of the upstream clutter filter. On the held-out benchmark, it eliminates all nuisance detections at matched 50\% target-flow recall on the same residual where power Doppler passes 97--100\% of nuisance. That is not a marginal image-quality change; it is the difference between a map that can be thresholded with confidence and one that still requires visual inspection to separate artifact from signal. Whether that difference transfers fully to real \emph{in vivo} task data remains the central open question.

The comparator set is intentionally residualizer-heavy rather than learning-heavy. Learned clutter filters remain important related work, but they address residual formation rather than the downstream detector-head question isolated here. Extending the same-residual comparisons in this paper to trained detector heads or learned residualizers would require a separate supervised training protocol and is left for future work.

The strict-tail operating points in the labeled experiments are calibrated using known negative sets, but a scanner does not have frame-by-frame labeled negatives at runtime. Here that tail calibration is an \emph{evaluation protocol}, not the intended clinical operating rule. The transfer checks show some non-retrospective signal on SIMUS-Struct and ULM-CoreShell, while the Shin proxy transfer remains negative, so a practical thresholding workflow on raw \emph{in vivo} IQ remains unestablished.

\subsection*{Real-data scope and clinical interpretation}
Most public \emph{in vivo} fUS releases do not preserve the raw high-PRF complex IQ and metadata needed for independent detector evaluation after beamforming. For that reason, the strongest efficacy evidence in this paper still comes from labeled simulations and structurally labeled phantom data, while the open real-IQ sections are narrower robustness and structural audits. The ULM-CoreShell result is directionally positive but modest, and only the fully whitened variant shows a clear advantage there. The real-data contribution is therefore improved vascular and nuisance discrimination at the detector stage, not biological inference or real-data functional task validation.

A stricter disjoint-source odd/even block audit remained directionally consistent but weaker, and a support-matched permutation control collapsed the structural audit toward chance. Together these checks reduce the simplest same-acquisition leakage objections, but they do not turn the result into independent multimodal or cross-session validation.

The simulation-to-reality gap should calibrate expectations: the held-out synthetic benchmark determines the default detector choice much more cleanly than the current real-IQ audits. What is still missing is a standardized open benchmark with repeated sessions and independent anatomical or task anchors for post-beamformed detector evaluation \cite{LambertNeuropixels2025,COMBOWindow2024,ChenComa2025,Rabut2024,MobileFUS2025}.

\subsection*{Recommended detector variant by regime}
Figure~\ref{fig:variant_selection_flowchart} summarizes the practical deployment rule supported by the current evidence. The fixed head remains the default, while the fully whitened variant is reserved for repeated guard-dominant windows with nonzero promotion and sufficient latency margin; the current adaptive telemetry is useful for label-free monitoring, but not yet a validated universal selector.

\section{Conclusion and future work}
We presented a localized matched-subspace detector framework for post-beamformed fUS and ultrafast Doppler data, centered on a band-limited fixed head and extended by an adaptive whitening switch plus a fully whitened variant. Across the evaluated labeled simulations, SIMUS-Struct, and one structurally labeled phantom setting, this detector family often improved low-FPR nuisance control relative to PD/Kasai-style downstream scores, but the preferred downstream head depended on setting. Across those tiers, the fixed band-limited head remains the structural default, while the adaptive and fully whitened variants help in selected clutter-heavy settings rather than serving as universal replacements. The phantom result should be read accordingly: it supports localized covariance-aware downstream scoring, but not a universal matched-subspace mechanism.

On one open real-IQ rat-brain dataset, the same-acquisition ULM structural audit shows only modest gains for the fixed and adaptive heads, while the fully whitened variant is the only head that clearly improves pooled AUC, strict-tail sensitivity, and shell suppression on the vessel-core versus perivascular-shell endpoint. A stricter disjoint-source odd/even block variant preserved that ordering with attenuated margins, so the real-IQ structural signal is not wholly dependent on block overlap inside the same acquisition. Fixed-threshold transfer remains only partly established: SIMUS-Struct and the ULM structural audit retain non-retrospective signal, while the Shin proxy transfer stays negative. The method is therefore positioned as a detector-stage contribution for post-beamformed fUS pipelines, not as completed real-data functional validation. The main next external test is prospective labeled \emph{in vivo} validation on raw high-PRF IQ with independent anatomical or task anchors and a practical non-retrospective thresholding workflow.

More broadly, the same-residual evaluation framework used here, freezing the clutter-suppressed cube and comparing only the downstream score, provides a general protocol for benchmarking post-beamformed fUS detection methods and could serve as a standardized evaluation layer as the field moves toward open reproducible pipelines.

\section{Practical Band Design for Brain fUS (PRF 1.5--5 kHz, T 8--16)}
\label{sec:band-geometry}

We provide practical band guidelines for selecting Pf/guard/Pa with explicit physical anchors.

For brain fUS at center frequencies $f_0 \approx 6$--$15$ MHz and PRF $\approx 1.5$--$5$ kHz \cite{Mace2013,Rabut2020,Imbault2017,Urban2022,Deffieux2021}:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Microvascular brain flows (arterioles/venules) in humans and rodents are typically in the range $v \approx 3$--$20$ mm/s for the vessels that dominate functional responses \cite{Imbault2017,Urban2022}. At $f_0=7.5$ MHz and $c\approx 1540$ m/s, this corresponds to Doppler shifts $f_D\approx 30$--$200$ Hz.
  \item Low--frequency tissue motion (respiration, cardiac pulsation) produces strong spectral content below $\approx 20$ Hz; this should be contained in Po and largely removed by high--pass filtering and/or SVD.
  \item Large--vessel flows (e.g.\ MCA--like arteries) reach $v\approx 0.3$--$0.8$ m/s, corresponding to $f_D$ in the kHz range \cite{TCDVelocities,TCDOptimalFlow}. Depending on PRF and angle, a portion of this energy appears in high Doppler bands and can alias into the $>$400 Hz region corresponding to Pa \cite{ICDFUS2021,MobileFUS2025}.
\end{itemize}

Thus a reasonable fixed band design for brain fUS with $T\approx 8$--$16$ and PRF $\approx 1.5$--$3$ kHz is:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Flow band $\Cf$: $[30,250]$ Hz, covering microvascular flows $\approx 3$--$20$ mm/s.
  \item Guard band $\Cg$: $[250,400]$ Hz, used to protect against band-edge leakage.
  \item Alias band $\Ca$: $[400, \text{Nyquist}]$ Hz, capturing high Doppler where large-vessel or aliased flows and certain motion artifacts live.
\end{itemize}

In discrete terms, for $T=8$ at PRF=1.5 kHz (bin spacing $187.5$ Hz), this maps to:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item $\Pf$ on first off--DC bin (with DPSS leakage covering $\approx 30$--$250$ Hz),
  \item guard around the second bin,
  \item $\Pa$ on higher bins with bin--level guard as per BG--1/BG--2.
\end{itemize}

\section{PD-only shrink-only prior summary panel (hemodynamic): band-energy statistics and aggregation}
\label{app:pdonly-dashboard}

This appendix specifies a PD-only instantiation of the shrink-only prior and the associated summary-panel aggregation used for systematic plane sweeps (e.g.\ Mac{\'e}/Urban whole-brain mouse fUS). The key discipline is unchanged: \emph{runtime} prior selection is label-free (proxy sets), while atlas-defined $H_1/H_0$ labels are used only \emph{offline} for descriptive reporting and are never used as prior inputs.

\subsection*{A) PD-only band-energy feature definitions (math)}
\paragraph{PD movie and tiling.}
Fix one coronal plane (one ``configuration'') with a PD movie
\[
P[t,y,x]\in\R_{\ge 0},\qquad t=0,\dots,T-1,
\]
sampled at rate $f_s=1/dt$ (Mac{\'e}: $f_s\approx 10$~Hz). Tile each frame into overlapping spatial tiles $\Omega_i$ of size $h\times w$ (Mac{\'e}: $8\times 8$) with stride $s$ (Mac{\'e}: $s=3$).

\paragraph{Tile PD time series.}
Define the tile-averaged PD time series
\[
p_i[t] := \frac{1}{|\Omega_i|}\sum_{(y,x)\in\Omega_i} P[t,y,x].
\]
For robustness across tiles, we optionally use a log-stabilized, mean-removed series
\[
u_i[t] := \log(p_i[t]+\epsilon_{\mathrm{pd}}),\qquad
\tilde u_i[t] := u_i[t]-\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t'=0}^{T-1} u_i[t'].
\]

\paragraph{Sliding-window temporal embedding (Hankel snapshots) and whitening.}
Fix a slow-time aperture $L_t$ (tens of frames in PD-only). Define $K=T-L_t+1$ and form multiple slow-time snapshots using a sliding-window temporal embedding (Hankel matrix construction):
\[
x_{i,k} :=
\begin{bmatrix}
\tilde u_i[k]\\
\tilde u_i[k+1]\\
\vdots\\
\tilde u_i[k+L_t-1]
\end{bmatrix}\in\R^{L_t},\qquad k=0,\dots,K-1.
\]
Estimate a per-tile covariance (SCM or robust variant)
\[
\wh R_i := \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=0}^{K-1} x_{i,k}x_{i,k}\Hh,
\]
apply conditioning/diagonal loading to obtain $R_i$, and whiten
\[
y_{i,k} := R_i^{-1/2}x_{i,k}.
\]
We pool whitened snapshots to a single per-tile slow-time vector
\[
z_i := \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=0}^{K-1} y_{i,k}\in\R^{L_t}.
\]

\paragraph{Hemodynamic band projectors and energies.}
Let $\Cf,\Cg,\Ca$ denote Pf/guard/Pa hemodynamic bands (Mac{\'e}: $\Cf=[0,0.5]$~Hz, $\Cg=(0.5,1.0]$~Hz, $\Ca=(1.0,3.0]$~Hz). Build fixed temporal projectors $(\Pf,\Pg,\Pa)$ onto the span of discrete Fourier tones whose frequencies lie in these bands, and define $\Po := I-\Pf-\Pg-\Pa$ (``other''/out-of-band). Define band energies
\[
E_f(i) := z_i\Hh \Pf z_i,\quad
E_g(i) := z_i\Hh \Pg z_i,\quad
E_a(i) := z_i\Hh \Pa z_i,\quad
E_o(i) := z_i\Hh \Po z_i,
\]
and $E_{\mathrm{tot}}(i) := E_f+E_g+E_a+E_o+\epsilon$.

\paragraph{Risk metrics and Pf-peak statistics.}
We use the same band-energy feature primitives as in the Doppler regime:
\[
m_{\mathrm{alias}}(i) := \log\frac{E_a(i)+\epsilon}{E_f(i)+\epsilon},\qquad
m_{\mathrm{guard}}(i) := \log\frac{E_g(i)+\epsilon}{E_f(i)+\epsilon},\qquad
r_g(i) := \frac{E_g(i)}{E_{\mathrm{tot}}(i)}.
\]
Define a whitened periodogram of the pooled vector $z_i$,
\[
\wh z_i[n] := \sum_{\ell=0}^{L_t-1} z_i[\ell]\,e^{-2\pi i n\ell/L_t},\qquad
S_i[n] := |\wh z_i[n]|^2,\qquad n=0,\dots,\lfloor L_t/2\rfloor,
\]
and the (non-DC) peak frequency $f_{\mathrm{peak}}(i)=\frac{n_{\mathrm{peak}}}{L_t dt}$ where
$n_{\mathrm{peak}}(i):=\arg\max_{n\ge 1} S_i[n]$. The Pf-peak indicator and occupancy on a set $\mathcal{T}$ are
\[
\pi_f(i) := \mathbf{1}\{f_{\mathrm{peak}}(i)\in\Cf\},\qquad
\mathrm{PfPeakFrac}(\mathcal{T}) := \frac{1}{|\mathcal{T}|}\sum_{i\in\mathcal{T}}\pi_f(i).
\]

\subsection*{B) PD-only proxy sets and prior inputs (math)}
The PD-only prior uses label-free proxy sets based on a PD-derived flow-coverage proxy, analogous to the IQ flow-mask coverage $c_f$.
Let $\bar P[y,x] := \frac{1}{T}\sum_t P[t,y,x]$ be the mean PD image (optionally restricted to a brain mask). Define a high-quantile flow proxy mask
\[
M_{\mathrm{flow}}(y,x) := \mathbf{1}\{\bar P[y,x]\ge Q_{q_{\mathrm{flow}}}(\bar P)\},
\]
and per-tile flow coverage
\[
c_f(i) := \frac{1}{|\Omega_i|}\sum_{(y,x)\in\Omega_i} M_{\mathrm{flow}}(y,x)\in[0,1].
\]
With frozen design thresholds $c_{\mathrm{bg}}$ and $c_{\mathrm{flow}}$ (typical $c_{\mathrm{bg}}\approx 0.05$, $c_{\mathrm{flow}}\approx 0.20$), define
\[
\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}} := \{i:\ c_f(i)\le c_{\mathrm{bg}}\},\quad
\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{flow}} := \{i:\ c_f(i)\ge c_{\mathrm{flow}}\},\quad
\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{nonbg}} := \{i:\ c_f(i)>c_{\mathrm{bg}}\}.
\]
If $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{flow}}$ is too small (insufficient proxy support), a conservative fallback is to define proxy sets via tilewise mean-$\bar P$ quantiles and record this fallback choice in the exported metadata.

\subsection*{C) Prior-regime selection for PD-only (math)}
\paragraph{Baseline score for PD-only prior.}
For Mac{\'e}, the baseline scalar detector is PD-z on tile time series: choose a baseline segment $B$ (e.g.\ first third of frames) and define
\[
s_{\mathrm{base}}(i) := \max_{t\notin B} \frac{u_i[t]-\mu_i^{(B)}}{\sigma_i^{(B)}+\epsilon_z},
\]
with $\mu_i^{(B)},\sigma_i^{(B)}$ the mean and standard deviation of $u_i[t]$ over $t\in B$. Higher $s_{\mathrm{base}}$ means more activation.

\paragraph{Risk-mode selection and integrity checks.}
The PD-only prior follows the same posture as the Doppler-IQ prior:
if risk statistics are flat (e.g.\ $\mathrm{IQR}(m_{\mathrm{alias}}\mid\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}})$ too small) or proxy support is insufficient, we default to the uninformative regime ($\mathcal{R}_0$; $w\equiv 1$) and record an explicit disablement reason in the metadata. If guard dominates (e.g.\ $Q_{0.90}(r_g\mid\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}})$ above a frozen threshold), we disallow the experimental alias-dominant regime $\mathcal{R}_2$ and either remain in a guard-risk safety lane ($\mathcal{R}_1$; requiring tail association of $r_g$) or default to $\mathcal{R}_0$ as out of regime.

\paragraph{Actionability checks (experimental alias-dominant regime $\mathcal{R}_2$, label-free).}
The experimental alias-dominant regime $\mathcal{R}_2$ (research-only eligibility; reporting-only) requires additional evidence that risk statistics explain high-score background tails. In the selected risk mode ($m_{\mathrm{risk}}=m_{\mathrm{alias}}$ or $m_{\mathrm{guard}}$), define
\[
\Delta_{bf} := \operatorname{median}(m_{\mathrm{risk}}\mid\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}})-
               \operatorname{median}(m_{\mathrm{risk}}\mid\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{flow}}),
\]
and within $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}}$ define a tail subset $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{tail}}$ as the top $1\%$ of $s_{\mathrm{base}}$ and a mid subset $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{mid}}$ as a central quantile band; require
\[
\Delta_{\mathrm{tail}} := \operatorname{median}(m_{\mathrm{risk}}\mid\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{tail}})-
                          \operatorname{median}(m_{\mathrm{risk}}\mid\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{mid}})
                          \ge \Delta_{\mathrm{tail},\min}.
\]
Finally, we enforce a Pf-realization eligibility condition for $\mathcal{R}_2$:
\[
\mathrm{PfPeakFrac}(\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{flow}})\ge p_{f,\min}.
\]
If these checks fail, the prior remains in $\mathcal{R}_0$ or $\mathcal{R}_1$ (guarded only) and does not enter $\mathcal{R}_2$.

\paragraph{Coverage sentinels.}
As in the Doppler-IQ prior, penalization must be neither inert nor global: after defining protected/candidate sets (below), the penalized fraction
\[
p_{\mathrm{shrink}} := \mathbb{P}\{g=1\mid \mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{cand}}\}
\]
must lie in frozen bounds (e.g.\ $p_{\min}\le p_{\mathrm{shrink}}\le p_{\max}$), otherwise we default to $\mathcal{R}_0$ ($w\equiv 1$) and record a coverage-out-of-bounds sentinel.

\subsection*{D) PD-only shrink-only prior mapping (optional; summary-panel parity)}
The PD-only instantiation supports both a hard suppression used in the Mac{\'e} systematic-sweep reporting and a soft shrink mapping for summary-panel parity.
Define a protected set $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{prot}}$ (tiles with high flow coverage and/or very high baseline scores) and a candidate set $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{cand}}$ (non-protected tiles in an uncertain score window). Using a background-calibrated risk threshold $\tau_{\mathrm{risk}}=Q_{q_{\mathrm{risk}}}(m_{\mathrm{risk}}\mid\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}})$, define the penalization indicator
\[
g(i)=\mathbf{1}\{i\in\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{cand}},\ m_{\mathrm{risk}}(i)\ge\tau_{\mathrm{risk}}\}.
\]
\emph{Hard suppression (Mac{\'e} sweep):} apply the binary indicator $g$ as a hard suppression on positive activations,
\[
s_{\mathrm{shrink}}(i)=s_{\mathrm{base}}(i) - g(i)\max(s_{\mathrm{base}}(i),0),
\]
which is equivalent to setting positive penalized tiles to zero while leaving negative scores unchanged. \emph{Soft shrink (summary-panel parity):} set $w(i)=\max(w_{\min},\exp(-k\,u(i)))$ with a robust normalized exceedance $u(i)\ge 0$ and apply $s_{\mathrm{shrink}}(i)=s_{\mathrm{base}}(i)-(1-w(i))\max(s_{\mathrm{base}}(i),0)$. In both cases, $g(i)=0$ (and $w(i)=1$) on $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{prot}}$ enforces the do-not-penalize constraint (protected-set scores unchanged) and $s_{\mathrm{shrink}}(i)\le s_{\mathrm{base}}(i)$ enforces shrink-only behavior.

\subsection*{E) Summary-panel schema and aggregation (PD-only)}
For PD-only datasets the summary panel is computed per plane (configuration) and written as a CSV with one row per (scan, plane). Each row records: (i) identifiers and frozen settings (scan/plane, $T$, $f_s$, $L_t$, tile geometry, band edges), (ii) proxy support counts ($|\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}}|$, $|\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{flow}}|$), (iii) prior outputs (region $\in\{\mathcal{R}_0,\mathcal{R}_1,\mathcal{R}_2\}$, reason label, risk\_mode; exported in code as \texttt{state}/\texttt{reason}), (iv) key band-energy statistics ($\mathrm{PfPeakFrac}(\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{flow}})$, $Q_{0.90}(r_g\mid\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}})$, $\mathrm{IQR}(m_{\mathrm{risk}}\mid\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}})$, $\Delta_{bf}$, $\Delta_{\mathrm{tail}}$), and (v) regularizer coverage/strength when active ($p_{\mathrm{shrink}}$ and scale/shrink quantiles). Atlas-labeled descriptive fields are prefixed \texttt{offline\_} (e.g.\ \texttt{offline\_pf\_peak\_frac\_H1}, \texttt{offline\_pf\_peak\_frac\_H0}, \texttt{offline\_hits\_pre/post}) to make clear that they are not prior inputs.

Summary-panel aggregation mirrors the Doppler-IQ diagnostic panel: histograms of prior regions and (region, reason) pairs, a risk-mode histogram, distributions of key band-energy statistics (Pf peak fraction, guard q90, risk IQR), and (for active planes) shrink coverage/strength summaries. Any atlas-stratified Mac{\'e} plots (e.g.\ PfPeak(VIS/SC/LGd) vs PfPeak(CP/CA/DG), alias separation) are presented as a separate, explicitly offline/descriptive panel.

\subsection*{F) ``Do not overclaim'' messaging (PD-only summary panel)}
When reporting a PD-only prior summary panel, we use the following posture in captions and limitations:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item PD-only band-energy statistics operate on low-rate PD time series and are an auditability check; they do not validate Doppler-side uplift on raw IQ.
  \item The uninformative regime ($\mathcal{R}_0$; $w\equiv 1$) is an intended success outcome when feature evidence is flat or non-actionable; the summary panel is designed to make these cases explicit via reason labels.
  \item Atlas-stratified results on Mac{\'e} are descriptive; prior-positive slices identified by scanning the same dataset are not treated as held-out confirmation.
  \item The experimental alias-dominant regime ($\mathcal{R}_2$) is a reporting discipline requiring additional label-free actionability evidence; $\mathcal{R}_1$ is guarded only and should not be presented as a clinical performance guarantee.
\end{itemize}

\section{Algorithmic details (supplementary)}
\label{app:alg-details}

\subsection{Shrink-only prior regime selection (frozen defaults)}
The shrink-only prior consumes per-tile arrays
$S_{\mathrm{base}}$, $m_{\mathrm{alias}}$, $r_g$, $c_f$, and $\mathrm{PfPeak}$ and returns a compact regime report (region label, a disablement reason when inert, and diagnostic metrics). The reference implementation is provided with the accompanying code. The frozen default thresholds used throughout this repository are:
  \begin{itemize}[nosep]
	  \item \textbf{Proxy thresholds:} $c_{\mathrm{bg}}=0.05$, $c_{\mathrm{flow}}=0.20$.
	  \item \textbf{Candidate/protected score quantiles:} candidate window $q_{\mathrm{lo}}=0.30$, $q_{\mathrm{hi,cand}}=1.0$; optional high-score protection $q_{\mathrm{hi,prot}}=0.99999$ when the protected set includes extreme-score tiles.
	  \item \textbf{Risk threshold:} $q_{\mathrm{risk}}=0.90$ applied to $m_{\mathrm{alias}}$ on $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}}$.
  \item \textbf{Feature integrity (flatness + guard):} $\mathrm{IQR}(m_{\mathrm{alias}}\mid\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}})\ge 0.25$; actionable eligibility disabled if $Q_{0.90}(r_g)>0.25$; ``guard safety'' lane allowed up to $Q_{0.90}(r_g)\le 0.70$ if guard is actionable, requiring $\mathrm{IQR}(r_g\mid\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}})\ge 0.03$ and $\Delta_{\mathrm{tail,guard}}\ge 0.01$.
  \item \textbf{C2 actionability thresholds:} $\Delta_{bf}\ge 0.30$ and $\Delta_{\mathrm{tail}}\ge 0.20$ (computed on $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}}/\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{flow}}$ proxies as in \ifSUPPLEMENTONLY the main paper\else Section~\ref{sec:ka-contract-v2}\fi).
  \item \textbf{C2 Pf-realization eligibility:} $\mathrm{PfPeakFrac}(\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{flow}})\ge 0.05$ with $|\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{flow}}|\ge 50$.
  \item \textbf{Coverage bounds:} safety lane $p_{\mathrm{shrink}}\in[0.02,0.25]$ (guard lane $[0.02,0.15]$), actionable lane $p_{\mathrm{shrink}}\in[0.05,0.40]$.
	  \item \textbf{Invariance sentinel:} if $\mathrm{IQR}(\log w\mid \text{penalized}) < 0.02$ then return the off state with an invariance/no-effect sentinel.
	  \item \textbf{Sample support:} $n_{\min}=500$ valid tiles, $n_{\mathrm{bg},\min}=200$ background-proxy tiles, $n_{\mathrm{cand},\min}=200$ candidate tiles, $n_{\mathrm{tail},\min}=20$, $n_{\mathrm{mid},\min}=20$.
  \end{itemize}
The report exports one of three states (off / safety / actionable), corresponding to feature-space regimes $\mathcal{R}_0/\mathcal{R}_1/\mathcal{R}_2$ in the main text. When the prior is forced inert, the report includes an explicit reason category (e.g.\ missing inputs, insufficient proxy support, feature flatness, invariance/no-effect sentinels, or invalid band discretization in short ensembles). Reason labels are enumerated in the code and logged in the exported metadata.

\subsection{Score-space shrink mapping and exported maps}
When the score-space shrink-only regularizer is enabled, per-tile shrink factors $w(i)\in(0,1]$ are derived deterministically from the regime report and applied in score space as
\[
S_{\mathrm{post}}(i)=w(i)\,S_{\mathrm{pre}}(i),\qquad w(i)\in(0,1].
\]
For convenience (and compatibility with legacy \path{pd} arrays), bundles store an equivalent per-pixel \emph{scale} map $\gamma(i)=1/w(i)\ge 1$, averaged over overlapping tiles and applied only on the union of penalized tiles as
\[
\mathrm{PD}_{\mathrm{post}}(i)=\mathrm{PD}_{\mathrm{pre}}(i)/\gamma(i).
\]
A binary gate map stores the penalized region. Protected pixels are enforced by setting $\gamma=1$ on the protected set (flow mask and extreme-score pixels as in \ifSUPPLEMENTONLY the main paper\else Section~\ref{sec:ka-contract-v2}\fi).

\paragraph{Implementation score conventions.}
Throughout the manuscript, quantitative low-FPR operating points are reported using flow-evidence score maps under a right-tail convention (higher = more flow-like; thresholds calibrated on the high-score tail) intended for labeled ROC and robustness reporting (Table~\ref{tab:score_taxonomy}). Exported bundles include the baseline score and the matched-subspace score before the optional shrink-only penalty, along with optional conventional baseline comparators (e.g.\ log-PD and Kasai) and diagnostic/proxy maps used for gating and audits. Proxy maps are not used as the primary detector scores for labeled ROC reporting. When the shrink-only penalty is enabled, the exported maps enforce a shrink-only mapping on the chosen detector score: in this right-tail score space, $S_{\mathrm{post}}=w\,S_{\mathrm{pre}}$ with $w\in(0,1]$; for convenience, bundles also store the equivalent per-pixel scale map $\gamma=1/w\ge 1$ applied on the penalized region.

\section{Simulation details (supplementary)}

\subsection{k-Wave brain pilots (profile definitions)}
The k-Wave brain pilots used in this repository are generated using our simulation pipeline (see \SuppOrApp{app:repro} for the exact commands). The shared acquisition/geometry settings are:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Grid: $N_x=N_y=240$ with $dx=dy=90~\mu$m; PML size 16; $c\approx 1540$~m/s; $f_0=7.5$~MHz; 3 cycles.

\paragraph{Synthetic flow/alias/clutter overlays.}
To make Doppler/alias structure explicit and reproducible, the pilots apply deterministic slow-time overlays on the beamformed cube (code: \path{sim/kwave/common.py}). The canonical open-skull pilot overlays (internally, profile \texttt{Brain-OpenSkull}) in the Makefile are:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Flow Doppler on the flow mask: \texttt{--flow-doppler-min-hz 60}, \texttt{--flow-doppler-max-hz 180}.
  \item Background alias tone: \texttt{--bg-alias-hz 650} with fraction 0.3 in depth $[0.30,0.70]$ and jitter $\pm 50$~Hz.
  \item Colored temporal clutter: \path{--clutter-beta} 1.0 with target SNR 20~dB over depth $[0.20,0.95]$.
  \item Aperture phase screen: \texttt{--aperture-phase-std 0.8} rad, correlation length 14 elements.
\end{itemize}
The shallow-pial variant (internal profile \texttt{Brain-Pial128}) uses 4 ensembles with 32 pulses each ($T=128$ compounded frames) and concentrates shallow alias in depth $[0.15,0.30]$ with a stronger alias fraction and a vibration tone. The alias-dominant and structured-clutter variants (internal profiles \texttt{Brain-AliasContract} and \texttt{Brain-SkullOR}) use additional alias/vibration settings and skull/aberration tagging; all simulation parameters are fixed per profile and are specified in the reproducibility manifest (\SuppOrApp{app:repro}).

\subsection{Replay/analysis pipeline used for ROC and latency}
Downstream processing is run on the saved pilot angle data using the standard replay script, which loads angle directories (or ensembles of angle directories), forms the slow-time cube, applies the chosen baseline, runs the matched-subspace detector, and writes a standard output bundle (\path{sim/kwave/common.py:write_acceptance_bundle}). Low-FPR ROC points and feasibility telemetry are extracted using \path{scripts/hab_contract_check.py}.

For the conservative-profile matched-subspace detector runs used in the latency suite and robustness sweeps, we additionally use \path{scripts/run_brain_replay_sweep.py}, which:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Reuses a fixed pilot per (regime, PRF) and replays the baseline plus matched-subspace detector under a frozen profile on 64-frame windows (\texttt{--time-window-length 64}).
  \item Evaluates the detector variants used in the latency suite, including conditional execution and a reduced-support mode that caps effective training support by subsampling training snapshots. Exact runtime settings are recorded in bundle metadata and listed in the reproducibility manifest (\SuppOrApp{app:repro}).
  \item Uses a frozen MC--SVD baseline and simulator-truth evaluation masks. Conditional execution is driven by a deterministic, label-free proxy flow mask derived from baseline PD brightness.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Latency reporting protocol (steady-state, CUDA graphs).}
When GPU acceleration is enabled, the first replay window can include one-time overheads such as CUDA context initialization, kernel compilation, and optional CUDA-graph capture. For any GPU latency numbers reported from this codebase, we therefore use a steady-state replay protocol: \path{scripts/latency_rerun_check.py} replays $N$ windows in a single process (\texttt{--steady-windows $N$} or \texttt{--window-offsets}) and reports per-window timing, with the publishable latency taken as the mean over windows 2..$N$ (window 1 treated as cold-start overhead).

\section{Implementation details (supplementary)}
\label{app:impl-details}

\subsection{RPCA baseline (full)}
The RPCA baseline used in this repository is implemented as \path{sim/kwave/common.py:_baseline_pd_rpca}. It performs a windowed Principal Component Pursuit (PCP) using an inexact augmented Lagrangian method (IALM) on a downsampled Casorati matrix:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Spatial downsampling: complex average pooling by factor 2 when $\max(H,W)>128$.
  \item Temporal windows: default subwindow length $t_{\mathrm{sub}}=80$ (or shorter if $T<80$); windows are processed sequentially.
  \item Optimization: IALM with at most 80 inner iterations per window (\path{max_iters_inner=min(max_iters,80)}), tolerance $10^{-4}$.
  \item Shrinkage: $\lambda = 1/\sqrt{\max(m,n)}$ when not supplied, where $m=T_w$ and $n=H_dW_d$.
  \item Low-rank step: singular value thresholding using either full SVD or a randomized SVD depending on matrix size, with a per-window rank heuristic $k=\min(8,\max(2,\lfloor T_w/10\rfloor))$.
  \item Output PD: sum of sparse-component energy over windows on the downsampled grid, then nearest-neighbor upsampling to the original resolution.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{HOSVD baseline (full)}
The tensor HOSVD baseline is implemented as \path{sim/kwave/common.py:_baseline_pd_hosvd}. The cube $X\in\mathbb{C}^{T\times H\times W}$ is optionally spatially downsampled (complex average pooling). Mode-0/1/2 SVDs are computed on the unfoldings and ranks are chosen either explicitly (\texttt{--hosvd-ranks}) or by energy fractions (\texttt{--hosvd-energy-fracs}, default $(0.99,0.99,0.99)$). The low-rank approximation is reconstructed by projecting onto the multilinear subspace, and PD is the time-averaged residual energy $\frac{1}{T}\sum_t |X(t)-L(t)|^2$. In the brain baselines used in this repository, the Makefile target \texttt{r4c-replay-hosvd} runs HOSVD with \texttt{--hosvd-spatial-downsample 2} and \texttt{--hosvd-energy-fracs 0.99,0.99,0.99}.

\subsection{Adaptive SVD baseline (spatial singular-vector similarity cutoff)}
\label{app:baseline-adaptive-svd}
To preempt the common critique that global SVD baselines are sensitive to an arbitrary fixed rank/energy threshold, we include an adaptive spatiotemporal SVD baseline inspired by Baranger \emph{et al.}\ \cite{BarangerAdaptiveSVD2018}. For a windowed slow-time cube $X\in\mathbb{C}^{T\times H\times W}$ we form the Casorati matrix $C\in\mathbb{C}^{T\times N}$ with $N=HW$ and compute its SVD $C=U\Sigma V\Hh$. Let $v_k\in\mathbb{C}^{N}$ denote the $k$th \emph{spatial} singular vector (column of $V$). Because singular vectors are defined up to an arbitrary complex phase, we measure spatial coherence on magnitudes and define
\[
  s_k := \frac{\langle |v_k|,\ |v_{k+1}|\rangle}{\| |v_k|\|\ \| |v_{k+1}|\|},\qquad k=1,\dots,T-1.
\]
We smooth $\{s_k\}$ by a short median filter and choose the clutter rank $r$ as the first index where $s_k$ falls below a robust threshold $\tau=\mathrm{median}(s)-\kappa\cdot \mathrm{MAD}(s)$, with $r$ clamped to a fixed range $[r_{\min},r_{\max}]$ (all hyperparameters frozen per regime). For the labeled brain simulation pilots ($T=64$) we use median-filter width 7, $\kappa=2.5$, and clamp $[r_{\min},r_{\max}]=[1,32]$. The filtered residual is
\[
  C_{\mathrm{res}} := \bigl(I-U_rU_r\Hh\bigr)\,C,
\]
and the baseline score is the same PD flow-evidence statistic used elsewhere, $S_{\mathrm{base}}(y,x)=\frac{1}{T}\sum_t |X_{\mathrm{res}}(t,y,x)|^2$ after reshaping $C_{\mathrm{res}}$ back to cube form. This baseline is implemented as \path{sim/kwave/common.py:_baseline_pd_svd_similarity}.

\subsection{Block-wise local SVD baseline (overlap-add reconstruction)}
\label{app:baseline-local-svd}
To address reviewer objections that \emph{local} adaptive SVD clutter suppression is already standard in small-vessel ultrasound imaging, we also include a block-wise local SVD clutter filter in the style of Song \emph{et al.}\ \cite{SongBlockwiseLocal2017}. We tile the spatial grid into overlapping blocks $\{\Omega_i\}$ (block size and stride frozen per regime) and form local Casorati matrices $C_i\in\mathbb{C}^{T\times (h w)}$ by stacking slow-time vectors within each block. For each tile, we compute $C_i=U_i\Sigma_i V_i\Hh$ and select a tile-local clutter rank $r_i$ using a deterministic, frozen energy-fraction rule:
\[
  r_i := \min\left\{r:\frac{\sum_{k=1}^r \sigma_{i,k}^2}{\sum_{k=1}^{T}\sigma_{i,k}^2}\ge e\right\},
\]
where $e\in(0,1)$ is fixed. For the labeled brain simulation pilots we use blocks $8\times 8$, stride 3, $e=0.90$, and Hann overlap-add weighting. The local residual is $C_{i,\mathrm{res}}=(I-U_{i,r_i}U_{i,r_i}\Hh)C_i$, reshaped back to a block cube. We reconstruct a full-resolution residual cube by overlap-add with a separable Hann weight inside each block and compute the baseline score as the PD statistic on the reconstructed residual. This baseline is implemented as \path{sim/kwave/common.py:_baseline_pd_local_svd}.

\subsection{Adaptive-local SVD baseline (tilewise similarity-drop rank selection)}
\label{app:baseline-adaptive-local-svd}
For the stricter SIMUS fairness study we additionally include an adaptive-local SVD baseline that combines the two literature ideas above rather than treating them as mutually exclusive. As in the Song-style local baseline, we tile the spatial grid into overlapping blocks $\{\Omega_i\}$, form local Casorati matrices $C_i\in\mathbb{C}^{T\times(hw)}$, and reconstruct by overlap-add with optional Hann weighting \cite{SongBlockwiseLocal2017}. However, instead of a fixed tilewise energy threshold, each tile chooses its clutter rank from the same Baranger-style spatial singular-vector similarity-drop heuristic used by the adaptive-global baseline \cite{BarangerAdaptiveSVD2018}. Concretely, for each tile we compute $C_i=U_i\Sigma_iV_i\Hh$, form tile-local spatial singular-vector similarities
\[
  s_{i,k} := \frac{\langle |v_{i,k}|,\ |v_{i,k+1}|\rangle}{\| |v_{i,k}|\|\ \| |v_{i,k+1}|\|},
\]
smooth $\{s_{i,k}\}$ with the same short median filter used in the global adaptive baseline, and choose
\[
  r_i := \min \left\{k : s_{i,k} < \mathrm{median}(s_i) - \kappa\cdot \mathrm{MAD}(s_i)\right\},
\]
with $r_i$ clamped to a frozen range $[r_{\min}, r_{\max}]$. The local residual remains $C_{i,\mathrm{res}}=(I-U_{i,r_i}U_{i,r_i}\Hh)C_i$, followed by overlap-add reconstruction and PD scoring on the residual cube. This baseline is implemented as \path{sim/kwave/common.py:_baseline_pd_adaptive_local_svd}. In the benchmark tables we report the three SVD-family baselines separately as \emph{Adaptive Global SVD}, \emph{Local SVD (Fixed Energy)}, and \emph{Adaptive Local SVD}.

\subsection{Matched-subspace detector temporal core (full)}
For k-Wave pilots and replay bundles, the reference temporal matched-subspace detector implementation follows the same conceptual decomposition as the main text: tile extraction and optional sliding-window temporal embedding (Hankel construction) in slow time; per-tile covariance estimation with a robust estimator (Tyler-type or Huber); diagonal loading to enforce conditioning targets; whitening; and band-energy scoring on a fixed Doppler tone grid. Deployment-oriented variants can (i) cap effective training support by subsampling training snapshots and (ii) reduce computation by skipping the matched-subspace stage on tiles with negligible proxy flow support, reverting to the baseline score there. Exact run settings and exported metadata used for the experiments in this manuscript are listed in the reproducibility manifest (\SuppOrApp{app:repro}).

\paragraph{CUDA acceleration strategy (batched GPU implementation).}
For the latency results reported here we implement the matched-subspace stage as a GPU-batched pipeline: tiles are extracted on device using tensor \emph{unfold} operators, processed in batches to amortize per-tile overhead, and stitched back to full-resolution maps by overlap-add. This hardware-aware formulation aligns with prior work on GPU eigen-based clutter filtering and randomized/approximate SVD acceleration for real-time Doppler processing \cite{CheeGPUEigen2017,LokRandomizedSVD2020}. Within each batch, the temporal core is expressed as batched linear algebra (sliding-window temporal embedding; Hankel matrix construction; robust covariance estimation; whitening; and band-energy scoring), with rearrangements that favor GEMM-dominated workloads on GPUs. For fixed input shapes, the batched core can optionally be captured as a CUDA graph and replayed to reduce kernel-launch overhead; the first processed window/frame therefore includes one-time GPU overheads and is excluded from reported steady-state means.

\noindent The final latency configuration reported in the main paper uses only exact-output-preserving implementation optimizations. In particular, the implementation reuses cached geometry-only overlap counts, defers redundant raw-baseline PD generation in the short-ensemble phantom path, omits nonessential per-tile diagnostics after extracting the quantities required by the reported detector hierarchy, and applies an exact zero-shift registration bypass when the replayed window has no estimated motion. These changes reduce bookkeeping and launch overhead without changing exported detector scores, routing masks, or baseline maps. Exact commands and runtime settings used for the CUDA latency tables are listed in the reproducibility manifest (\SuppOrApp{app:repro}).

\subsection{Covariance blending constraints and shrink-only regularizer modes}

We permit anisotropic shrinkage, but only \emph{within} bands. In practice we consider two shrink-only regularizer paths:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Updated (commuting band--scalar).} $Q\Hh Q$ is forced to be bandwise scalar with target gains $(s_f,s_a,s_o)$ (typical targets: $s_f=s_o\approx 1$, $s_a\approx 1.5$), small mixing, and no off--block priors; the content of $R_0$ beyond $(\Pf,\Pa)$ is ignored.
  \item \textbf{Blend.} A trace--normalized blend $R_\beta = (1-\beta) R_t^{(n)} + \beta R_0^{(n)}$ with a small diagonal lift. In practice $R_0$ is overridden to a band--scalar form (Pf weight 1, Pa weight $\gamma_a>1$, Po weight 1) so the geometry is explicit; $\beta$ is treated as a fixed hyperparameter per regime.
\end{itemize}
Both modes enforce bandwise structure; there are no off--block priors.

\subsection{CB--1: Band--Block Blending}

We construct
\[
R_\beta := (1-\beta)\,\wh R + \beta \bigl(R_{0,f}\oplus R_{0,a}\oplus R_{0,o}\bigr) + \delta I,
\]
where $R_{0,f} = \Pf R_0 \Pf$, etc., for some prior $R_0$. There are no off--block priors; $\beta\in[0,1)$ and $\delta\ge 0$.

Interpretation: this structure makes $Q$ nearly block--diagonal in $(\Po,\Pf,\Pg,\Pa)$ while still allowing bandwise control to realize OC--2.

\subsection{CB--2: Bandwise Spectral Targets}

We choose $(\beta,\delta)$ to enforce OC--2 explicitly:
\[
\lambda_{\min}(Q\Hh Q|_{\Pf}) \in [s_f^2(1-\delta_f), s_f^2(1+\delta_f)],\quad\text{etc.}
\]
with design tolerances $\delta_\bullet\le 0.1$ and separation $\Lambda = s_a/s_f \ge 1.25$.

\subsection{CB--3: Bandwise Condition Number Safeguard}

We constrain bandwise conditioning:
\[
\kappa(R_\beta|_{\Pf}) \le \kappa_f^{\max},\qquad
\kappa(R_\beta|_{\Pa}) \le \kappa_a^{\max},
\]
avoid an incompatibility between conditioning and preserving flow Rayleigh quotients.

\subsection{Localization (restricting shrink-only regularizer in score space)}
\label{app:gating}

A global band--constant transform with $Q$ cannot improve ROC if applied everywhere (it is close to a scalar reweighting of BR/GLRT). To create re--ranking, the shrink-only regularizer must be \emph{localized} to tiles where:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item scores are dominated by alias/undesired components,
  \item positives are unlikely to lie.
\end{itemize}

We define localization based on a combination of alias metrics, flow coverage, depth, PD, and registration. This supplements G--1/G--2 with a generalized alias score $m_{\mathrm{alias}}$ from Section~\ref{sec:regime}.
In plain language, the optional penalty layer is allowed to act only in tiles that look clutter-heavy and not vessel-dominant; otherwise it stays off and leaves the detector score unchanged.

\subsection{G--1: Alias--dominant tile selection}

Given a baseline tile $z$, compute per--band energies $(E_o,E_f,E_g,E_a)$ and a generalized alias score $m_{\mathrm{alias}}(z)$ satisfying R--2.

\paragraph{Data--adaptive threshold.}
For BG tiles (or their proxy), collect $m_{\mathrm{alias}}(z)$ and define
\[
\tau_{\mathrm{alias}} := Q_{q_{\mathrm{alias}}}\bigl(m_{\mathrm{alias}} \mid H_0\bigr),
\]
where $Q_q$ is the $q$--quantile and $q_{\mathrm{alias}}\in[0.7,0.9]$ is a design choice (typical: $0.8$ or $0.9$).

Define an alias gate
\[
g_{\mathrm{alias}}(z) = \mathbf{1}\left\{ m_{\mathrm{alias}}(z) \ge \tau_{\mathrm{alias}} \right\}.
\]

\paragraph{Band--edge purity.}
Additionally require that guard energy is small relative to Pf+Pa:
\[
\frac{E_g}{E_o + E_f + E_a + E_g} \le \eta_{\text{edge}},\quad
\eta_{\text{edge}}\le 0.1.
\]

Interpretation: this gate selects the top tail of alias--heavy tiles among negatives, while protecting against band--edge contamination. The distribution of $m_{\mathrm{alias}}$ across classes is governed by R--2; empirical validation (Section~\ref{sec:regime}) must show a nontrivial difference between $H_0$ and $H_1$.

\subsection{G--2: Flow coherence protection}

We can optionally compute a flow coherence metric $\mathrm{FPC}$ (first principal component fraction) within the flow band:
\[
\mathrm{FPC} := \frac{\lambda_1(\wh S_f)}{\tr(\wh S_f)},\qquad
\wh S_f := \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K u_k u_k\Hh,\quad
u_k := \Pf z^{(k)},
\]
where $z^{(k)}$ are projected slow--time vectors across DPSS tapers or within a local ensemble.

Gate \emph{may} require
\[
\mathrm{FPC}\le c_{\text{pc}},\qquad c_{\text{pc}}\in[0.4,0.6].
\]

Interpretation: flow tiles tend to exhibit higher coherence in Pf; when enabled, we suppress penalization when $\mathrm{FPC}$ is large, thereby reducing positive penalization probability $p_+$. Alias/motion tiles have lower flow coherence and pass this condition. In the current runs this check is typically left off; coverage and depth localization carry most of the class separation.

\subsection{G--2': Flow coverage protection}

Let $c_f$ be the tile--wise flow coverage fraction:
\[
c_f := \frac{\#\{\text{pixels labeled as flow}\}}{\#\{\text{pixels in tile}\}},
\]
using a label--free proxy such as a PD--derived flow mask.

We require
\[
c_f \le c_{f,\max},\qquad c_{f,\max}\in[0.1,0.2],
\]
and optionally $c_f \ge c_{f,\min}$ to avoid pure background noise tiles (typical $c_{f,\min}\approx 0$).

Interpretation: we prefer penalization on tiles with \emph{low to moderate} flow coverage. This is the regime where alias/motion dominates and where down--scaling scores is most beneficial. Dense flow tiles are either left to the matched-subspace detector alone or are protected via the flow coherence check. (In the current implementation, coverage is treated as an \emph{upper} bound; missing coverage defaults to ``pass'' in this test.)

\subsection{G--3: Baseline score quantile window}

Let $S_{\text{base}}(z)$ be the baseline BR/GLRT score. Within a given depth stratum, compute score quantiles $q_{0.3},q_{0.95}$ over BG tiles. Gate requires
\[
S_{\text{base}}(z)\in [q_{0.3},q_{0.95}].
\]

Interpretation: avoid penalization on the noise floor (very low scores) and on already high--confidence positives (very high scores). This focuses the shrink-only regularizer on the part of the negative tail that matters near the target FPR.

\subsection{G--4: Registration quality (optional)}

If motion compensation / registration is used, include a registration quality metric (e.g.\ PSR) and require
\[
\mathrm{PSR}_{\text{reg}}\le \text{PSR}_{\max},
\]
or any similar condition ensuring that tiles with gross registration failure are handled separately.

\subsection{Offline validation of gating}

On a held--out labeled configuration, define
\[
p_+ := \mathbb{P}\{g=1\mid H_1\},\qquad
p_- := \mathbb{P}\{g=1\mid H_0\},
\]
for the combined gate $g$ that incorporates G--1, G--2, G--2', G--3, G--4. Require:
\[
p_-\ge 0.2,\quad p_- \le 0.5,\quad p_+ \le 0.1 p_-.
\]

Interpretation: a nontrivial fraction of negatives are gated (but not too many), while positives are rarely gated. This condition is central to the SL--2 quantile shift argument.

\subsection{Summary: Compact Feasibility Checklist (Updated)}

The combination of matched-subspace scoring and the shrink-only regularizer can be expected to outperform MC--SVD (or matched-subspace-only scoring) in the low-FPR regime if and only if:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.,nosep]
  \item \textbf{Regime preconditions.} Band occupancy R--1, alias separation R--2, and baseline headroom R--3 are satisfied on a held--out validation set.
  \item \textbf{Band design.} BG--1/BG--2/BG--3 hold for the chosen PRF/$T$/DPSS; Pf/Pa are physically meaningful (e.g.\ Pf $\approx$ 30--250 Hz, Pa $\gtrsim$ 400 Hz in brain fUS).
  \item \textbf{Operator geometry.} OC--1 with $\varepsilon_{\text{mix}}\le 0.05$ and OC--2 with $\Lambda = s_a/s_f \ge 1.25$ and $\delta_\bullet\le 0.1$.
  \item \textbf{Blending realizable.} CB--1/CB--2/CB--3 satisfied with chosen $(\beta,\delta)$.
  \item \textbf{Gating conservative and class--differential.} G--1/G--2/G--2'/G--3/G--4 implemented; offline, $p_-\in[0.2,0.5]$ and $p_+\le 0.1p_-$.
  \item \textbf{Regime sentinel.} RS--1 does not trigger (the shrink-only regularizer is not out of regime).
  \item \textbf{Invariance sentinel.} $\wh s_a/\wh s_f \notin[1-\epsilon,1+\epsilon]$ with $\epsilon\le 0.02$; otherwise shrink-only regularizer is effectively scalar and can be skipped.
  \item \textbf{Safety guard.} Safety--1 (monotonicity on gated flow tiles) passes.
  \item \textbf{Evaluation discipline.} GLRT controls and tail CI practices (see \ifSUPPLEMENTONLY the main paper\else Section~\ref{sec:eval}\fi) in place for fair low--FPR comparison.
\end{enumerate}

When all of the above hold, the shrink-only regularizer applies a non--mixing, alias--inflating band transform chiefly to alias--dominant tiles, pushing negative scores down while largely leaving positives unchanged. This lowers the negative $\alpha$--quantile and increases TPR at fixed small FPR in a manner consistent with the SL--2 quantile shift analysis.
