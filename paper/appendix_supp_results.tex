\section{Supplementary Results}\label{app:supp-results}

This appendix collects additional dataset audits and diagnostic panels that support the main paper's methodology, but are not required for the primary labeled low-FPR detection claims.

\begin{center}
\fbox{\begin{minipage}{0.95\linewidth}
\textbf{Evaluation discipline (summary).}
Quantitative ROC claims are restricted to regimes with independent labels (simulator truth from the labeled brain simulations; Gammex B-mode structural masks).
Operating points are reported under prespecified settings by selecting thresholds on labeled (or structurally labeled) $H_0$ sets at target FPR and evaluating TPR on the corresponding $H_1$ set; the empirically supported floor $fpr_{\min}=1/n_{\mathrm{neg}}$ is reported.
Uncertainty is quantified at the natural unit of variation (windows for the labeled brain simulations; frames for cine acquisitions) rather than treating pooled pixels as independent samples.
Conditional execution is treated as a changed method and is reported only with explicit leakage controls.
For Twinkling sequences, PRF is fixed once per sequence family using source-paper values where available and explicit frozen overrides otherwise; band-discretization sentinels force conservative behavior when discretized bands are invalid.
When the shrink-only regularizer is enabled, it preserves a protected do-not-penalize set (scores unchanged on high-confidence flow locations); labeled ROC reporting uses the pre-regularizer matched-subspace score.
\end{minipage}}
\end{center}

\paragraph{Supplement roadmap.}
\noindent The supplementary material is organized as supporting evidence for the main paper, with structurally labeled and real-IQ audits first, optional adaptive and shrink-only-regularizer diagnostics next, and archival fairness or repository-style material later.

\subsection{Parameter sensitivity (Open-Skull Pilot)}
\label{app:brain-openskull-profile-sensitivity}
Because the detector relies on covariance whitening, a natural concern is sensitivity to conditioning and geometry choices. We therefore vary one parameter at a time around the prespecified open-skull setting: covariance diagonal loading $\lambda$ (conditioning strength), tile size (with stride chosen to preserve overlap), and slow-time aperture $L_t$. Each point reports within-window TPR medians (IQR) over five disjoint 64-frame windows at fixed strict-tail FPR targets $\alpha\in\{10^{-4},3\times 10^{-4},10^{-3}\}$ under the same baseline and mask protocol as the main labeled brain simulation tables. In this pilot, performance is effectively insensitive to $\lambda$ over the tested range, shows modest dependence on tile geometry, and is strongly sensitive to under-sized $L_t$ (e.g.\ $L_t=6$ collapses strict-tail TPR), consistent with the need for adequate slow-time support to estimate and invert $R_i$.

\subsection{Sensitivity of the finalized adaptive and shrink-only regularizer thresholds}
\label{app:param-threshold-sensitivity}
An obvious overfitting concern is that the adaptive guard threshold $\tau_g$ and the shrink-only regularizer flow-support proxy threshold $c_{\mathrm{flow}}$ might have been tuned so tightly that small perturbations would materially change the reported detector behavior. We therefore perturb each finalized threshold by $\pm 10\%$ while leaving the rest of the pipeline fixed.

For the adaptive variant, Table~\ref{tab:adaptive_tau_sensitivity} evaluates the frozen tile-mean guard threshold around the deployed value on representative regimes spanning labeled brain stress tests, a structurally labeled phantom, and the Shin real-IQ corroboration. For the shrink-only regularizer, Table~\ref{tab:ka_cflow_sensitivity} recomputes the shrink-only calculi audit after perturbing $c_{\mathrm{flow}}$ by $\pm 10\%$ on the same frozen bundles. In both cases the behavior changes smoothly rather than collapsing: the labeled-brain TPRs are unchanged at the reported precision, the real-IQ and phantom endpoints shift modestly, and the calculi audit preserves both the regularizer activation pattern and exact protected-set invariance.

\subsection{Conditional execution leakage ablation (Open-Skull Pilot)}
Conditional execution (compute gating) skips matched-subspace detector outside a flow-proxy region and falls back to the baseline score. Because this changes the overall decision rule, we evaluate it explicitly under leakage controls by varying only the \emph{gating} mask: (i) a proxy mask computed from the same scored window, (ii) a proxy mask carried from a disjoint window, and (iii) a randomized mask with matched coverage. Table~\ref{tab:condstap_leakage_ablation} reports the corresponding change in TPR relative to full matched-subspace detector at fixed low-FPR targets.

\subsection{ULM 7883227: localization-derived structural-label audit on real in-vivo brain IQ}
To obtain a stronger anatomical reference on the same open ULM dataset, we use the published PALA rat-brain ULM example rather than the earlier local-density surrogate \cite{ChavignonZenodo7883227,HeilesULMBenchmark2021}. The construction is deterministic and frozen. We reproduce the corresponding short-horizon PALA PowDop rendering from the Zenodo IQ archive using the published odd-block selection and SVD cutoff, register that published PowDop crop back to the Zenodo IQ field of view, and then downsample the published super-resolution MatOut vascular rendering onto the short-window evaluation lattice. Vessel pixels are defined as a peak-based core mask on that registered localization map, and background pixels are defined as the immediately surrounding shell. This creates a bounded but genuinely localization-derived vessel-core versus perivascular-shell endpoint on real in-vivo brain IQ, with labels derived from microbubble localization microscopy rather than from the Doppler detector being evaluated. The one-time PALA-to-IQ registration is used only to construct the labels; the frozen short-window detector itself runs without additional frame-to-frame registration.

The completed 10-block / 20-window audit uses the fully whitened matched-subspace specialist because that is the strongest short-window head on the corrected vessel-core versus perivascular-shell endpoint. On short 64-frame windows, the specialist reaches AUC $0.526$ [0.514, 0.536], compared with 0.503 [0.493, 0.513] for PD and 0.502 [0.492, 0.512] for Kasai. At matched 70\% vessel-core recall, the same specialist slightly lowers perivascular-shell false positives from 0.701 [0.687, 0.715] for PD and 0.704 [0.689, 0.717] for Kasai to 0.690 [0.670, 0.710]. The effect is bounded and modest in absolute magnitude, but it is directionally consistent. This detector choice is also endpoint-specific: the corrected PALA-backed structural audit rewards concentration on the localization-derived vascular core relative to the immediately adjacent shell, whereas the held-out SIMUS nuisance-control benchmark favors the stronger fixed downstream robustness of the simpler matched-subspace default. A direct latency check on the same frozen 64-frame no-registration configuration yields 41.3~ms steady-state total on an RTX~4080 SUPER for a 64~ms acquisition budget. This is still a bounded same-acquisition structural audit using a localization-derived vascular reference rather than independent multimodal validation, and it should be interpreted as a structural vascular-fidelity result rather than as a functional-activation or hemodynamic-recovery benchmark.

\subsection{Mac{\'e} whole-brain fUS: hemodynamic band-energy statistics and hold-out check}
We treat the Mac{\'e}/Urban whole-brain dataset as a PD-only, open-skull analogue of the open-skull brain pilot and compute tile-level hemodynamic band-energy statistics with fixed bands $\Pf=[0,0.5]$~Hz, guard $[0.5,1.0]$~Hz, and $\Pa=[1.0,3.0]$~Hz on $8\times 8$ tiles (stride 3). Offline we also compute atlas-derived \emph{region strata} (e.g.\ VIS/SC/LGd versus CP/CA/DG) for descriptive summaries; these strata are not treated as ground-truth flow labels and are not used as shrink-only regularizer inputs. Figure~\ref{fig:mace_phase2_summary} summarizes a systematic sweep in ``Pf occupancy vs alias separation'' space. We provide an explicit atlas-alignment sanity check in \SuppOrApp{app:mace-atlas-align}.
Here PD-z denotes a per-tile change-from-baseline z-score on the PD time series (distinct from the Doppler/IQ PD-mode score maps $S_{\mathrm{PD}}$ exported by the matched-subspace processing pipeline).

\paragraph{Hold-out discipline (avoid plane selection bias and label-tuned thresholds).}
To address the selection-bias critique and to avoid per-plane PD-z thresholds chosen using atlas strata (e.g.\ setting thresholds to force a fixed activation fraction in a preferred stratum), we add a scan-level hold-out evaluation (reproducibility details in \SuppOrApp{app:repro}). We (i) define label-free proxy sets from a mean-PD vascularity mask and set a per-plane PD-z operating point by a fixed background-proxy tail rule $\mathbb{P}\{\mathrm{PD}\text{-}z\ge\tau\mid \mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{bg}}\}=\alpha_{\mathrm{pd}}$ (no atlas labels), (ii) define the alias suppression threshold as a fixed background-proxy quantile of $m_{\mathrm{alias}}=\log((E_{\mathrm{Pa}}+\epsilon)/(E_{\mathrm{Pf}}+\epsilon))$, and (iii) select Pf-rich planes in held-out scans using a label-free band-energy statistic (Pf-peak fraction on flow-proxy tiles), with the selection threshold fixed from the training scans. Table~\ref{tab:mace_holdout_alias_gate} summarizes the resulting held-out counts on \texttt{scan4}--\texttt{scan5} (training: \texttt{scan1/2} and \texttt{scan3/6}; note \texttt{scan2} duplicates \texttt{scan1} and \texttt{scan6} duplicates \texttt{scan3} in this loader). This appendix-only check is intended as a conservative retrospective sanity check on threshold discipline, not as a primary efficacy result.

On held-out scans (\texttt{scan4}--\texttt{scan5}), this fixed, shrink-only alias suppression reduces proxy activations in the CP/CA/DG stratum while only modestly affecting activations in the VIS/SC/LGd stratum at the chosen label-free operating point. At $\alpha_{\mathrm{pd}}=0.01$ on the background proxy, aggregate (VIS/SC/LGd / CP/CA/DG hit counts) change from $23/142$ to $22/121$ (VIS/SC/LGd retention $\approx 0.96$, CP/CA/DG reduction $=21$ counts). On the Pf-peak-selected subset of held-out planes (PfPeak$_{\mathrm{flow}}\ge\tau_{\mathrm{sel}}$), only two planes pass selection and the descriptive counts are $19/0 \rightarrow 19/0$; this subset therefore supports the no-peeking selection discipline and perfect retained hits on the selected held-out planes, but it is too small to support a separate false-positive reduction claim. We interpret the hold-out result as a conservative safety-regularization behavior rather than an efficacy claim: Mac{\'e} is PD-only and provides no Doppler-side flow labels, so its primary role here is to validate the band-energy/proxy machinery and the no-peeking threshold discipline.

\subsection{Shin RatBrain Fig3 (LOCA-ULM): real kHz IQ translation and shrink-only regularizer diagnostics}
\label{sec:shin-ratbrain}

We use the LOCA-ULM RatBrain Fig3 dataset released with Shin et al.\ as a real kHz IQ audit of end-to-end ingest, band-energy summaries, and shrink-only regularizer behavior \cite{ShinLOCAULM2024,ShinZenodo10711806}. This is a microbubble ULM IQ dataset, so we do not treat it as a physiological fUS benchmark. Instead, we use it to ask a narrower translational question: can the full pipeline run reproducibly on real beamformed IQ, and do the resulting score maps behave sensibly under label-free proxy checks? The Fig3 archive contains 80 clips (\texttt{IQData001.dat}--\texttt{IQData080.dat}) and a \texttt{SizeInfo.dat} file describing the tensor shape. Each clip stores $T=250$ slow-time frames on a $(238\times 130)$ spatial grid as little-endian float64 arrays containing I then Q in column-major order (matching the authors' reference MATLAB loader); the effective slow-time sampling is $1$~kHz (post-compounding), with center frequency $f_0\approx 15.6$~MHz \cite{ShinLOCAULM2024}. Because there are no independent flow/background labels, we do not report ROC improvement here. Instead, we report label-free proxy separation, prior-regime stability, shrink-only invariants, and motion-robustness summaries.

\paragraph{Processing workflow.}
We mirror the authors' loader and convert IQ clips into a common analysis format used throughout this work (reproduction details in \SuppOrApp{app:repro}). For a chosen clip and time window we:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Apply an SVD clutter filter on the Casorati matrix $X\in\mathbb{C}^{T\times HW}$ to form a baseline PD-derived map $pd_{\mathrm{base}}$ and a residual IQ cube (the input to matched-subspace detector).
  \item Construct pixel-level proxy masks from $pd_{\mathrm{base}}$ (used here as a conventional PD-brightness proxy $\mathrm{PD}^{\uparrow}$ distinct from the ROC score maps): a flow/vessel proxy (top quantile; minimum pixel count) and a background proxy (complement of the flow mask in a central crop).
  \item Run the temporal matched-subspace detector core on the residual IQ cube with fixed tile geometry ($8\times 8$ tiles, stride 3) and a longer slow-time aperture $L_t=64$ suited to $1$~kHz slow time (bin spacing $15.6$~Hz), producing a pre-regularizer PD-mode score map.
  \item Apply the shrink-only regularizer (\ifSUPPLEMENTONLY described in the main paper\else Section~\ref{sec:ka-contract-v2}\fi) to produce a region index, reason label, and (when active) a non-increasing post-regularizer score map together with gate/scale maps.
  \item Record tilewise band-energy statistics (peak frequency, guard fraction, alias/guard risk metrics) and the penalization maps in the same analysis format used throughout this work.
\end{itemize}
The proxy masks are used only for label-free proxying/protection; prior-regime decisions and hygiene/robustness proxies follow the same right-tail score convention used elsewhere in this manuscript: operating points are expressed as a threshold on a right-tail PD score map, i.e.\ $S_{\mathrm{PD}}\ge\tau$ at fixed background-proxy tail rate.

\emph{Scale-map convention.} When the shrink-only prior is active ($w<1$ on a nontrivial set), we record an equivalent per-pixel scale factor $\gamma(i)\ge 1$ and apply it to the PD score map as
\[
\mathrm{PD}_{\mathrm{post}}(i)=\mathrm{PD}_{\mathrm{pre}}(i)/\gamma(i),\qquad \gamma(i)\ge 1,
\]
which makes the PD score map non-increasing on penalized regions. For any fixed threshold $\tau$ this is a pure shrink-only penalty; when thresholds are recalibrated post-regularizer to match a target background tail rate, any TPR change reflects background-tail suppression rather than score inflation.

\paragraph{Shin operating settings and one-time baseline calibration.}
To avoid retuning the main brain-Doppler setting to Shin until the results look favorable, we treat Shin as its own prespecified operating regime and calibrate only the baseline clutter filter once. We sweep the MC--SVD energy-fraction cutoff over a calibration subset (10 representative clips; window $0{:}128$) and select a single baseline setting based on band-energy integrity and stability, not on apparent shrink-only regularizer activity. Table~\ref{tab:shin_baseline_sweep} reports the corresponding prior-regime counts for transparency, but selection is driven by the label-free integrity and background-tail metrics ($\Delta_{bf}$, $\Delta_{\mathrm{tail}}$) together with overall stability. Overly aggressive baseline removal increases Pf peak occupancy but can invert the proxy-separation signal and suppress the more selective prior regime. We therefore freeze MC--SVD with energy fraction $\approx 0.97$ as a balanced setting for the Shin-U configuration (reproducibility details in \SuppOrApp{app:repro}).

\paragraph{Baseline comparisons (proxy separability on real IQ).}
Although Shin provides no independent $H_1/H_0$ labels, it is useful to test whether the matched-subspace detector core improves \emph{proxy} separation on real complex IQ.
We run a fixed scenario matrix on 10 clips and three overlapping windows ($0{:}128$, $64{:}192$, $122{:}250$), exporting right-tail score maps for MC--SVD, RPCA, HOSVD, matched-subspace detector (on the MC--SVD residual), and an \emph{matched-subspace-only} ablation that runs matched-subspace detector directly on raw/registered IQ.
Proxy masks are derived deterministically from the MC--SVD baseline PD map via a \texttt{pd\_auto} quantile rule (0.995 quantile within depth fraction $[0.25,0.85]$ with dilation), with the generic default-mask union disabled; masks are then held fixed across methods.
We report (i) AUC between flow-proxy and background-proxy score distributions (threshold-free), and (ii) flow-proxy hit rate at matched background tail rates $\alpha\in\{10^{-1},10^{-2},3\times 10^{-3},10^{-3},3\times 10^{-4},10^{-4}\}$ (Figure~\ref{fig:shin_baseline_matrix_proxy_metrics}; Table~\ref{tab:shin_baseline_matrix_proxy_summary}).
In this setup $n_{\mathrm{bg}}\approx 2.6\times 10^4$ per scenario ($fpr_{\min}\approx 3.8\times 10^{-5}$), so $\alpha=10^{-4}$ corresponds to roughly $2$--$3$ background pixels.

Across scenarios, matched-subspace detector improves proxy separation (median AUC 0.951 vs 0.922 for MC--SVD) and yields nonzero flow-proxy hit rates at strict tails where MC--SVD is typically zero (e.g.\ median hit rate 0.070 at $\alpha=10^{-3}$).
The matched-subspace-only ablation is comparable to matched-subspace detector-on-residual, indicating that the localized matched-subspace detector core is the primary driver of the improvement on this dataset.
At moderate strictness ($\alpha\approx 10^{-2}$), RPCA/HOSVD can exhibit higher proxy hit rates than matched-subspace detector despite lower AUC, consistent with a broader, lower-contrast score elevation rather than strict-tail selectivity.
Finally, a conditioning-target sweep ($\kappa_{\mathrm{target}}=80$ vs 200) produced identical matched-subspace detector score maps under this frozen profile, indicating that the conditioning target is non-binding in this particular configuration.

\paragraph{Profile variants (Shin-U vs Shin-S/L).}
In addition to Shin-U, we evaluated two alternative frozen band settings (Shin-S and Shin-L) designed to push the ``flow'' band Pf toward lower frequencies and increase Pf peak occupancy. Across the same 10-clip calibration subset, these variants typically increase Pf-peak(flow) but weaken the label-free background-tail evidence that would justify a more selective prior response. We therefore treat Shin-S/L as conservative alternatives and use Shin-U as the primary real-IQ audit setting reported here.

\paragraph{Shrink-only regularizer behavior, stability, and hygiene endpoints.}
Under the frozen Shin-U setting (MC--SVD energy fraction $\approx 0.97$, matched-subspace detector $L_t=64$, fixed Pf/Pa bands, and shrink-only regularizer enabled in \texttt{auto} mode), we run the pipeline on 10 pre-selected clips (\texttt{IQData001}--\texttt{IQData005}, \texttt{IQData010}, \texttt{IQData020}, \texttt{IQData040}, \texttt{IQData060}, \texttt{IQData080}) and evaluate stability across three overlapping time windows ($0{:}128$, $64{:}192$, $122{:}250$). Because Pf realization is weak on this dataset, Shin is not treated as a setting where shrink-only regularizer should deliver measurable performance improvement. Instead, we use it to test determinism, shrink-only invariants, and label-free map-hygiene summaries. Because there are no labels, we report only label-free endpoints (reproducibility details in \SuppOrApp{app:repro}):
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Regularizer regime stability.} The conservative prior regime dominates in all windows (8--10 of 10 clips), while the more selective regime is rare (0--2 clips); all decisions emit auditable reason labels.
  \item \textbf{Shrink-only and protected invariance.} With the shrink-only regularizer active, the map receives a mild, localized shrink (median $p_{\mathrm{shrink}}\approx 0.09$; median scaled pixel fraction $\approx 0.20$) and preserves the protected set exactly (maximum absolute PD change on protected pixels is $0$ in all evaluated clips).
  \item \textbf{Background-tail hygiene.} A label-free background-tail metric shows consistent tail compression on early windows: for each clip/window we choose a threshold $\tau$ so that the background proxy satisfies $\mathbb{P}\{S_{\mathrm{PD}}\ge\tau\mid \text{bg proxy}\}=10^{-3}$ on the pre-regularizer map, then count clusters/area of pixels satisfying $S_{\mathrm{PD}}\ge\tau$ before vs.\ after shrink-only regularizer. Total background-tail clusters decrease from $125\rightarrow 106$ (window $0{:}128$) and $121\rightarrow 104$ (window $64{:}192$), while the late window is closer to neutral ($129\rightarrow 124$). We interpret this as improved map hygiene rather than as a detection-improvement claim.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Synthetic motion robustness sweeps (label-free).}
To probe motion robustness on real IQ without ground-truth labels, we implement controlled motion-injection sweeps on Shin IQ cubes (reproduction details in \SuppOrApp{app:repro}). In addition to simple per-frame subpixel translations, global phase modulation, and spatially smooth low-frequency clutter tones, we define a \emph{brain-like} motion model designed to be mechanistically comparable to the structured-clutter pilot: a bulk rigid translation (correctable by phase-correlation registration) plus a smooth, depth-decaying elastic warp with temporally correlated coefficients (not removable by translation-only registration). Motion is applied directly to the beamformed IQ cube (a stress test rather than a full scatterer-level simulator), after which we rerun the same baseline and matched-subspace processing pipeline (the shrink-only regularizer disabled to isolate matched-subspace scoring).

We report two label-free robustness endpoints as functions of motion amplitude: (i) PD-mode score-map correlation versus the no-motion reference (after rigid alignment and a boundary crop), and (ii) a flow-proxy hit rate at a matched background-proxy tail operating point (background-proxy tail rate $=10^{-3}$), expressed in score space as $\mathbb{P}\{S_{\mathrm{PD}}\ge\tau\mid \text{bg proxy}\}=10^{-3}$. Concretely, for each clip/window we define a fixed pixel-level flow-proxy mask from the no-motion baseline PD-derived map $pd_{\mathrm{base}}$ (top quantile; minimum pixel count) and a background-proxy mask from its complement; for each run/method we choose $\tau$ on that run's background proxy and then report the fraction of flow-proxy pixels satisfying $S_{\mathrm{PD}}\ge\tau$. As a degeneracy check (to rule out ``stability by collapse''), the sweep also records non-collapse diagnostics (map std/entropy and background variance ratios). A post-hoc sensitivity sweep recomputes correlation under different crop margins and with alignment disabled.
Over 10 pre-selected clips and three overlapping time windows ($0{:}128$, $64{:}192$, $122{:}250$) under the frozen Shin-U profile (PRF $=1$~kHz, $L_t=64$, baseline MC--SVD with rigid registration enabled), matched-subspace score maps are consistently more stable than MC--SVD as motion increases: at a 2~px motion amplitude, median correlation improves from $\approx 0.18$--0.19 (MC--SVD) to $\approx 0.36$--0.45 (matched-subspace detector) across windows, and the flow-proxy hit rate improves from 0 to $\approx 0.04$ at bg tail rate $=10^{-3}$. \paragraph{Limitations and claim boundaries (Shin).}
The Shin RatBrain Fig3 analysis is intended as an end-to-end \emph{real-IQ translation check}, not a clinical detection benchmark.
Because there are no task labels and no ground-truth vessel segmentation, quantitative endpoints reported here are label-free proxies: baseline comparisons use deterministic flow/background proxy masks and report both threshold-free proxy separation (AUC) and tail-calibrated flow-proxy hit rates, while motion-sweep endpoints measure within-method consistency relative to each method's own no-motion reference and persistence of a fixed flow proxy under a matched background-proxy tail operating point.
In addition, the ``brain-like'' motion stress test is an image-domain warp applied to beamformed IQ (rigid + depth-decaying elastic components) rather than a scatterer-level forward model; it is designed to mimic the specific high-rank, spatially varying residual-motion mismatch represented in the structured-clutter pilot, not to reproduce full ultrasound physics.
Accordingly, we interpret Shin results as evidence of (i) deterministic real-IQ ingestion and band-energy summaries, (ii) shrink-only prior auditability and invariants (shrink-only; protected tiles unchanged), and (iii) self-consistency under controlled residual-motion corruption, while leaving ROC improvement claims to labeled simulation regimes and to future raw-IQ datasets with task or landmark structure.

\subsection{Corrected SIMUS/PyMUST moving-scatterer benchmark (frozen v1)}
\label{app:simus-frozen-benchmark}
To complement the deterministic labeled k-Wave brain simulations with a moving-scatterer forward model, we freeze a corrected SIMUS/PyMUST benchmark after fixing the SIMUS motion-scaling bug uncovered during the motion-ladder audit. At a high level, the bug mis-scaled some simulated motion amplitudes, so the absolute nuisance pressure in earlier exploratory motion-stress runs was not trustworthy. After correcting that path, we refroze the benchmark reported here and the narrower \emph{SIMUS-ClinUtility-v2} benchmark used in the main text. The protocol here is intentionally symmetric across methods: using corrected SIMUS cases from seeds 21--22 as a development split, we select one frozen configuration per method family, then evaluate those fixed choices on held-out corrected cases from seeds 23--24. The matched-subspace detector is scored with its stored pre-regularizer matched-subspace score, while baselines are scored with their native baseline maps. This broader corrected benchmark should be read as a moving-scatterer realism and fairness checkpoint, not as the canonical translational benchmark. The main paper's SIMUS claims are carried instead by the corrected \emph{SIMUS-ClinUtility-v2} results, the corrected held-out stress frontier, and the corrected fixed-calibration transfer audit. Any pre-correction SIMUS numbers from older drafts are superseded and should be ignored.

For readers who prefer a simple decomposition, the pipeline can be viewed in two stages. First, an upstream clutter-suppression method produces a residual slow-time cube. Second, a downstream score is computed on that residual. In this benchmark the upstream methods include global and adaptive-global SVD, local and adaptive-local SVD, RPCA, and HOSVD \cite{DemeneClutterSVD2015,BarangerAdaptiveSVD2018,SongBlockwiseLocal2017,CandesRPCA,OzgunByramApertureHOSVD2021,HuangAdaptiveHOSVD2024}. The downstream scores are conventional power Doppler or Kasai on the baseline side, and the matched-subspace score on the proposed side \cite{KasaiAutocorrelation1985,GuerciSTAPBook2003,WardSTAPTutorial}. Table~\ref{tab:simus_frozen_benchmark_v1p2} freezes one full processing chain per method family. Table~\ref{tab:simus_symmetric_pipeline_v1p2} then asks the stricter fairness question: if the upstream residual is held fixed and only the downstream score is changed, does matched-subspace scoring still help?

\paragraph{Clinically grounded stress frontier (held-out paper-tier SIMUS confirmation).}
One remaining fairness concern is that the held-out structural checkpoint may still be too clean: perfect or near-perfect summary metrics can invite the criticism that the task is trivial rather than that the detector is meaningfully better. To address that directly, we first ran a reduced-grid sweep on the same mobile scene family to identify the strongest residualizer families under two clinically motivated stress axes, then reran a held-out paper-tier confirmation restricted to those strongest families (RPCA and adaptive-global SVD). The two stress axes are closer to the nuisance mechanisms emphasized in clinical fUS reports than the earlier generic motion/reflectivity audit. The first is a cardiac-like 1.30~Hz low-frequency tissue-pulsation term whose amplitude is increased across reference, moderate, and hard rows; this is intended to stress the same tissue-motion regime highlighted by Demen\'e \emph{et al.} for SVD clutter filtering and by Baranger \emph{et al.} for neonatal/open-skull Doppler \cite{DemeneClutterSVD2015,BarangerAdaptiveSVD2018}. The second shortens the slow-time support from 64 frames to 48 and 32 frames to mimic clinically constrained acquisitions in which shorter ensembles are used to limit motion corruption or workflow burden \cite{Imbault2017,Soloukey2020FUSAwake,MobileFUS2025}. This section asks a narrower question than the main benchmark. It does not replace the full-resolution prespecified benchmark in the main text, and it is not framed as a new universal ranking of all methods. Instead, it asks whether the detector family remains preferable to strong public baseline stacks once the acquisition is pushed toward pulsatile tissue leakage or reduced slow-time support.

\subsection{Compact real-data translation audit}
\label{app:realdata-translation-audit}
To make the translational story less dependent on single point estimates, we summarize the existing real-data outputs with paired uncertainty and consistency summaries. On the Mac\'e whole-brain phase-2 sweep, label-free alias gating reduces matched-TPR false positives by 18.6\% (95\% CI 16.8--20.2) while retaining 81.0\% of tiles, and the plane-wise false-positive reduction win rate is 118/120. On the appendix-only held-out alias-gate check (\texttt{scan4}/\texttt{scan5}), false positives are reduced by 14.5\% (95\% CI 7.9--21.4) with a 97.5\% safety win rate across held-out planes. These readout-level results are retrospective and label-free, but they strengthen the claim that nuisance gating can improve whole-brain map hygiene without aggressive hit loss \cite{UrbanWholeBrain2015,Deffieux2021}.

For the same-residual scoring claim itself, the most direct real-data test is simple: hold the residual cube fixed and compare PD, Kasai, and matched-subspace detector on the same real-IQ bundle. Coverage is intentionally narrow and explicit. On currently available ULM 7883227 bundles, matched-subspace detector is uniformly positive relative to both PD and Kasai under the same residual, whereas the currently available Shin bundles remain mixed and should be read as a low-motion real-IQ audit rather than a superiority dataset \cite{ChavignonZenodo7883227,HeilesULMBenchmark2021,ShinLOCAULM2024,ShinZenodo10711806}.
