## 0) What “fully physical Doppler” should mean here (and what it doesn’t need to mean)

A **credible preprint-level physical Doppler simulation** (without in‑vivo) should satisfy:

1. **Slow-time phase evolution is a consequence of scatterer motion** under your acquisition PRF (not injected as a sinusoid at IQ).
2. **Spectral broadening** appears naturally (velocity distribution across a vessel, beam/PSF effects, motion decorrelation).
3. **Clutter is physical-ish**: tissue scatterers (static or moving) create DC/low‑freq dominance and leakage.
4. **Aliasing arises naturally** when (f_D > \mathrm{PRF}/2) (rather than injected “alias tone”).
5. You can produce **pixel-level ground truth labels** (flow vs background; optionally “aliased flow” vs “unaliased flow”).
6. You show a **sanity link**: simulated PSD summaries resemble at least one real dataset’s PSD distributions (your Fig. 3 defines exactly the kind of PSD partitioning you care about).

It **does not** need to be “CFD + viscoelastic tissue + nonlinear propagation” to be valuable at preprint stage. What matters is that the *things your detector exploits* (band energies, covariance structure, tail behavior) come from plausible physics, not from deterministic overlays.

## 3) Core physics you need (the conceptual model)

### Doppler mapping

For a scatterer moving with axial velocity (v_z), Doppler shift:
[
f_D = \frac{2 f_0}{c} v_z.
]
Equivalently:
[
v_z = \frac{c}{2 f_0} f_D.
]

Example using (f_0 = 7.5\text{ MHz}), (c = 1540\text{ m/s}):
[
\frac{c}{2 f_0} = \frac{1540}{2 \cdot 7.5\times 10^6} = \frac{1540}{15\times 10^6} \approx 1.0267\times 10^{-4}\ \text{m/Hz}
]
which is (0.10267\ \text{mm/s per Hz}).

So your current “Brain-OpenSkull overlay” band (60–180 Hz) corresponds to about:

* 60 Hz → (60 \times 0.10267 \approx 6.16) mm/s
* 180 Hz → (180 \times 0.10267 \approx 18.48) mm/s

And your alias tone 650 Hz corresponds to:

* (650 \times 0.10267 \approx 66.74) mm/s

That’s exactly the right order for “microvascular-ish” vs “near-Nyquist alias” regimes.

### What creates realistic spectral width (what overlays miss)

Broadening comes from:

* **velocity distribution** across vessel cross-section (Poiseuille profile),
* **vessel angle distribution** (axial component varies spatially),
* **finite PSF / beam pattern** (amplitude modulation as scatterers move),
* **tissue motion decorrelation** (clutter leakage into Pf/Pa),
* **noise** and **phase aberration**.

Deterministic tone overlays usually produce unrealistically narrow peaks unless you explicitly jitter them.

## 4) Implementation path A (recommended): Field II moving-scatterer simulator

Field II is almost always the right first “physical Doppler” step because you can simulate **hundreds–thousands of pulses** in a reasonable time (still heavy, but feasible), and Doppler emerges from scatterer motion.

### A.1 What you will output

For each slow-time pulse (t = 1..T):

* Channel RF (s_{m}(t,\tau)) for receive element (m) vs fast-time (\tau), for one transmit event (single plane wave or single focused transmit).
* Beamformed complex IQ image (X[t, y, x]).
* Ground truth label maps:

  * (M_{\text{flow}}(y,x)): vessel lumen / flow region mask
  * (M_{\text{bg}}(y,x)): background mask
  * Optional (M_{\text{alias}}(y,x)): where local (v_z) distribution implies (f_D > \mathrm{PRF}/2)

### A.2 Required inputs / parameters (write these down first)

**Acquisition**

* Center frequency (f_0) (e.g., 7.5 MHz like your brain pilots)
* Sampling rate (f_s) (RF; e.g., 40–100 MHz)
* PRF (slow-time sampling), e.g., 1500 Hz for your brain pilots
* Number of pulses (T) (e.g., 128 or 320; your pilots mention 5 ensembles × 64 pulses = 320 compounded frames)
* Transmit scheme:

  * simplest: **single plane wave at 0°**
  * next: multiple angles (−12, −6, 0, 6, 12)° like your pilots
    (but be careful: compounding changes effective PRF if you compound per frame)

**Transducer**

* Linear array: number of elements (N_e), pitch, kerf, height, element width
* Apodization (TX and RX)

**Medium**

* Speed of sound (c) (1540 m/s baseline unless simulating skull)
* Attenuation model (optional)
* Noise level (additive electronic + quantization)

**Scatterer model**

* Tissue scatterers: density, amplitude distribution, motion model
* Blood scatterers: density inside vessels, amplitude distribution, motion/flow model
* Vessel geometry: cylinder(s) with radius, location, orientation

---

### A.3 Step-by-step build (Field II)

#### Step 1 — Define simulation coordinate system and ROI

Let:

* (x): lateral (array axis),
* (z): depth,
* (y): elevation (out of imaging plane).

Even if you only display a 2D (x)-(z) image, you should still simulate a finite elevation thickness to get realistic speckle.

Pick:

* lateral extent: e.g., ±10 mm
* depth extent: e.g., 5–25 mm
* elevation thickness: e.g., 3–5 mm

#### Step 2 — Define vessel(s) and flow field

Example: one cylindrical vessel with centerline passing through ((x_0,z_0)), radius (R), oriented at angle (\alpha) relative to beam (z-axis) in the imaging plane.

Laminar profile (Poiseuille):
[
v(r) = v_{\max}\left(1 - \left(\frac{r}{R}\right)^2\right)
]
where (r) is distance from vessel centerline in cross-section.

Axial component:
[
v_z(r) = v(r)\cos(\alpha).
]

**Pulsatility (optional but good):**
[
v_{\max}(t) = \bar{v}*{\max}\left(1 + A \sin(2\pi f*\text{cardiac} t + \phi)\right)
]
Use (f_\text{cardiac}\sim 4\text{–}10\ \text{Hz}) for rodent-like, or lower for human-like.

**Spatially varying velocities (what you asked for):**

* Add a second vessel with different (\alpha) and (v_{\max})
* Or modulate (v_{\max}) slowly with position along the vessel (stenosis-like)
* Or include a bifurcation (two cylinders merging)

#### Step 3 — Populate scatterers (initial condition)

You need two scatterer populations:

**(a) Tissue scatterers**

* Uniform distribution throughout ROI excluding vessel lumen (or include lumen walls if you want).
* Amplitudes: random complex Gaussian (gives Rayleigh speckle magnitude).
* Density guideline: ensure ~10 scatterers per resolution cell (axial × lateral × elev). In practice, you may need *many* scatterers.

**(b) Blood scatterers**

* Uniform distribution inside vessel lumen.
* Amplitudes: smaller than tissue (calibrate from real data).
* Store each blood scatterer’s position and its local velocity vector (v(x,y,z)).

**Important:** handle outflow/inflow.

* When scatterers leave ROI, re-inject new scatterers upstream to keep density stationary.
* Avoid “wrap-around” artifacts that create periodicity in slow time unless you randomize phases/amplitudes.

#### Step 4 — Define the transducer and pulse (Field II)

In Field II you define:

* TX and RX apertures (linear array geometry)
* excitation waveform (the emitted pulse)
* impulse response (transducer bandwidth)
* delays for each TX element to make a plane wave at angle θ (or use focused transmit if you want)

**Plane-wave delay law**
For element at lateral position (x_m), plane wave at angle θ:
[
\Delta t_m = \frac{x_m \sin\theta}{c}.
]
Shift all delays so the smallest is zero (Field II often expects non-negative delays).

**Practical note:** If you’re not 100% sure about the Field II “plane wave” focusing calls, use a known wrapper (e.g., USTB’s Field II interface) to avoid wasting time on API minutiae. Conceptually, though, the delay law above is what you need.

#### Step 5 — Main simulation loop over slow time

Let (\Delta T = 1/\mathrm{PRF}).

For each slow-time pulse (t=1..T):

1. **Update blood scatterers:**
   [
   \mathbf{r}_k \leftarrow \mathbf{r}_k + \mathbf{v}(\mathbf{r}_k,t)\Delta T
   ]
   Replace any that leave ROI.

2. **Update tissue scatterers (clutter):** choose one of:

   * *Static tissue* (baseline)
   * *Rigid bulk motion:* translate all tissue scatterers by (\delta(t)) (e.g., random walk)
   * *Nonrigid deformation:* apply a smooth displacement field (u(x,z,t)) (e.g., low-order B‑spline field)

   This is the **scatterer-level** version of what you currently do as image warps (which you explicitly call a stress test, not a forward model).

3. **Simulate channel RF for this pulse** using Field II scattering:

   * Input: scatterer positions + amplitudes (tissue + blood), transducer geometry, TX delays, impulse response.
   * Output: RF vs fast-time at each receive element.

4. **(Optional) Add phase aberration**

   * You currently simulate a phase screen at the aperture (`--aperture-phase-std 0.8 rad`, corr length 14 elements).
     Apply an element-wise phase term to the received analytic signal, or equivalently, a delay jitter per element.

5. **Add electronic noise**

   * Additive white noise on channel RF or on IQ after demodulation.

6. **Beamform each pulse**

   * Delay-and-sum plane-wave beamforming to an image grid.
   * Demodulate to IQ (or beamform analytic signals directly).

Store IQ image (X[t,:,:]).

#### Step 6 — Doppler processing (to match your paper)

From the IQ cube:

* Form per-pixel slow-time series (x_{y,x}[t]).
* Apply your baseline clutter suppression (MC‑SVD etc).
* Compute PSD features and PD/score maps.

Now your STAP pipeline sees a cube whose covariance and band energies arise from moving scatterers, not overlays.

## 5) Implementation path B: k‑Wave moving-scatterer / moving-heterogeneity simulator

k‑Wave is “more physical” (full-wave propagation in heterogeneous media, attenuation, etc.) but expensive. The key trick is to use a **quasi-static approximation**:

* Within a single transmit–receive event (microseconds), blood motion is negligible.
* Between pulses (milliseconds), blood moves measurably.

So you can simulate each pulse as a **static medium snapshot**, then update the scatterer distribution between pulses.

### B.1 Representing scatterers in k‑Wave

k‑Wave simulates wave propagation in a medium defined by maps:

* `medium.sound_speed`
* `medium.density`
* optionally attenuation

To create speckle-like scattering:

* Introduce small random perturbations (\delta c(x)), (\delta\rho(x)).
  For weak scattering, percent-level perturbations are typical.

To create a vessel with moving blood scatterers:

* Define a vessel mask (V(x)).
* Inside (V), define a “blood scatterer perturbation” field that you **advect** each PRF step (shift/warp the random pattern).
* Outside (V), keep “tissue perturbation” static (or slowly moving if you want clutter motion).

### B.2 k‑Wave step-by-step

#### Step 1 — Build grid

* Choose 2D first (fast) to validate PSD logic.
* Set `dx, dz` small enough for your center frequency:

  * rule of thumb: ≥ ~6 points per wavelength.
    For λ ≈ 0.2 mm, dx ~ 30 µm is “nice”; 90 µm like your pilots is coarser but might be acceptable depending on bandwidth. Your brain pilots use dx=dy=90 µm.

#### Step 2 — Define baseline medium + scatterers

* `c0`, `rho0`
* tissue perturbations outside vessel
* blood perturbations inside vessel (a random texture)

#### Step 3 — Define transducer and plane-wave source

* Use a line of grid points as a source mask (elements).
* Apply per-element delays for angle θ (same delay law as Field II).
* Define the excitation pulse at (f_0) with bandwidth.

#### Step 4 — Define sensors

* Record pressure time series at the same element positions (or use k‑Wave’s transducer object if you prefer).

#### Step 5 — Loop over slow-time pulses

For each pulse (t):

1. Update blood perturbation field by shifting according to local velocity × ΔT.
2. Run k‑Wave forward simulation for this pulse.
3. Save sensor data (channel RF).
4. Beamform to IQ (offline).

### B.3 Where k‑Wave is uniquely worth it for you

Your paper’s simulated brain regimes are explicitly about skull/aberration (Brain‑OpenSkull / SkullOR, etc.), but the flow/alias content is currently imposed by overlays. The strongest “physics upgrade” story is:

* keep your skull/aberration forward model (k‑Wave),
* but generate slow-time Doppler by moving blood scatterers (or moving heterogeneity in vessel),
* so that alias leakage + clutter + aberration interact naturally.

### B.4 Practical limitation

Running k‑Wave per pulse is expensive. For preprint purposes, it can still be compelling if you:

* do **one** k‑Wave “physical slow-time” regime (shorter T, smaller grid),
* and show it reproduces qualitative failure modes / PSD broadening that overlays don’t.

## 6) Implementation path C (often best): Hybrid k‑Wave + Field II

This is what I’d recommend if your goal is “trustworthy without becoming a simulation paper.”

### Hybrid idea

* Use **Field II** to generate the moving-scatterer Doppler (fast enough for long slow-time ensembles).
* Use **k‑Wave** (or your existing skull modeling) to derive an **aberration/phase screen / PSF distortion model**.
* Apply that distortion to Field II channel data (element-dependent delays/phases, frequency-dependent attenuation, etc.).

This gets you:

* physical Doppler generation (moving scatterers),
* transcranial/aberration realism (from k‑Wave),
* without running full k‑Wave for every pulse.

And it aligns with your current use of an “aperture phase screen” overlay parameter—you just make that screen *derived* (or at least calibrated) rather than purely synthetic.

## 7) “Trustworthy” validation plan (what to match to real open data)

You suggested “PSD sanity link.” Yes—but make it systematic and hard to argue with.

### 7.1 Match the PSD partition statistics you already use

Your Fig. 3 shows PSD partitioning into Po/Pf/Pg/Pa and example tile PSDs. Do the same on **real** IQ datasets (Twinkling, Shin, ULM) and on your simulation:

Compute, over many tiles/pixels:

* distribution of peak frequency (f_\text{peak}),
* distribution of band energies (E_o, E_f, E_g, E_a),
* distribution of ratios like (\log(E_a/E_f)),
* “Pf peak occupancy” type stats you already use in telemetry.

Then show:

* simulated distributions fall in the ballpark of real distributions (median + IQR),
* or at least the *ordering* and the existence of both “flow-like” and “alias-dominant” modes matches.

### 7.2 Match the low-rank clutter structure

Compute SVD spectrum of the Casorati matrix (pixels × time) in real vs sim:

* rank at which X% energy is captured (e.g., 90%, 95%),
* compare to the kind of MC‑SVD energy fraction you use (e.g., e=0.90 in Brain pilots).

If your sim produces wildly different rank behavior, your STAP gains may not translate.

### 7.3 Match temporal coherence / decorrelation

For real IQ (Shin/ULM), compute per-pixel lag‑1 correlation magnitude:

* ( |\mathrm{corr}(x[t], x[t+1])| ) distribution
  Compare to sim.

This is especially important because you currently note that image-domain motion injection is **not** channel-domain decorrelation.

### 7.4 Calibration discipline (avoid “tuning to win”)

Use a simple split:

* Tune sim parameters on **one** dataset (e.g., Twinkling phantom),
* Validate similarity on **another** dataset (e.g., Shin or ULM) without retuning.

That makes the story more defensible.
